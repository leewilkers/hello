# Adjacent Sources – What the Informed Curator Knows

> This document maps what's adjacent to the shelf: sources that belong in the same conversation, quality judgments within traditions, what's happening in the scene right now, and what to avoid. This is the "taste" layer – the knowledge that separates a reading list from a curated identity.

---

## Should Be On Your Radar

Sources that fit the shelf's logic and would strengthen it. Not "you should read these" – more "the person who built this shelf would know about these."

### For the Perception Lane

**James C. Scott – "Seeing Like a State" (1998)**
The book Rao's "Legibility" essay is about. The ur-text for your perception lane's concern with simplification, state vision, and the destruction of *mētis* (practical knowledge). Its absence from the shelf is the most conspicuous gap. Possible reason: so foundational it goes without saying, like citing oxygen.
- [Yale UP](https://yaleupress.yale.edu/book/9780300246759/seeing-like-a-state)

**Matthew Crawford – "The World Beyond Your Head" (2015)**
Better than "Shop Class as Soulcraft" (2009), which is the one everyone knows. Crawford argues that attention is a cultural achievement, not a personal failing – and that skilled manual work (motorcycle repair, organ building) trains a kind of perception that screen-mediated life atrophies. Bridges your craft and perception lanes.
- [Penguin](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/235558/the-world-beyond-your-head-by-matthew-b-crawford/)
- *Note*: Crawford's politics lean libertarian. The argument about embodied attention holds regardless.

**Lorraine Daston & Peter Galison – "Objectivity" (2007)**
The history of how "objectivity" was invented as a scientific virtue. Connects to Flyvbjerg (what counts as valid knowledge), Berger (how seeing is trained), and Polanyi (tacit knowledge in scientific practice). Academic but visually rich – it's about images.
- [Zone Books](https://www.zonebooks.org/books/44-objectivity)

### For the Technology Lane

**Fred Turner – "From Counterculture to Cyberculture" (2006)**
The definitive account of how Brand's Whole Earth network became Silicon Valley's ideology. Essential context for why Brand is interesting AND why the Valley is absent from your shelf. Without Turner, the Brand → tech pipeline is invisible.
- [Chicago UP](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/F/bo3773600.html)

**Jedediah Purdy – "After Nature: A Politics for the Anthropocene" (2015)**
Purdy does for nature what Winner does for technology: shows it's politically constructed. Connects the technology critique to environmental politics without the usual eco-catastrophism. Would bridge your technology and intervention lanes.
- [Harvard UP](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674368224)

**Andrew Russell – "Open Standards and the Digital Age" (2014)**
Russell's earlier, less accessible book – the history of how technical standards shape society. For the deep-end reader who wants to understand why the maintenance lineage exists. Academic but excellent.

### For the Intervention Lane

**Arturo Escobar – "Encountering Development" (1995/2012)**
The postcolonial development critique sitting between Ferguson and Tsing. More directly about how "development" was constructed as a discourse – Foucauldian without the jargon. If your intervention lane has a gap, it's between Ferguson (1990) and Tsing (2004).
- [Princeton UP](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691150451/encountering-development)

**Tania Murray Li – "The Will to Improve" (2007)**
Government improvement schemes in Indonesia. Same diagnosis as Ferguson (good intentions → bad outcomes) but more ethnographically rich. If you liked Ferguson's argument but wanted more field texture.
- [Duke UP](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-will-to-improve)

**David Graeber – "The Utopia of Rules" (2015)**
Bureaucracy as a form of violence. Connects your intervention lane (NGO logic, scaling, metrics) to your technology lane (prescriptive technologies, systems of control). Graeber writes accessibly and angrily – closer to Berry's register than Ferguson's.
- [Melville House](https://www.mhpbooks.com/books/the-utopia-of-rules/)

### For the Craft Lane

**Brian Eno – "A Year with Swollen Appendices" (1996)**
Eno's diary. Not a theory book – a record of how a generative thinker actually works. The studio-as-instrument, constraints-as-creativity framework. Would add a music/sound dimension to the craft lane that's currently visual/textual.
- [Faber](https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571355853-a-year-with-swollen-appendices/)

**Matthew Kirschenbaum – "Track Changes: A Literary History of Word Processing" (2016)**
The material history of how writers adopted computers. Berry refused; most didn't. Connects your craft lane (tools shape work) to your technology lane (what adoption costs). A better version of the "tools and writing" story than most.
- [Harvard UP](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674417076)

**Keller Easterling – "Extrastatecraft" (2014)**
Infrastructure as a medium of political power. The design equivalent of Ferguson's anti-politics machine – how spatial designs (free trade zones, fiber optic networks, highway systems) carry political ideology without ever being voted on. Bridges craft, technology, and intervention.
- [Verso](https://www.versobooks.com/products/133-extrastatecraft)

### For the Stream (Sites/Writers to Watch)

**Mandy Brown** – [aworkinglibrary.com](https://aworkinglibrary.com) (already in stream) – recently became CEO of a small press. Her writing about reading, work, and care is increasingly relevant.

**Dan Cohen** – [dancohen.org](https://dancohen.org) – former head of DPLA, now at Northeastern. Digital humanities, public knowledge infrastructure. Maintenance-lineage adjacent.

**Deb Chachra** – wrote "How Infrastructure Works" (2023). Explicitly in the Franklin/maintenance tradition. Active on social media.

**Clive Thompson** – [clivethompson.net](https://clivethompson.net) – tech journalist who writes about coders, tools, and making. Accessible but not shallow.

**Robin Rendle** – [robinrendle.com](https://robinrendle.com) – essays about web design and typography. Chimero-adjacent in sensibility. His "Essays on Web Typography" newsletters are excellent.

**Ethan Marcotte** – [ethanmarcotte.com](https://ethanmarcotte.com) – coined "responsive design." Now writing about tech labor and power. Craft → politics pipeline.

## What to Avoid (Anti-Recommendations)

Sources that look adjacent but don't fit the shelf's logic. Knowing what NOT to add is as important as knowing what to add.

### Looks Right, Isn't

| Source | Why it looks right | Why it doesn't fit |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Kevin Kelly – "What Technology Wants" | Whole Earth lineage, Brand adjacent | Techno-determinist optimism. Brand without the self-correction. |
| Evgeny Morozov – "To Save Everything, Click Here" | Tech critique | Right diagnosis, exhausting prose. Critique as performance, not practice. |
| Tim Brown / IDEO – "Change by Design" | Design thinking | Von Busch/Palmås IS the critique of this. Adding the object of critique weakens the shelf. |
| Cal Newport – "Deep Work" etc. | Attention, craft | Productivity register. The shelf's relationship to work is through craft, not optimization. |
| Jaron Lanier – "You Are Not a Gadget" | Tech critique, humanist | Silicon Valley's internal debates. Not your conversation. |
| Nir Eyal – "Hooked" / "Indistractable" | Attention, design | Behavioral design for engagement. Antithetical to everything on the shelf. |
| Malcolm Gladwell – anything | Accessible, ideas-driven | Pop-intellectual shortcut. The shelf prefers primary texts to popularizers. |
| Yuval Noah Harari – anything | Big-picture thinking | Sweeping narratives that dissolve specificity. The shelf values material detail. |

### Right Tradition, Wrong Register

| Source | The tradition | Why the register is off |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| Bruno Latour – "Reassembling the Social" | STS, same world as Law/Tsing | Too methodological, too inside-baseball. The shelf reads applications, not theory. |
| Donna Haraway – "Cyborg Manifesto" | Feminist STS | The manifesto is too performed, too 1980s-academic. "Staying with the Trouble" (2016) is the better entry – but may still be too theoretical. |
| Arturo Escobar – "Designs for the Pluriverse" (2018) | Decolonial design | More interesting than "Encountering Development" but more abstract. The 1995 book is more grounded. |
| David Graeber – "Bullshit Jobs" | Bureaucracy critique | More viral but less coherent than "Utopia of Rules." The concept is the book; the book is too long. |

## Quality Gradients Within Your Traditions

What an insider would know about the best and worst within each lineage.

### Maintenance Lineage
- **Best prose**: Mattern's Places Journal essays. Dense, vivid, capacious examples.
- **Best entry point**: Russell & Vinsel's "Hail the Maintainers" – the essay, not the book. The book (Innovation Delusion) is padded.
- **Best deep cut**: Franklin's Massey Lectures – the audio versions are better than the book. Her speaking voice carries authority the page doesn't fully capture.
- **Overhyped**: The Maintainers conference proceedings. Uneven quality, academic-conference mode.

### Development Critique
- **Best single book**: Ferguson's "Anti-Politics Machine." Nothing in the lineage is as tight.
- **Best ethnography**: Tsing's "Friction." Richer than Ferguson, but messier – by design.
- **Best for practitioners**: Krause's "Good Project." Shortest, most directly applicable to anyone working in NGOs.
- **Most uneven**: Adams' "Metrics" – it's an edited collection. Some chapters are excellent (Adams' own, Erikson's), others are filler.
- **Missing voice**: Packard surveys the terrain well but doesn't have a distinctive analytical move. Useful, not brilliant.

### Making Lineage
- **Best for understanding**: Polanyi's "Tacit Dimension." 108 pages. Every sentence does work.
- **Best for feeling**: Korn's "Why We Make Things." A memoir that embodies the theory without theorizing.
- **Most ambitious**: Ingold's "Making." He disagrees with Sennett and Polanyi on key points (hylomorphism). Worth reading for the disagreement.
- **Most practical**: Schwarz's "Anarchist's Tool Chest." Actually about building a tool chest. The philosophy is in the doing.
- **Alexander note**: "Timeless Way of Building" > "A Pattern Language" for the ideas. But "A Pattern Language" is the one the software world adopted (and misread).

### Web/Design Space
- **Best single essay on the shelf**: Victor's "Magic Ink." Nothing else in web design writing is as ambitious or as right.
- **Best for voice calibration**: Mod's essays. His prose is the closest to what Lee's shelf sounds like when it talks about the web.
- **Best debate**: Chimero's "The Web's Grain" vs. Chimero's own "The Shape of Design" (2012). The earlier book is more optimistic; the essay is wiser.
- **What Hundred Rabbits demonstrates**: You don't have to accept the terms. Build your own stack, live on a boat, run everything on a Raspberry Pi. The site IS the argument.

## The Scene Right Now (April 2026)

What's actively being published, discussed, and debated in the conversations your shelf is part of.

**Active threads:**
- **Maintenance studies** is now a real field. The Maintainers network has annual conferences, published collections, university courses. Shannon Mattern is the most visible public intellectual.
- **Local-first software** (Ink & Switch) has become a movement. Martin Kleppmann's "Local-first cooperation" framework is being adopted. Directly relevant to craft lane.
- **L.M. Sacasas** (Convivial Society) is the most active Illich interpreter. His 41 questions essay is a pedagogical tool being used in design schools. He's writing a book.
- **"Folk interfaces"** – Maggie Appleton's concept for non-professional, culturally situated tool-making. Bridges your craft and perception lanes.
- **Repair culture** is going mainstream via Right to Repair legislation. Mattern's "Step by Step" (2024) anticipated this.
- **Deb Chachra's "How Infrastructure Works"** (2023) is the closest thing to a new Franklin. The Massey Lecture comparison is deliberate – she's in that tradition.

**What's fading:**
- "Tools for thought" as a term is losing energy. Appleton's critique (it was always about apps, not culture) landed. The Notion/Roam/Obsidian hype cycle peaked ~2022.
- Design thinking as a professional identity is in decline. The IDEO layoffs, von Busch/Palmås critique, and broader disillusionment with "innovation" rhetoric are all connected.
- Ribbonfarm is less active. Rao's best essays are behind him. Sarah Perry stopped writing there. The site is an archive now, not a living conversation.

**What's emerging:**
- **AI-and-craft** discourse: How does LLM-assisted work relate to Sennett's craftsman, Polanyi's tacit knowledge, Franklin's prescriptive technologies? Your shelf is perfectly positioned for this conversation – you just haven't written about it yet.
- **Degrowth and technology**: The Berry/Illich tradition is being rediscovered by degrowth economists. Your shelf is already there.
- **Post-development**: The development critique lineage is informing new approaches to global health that your FACE work touches directly.
