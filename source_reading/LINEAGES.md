# Intellectual Lineages – Lee's Shelf

> How the shelf items connect. Each lineage is a traceable chain of influence – who read whom, who responded to whom, where the ideas traveled. Items on Lee's shelf are **bold**. Items in his stream are *italic*. Items not on the site but part of the chain are plain.

---

## 1. The Maintenance Lineage

The longest and most active chain on the shelf. Runs from 1973 to 2024.

```
Ivan Illich, Tools for Conviviality (1973)
  └─→ Langdon Winner, The Whale and the Reactor (1986)
       └─→ Ursula Franklin, The Real World of Technology (1989)
            └─→ Russell & Vinsel, "Hail the Maintainers" (2016)
                 ├─→ Shannon Mattern, "Maintenance and Care" (2018)
                 ├─→ Vinsel & Russell, The Innovation Delusion (2020)
                 └─→ Shannon Mattern, "Step by Step" (2024)
```

**The thread**: Illich argued that tools should serve conviviality – human-scale agency – not institutional control. Winner asked what political choices are embedded in technologies. Franklin distinguished holistic technologies (where the worker controls the process) from prescriptive ones (where the process controls the worker). Russell & Vinsel named the maintenance problem: innovation gets the glory, maintenance does the work. Mattern expanded "maintenance" beyond infrastructure to include care work, record-keeping, and repair culture.

**Evidence of connection**: Russell & Vinsel explicitly cite Illich, Winner, and Franklin. Mattern's "Maintenance and Care" responds directly to "Hail the Maintainers." Mattern's "Step by Step" extends her own earlier work. Franklin's lectures were a direct response to the techno-optimism Illich had already diagnosed.

**Where it branches**: Beer's "Designing Freedom" (1973) sits alongside Illich chronologically and shares the cybernetics-meets-politics concern, but the two don't directly cite each other. Beer came from operations research; Illich from radical theology. They converge on the same conclusion from different directions.

**Lee's hover note trail**: "maintainers > innovators" → "book-length Hail the Maintainers" → "not every road should be repaired" → "who gets to fix what" → "holistic vs prescriptive technologies." The notes themselves trace the lineage.

## 2. The Seeing Lineage

How perception is conditioned by media, power, and method.

```
Marshall McLuhan, Understanding Media (1964)
  ├─→ McLuhan & Fiore, The Medium Is the Massage (1967)
  └─→ John Berger, Ways of Seeing (1972)
       └─→ Christopher Pinney, Photography's Other Histories (2003)

[parallel branch]
C. Wright Mills, The Sociological Imagination (1959)
  └─→ Bent Flyvbjerg, Making Social Science Matter (2001)
       └─→ John Law, After Method (2004)
```

**The thread**: McLuhan said the medium shapes the message. Berger applied this to visual culture – who gets to see, how images construct reality. Pinney extended Berger to non-Western photography, arguing that photography has "other histories" that Western accounts suppress.

**The parallel method branch**: Mills argued that sociology had become too abstract and too empiricist simultaneously – it needed to reconnect personal troubles with public issues. Flyvbjerg picked up this critique forty years later, arguing for phronesis (practical wisdom) over episteme (theoretical knowledge). Law radicalized it further: social science methods are inherently messy, and the mess is a feature, not a bug.

**Evidence of connection**: Berger's BBC series was explicitly a response to Kenneth Clark's "Civilisation" – McLuhan's ideas about media are in the background. Pinney cites Berger's "Ways of Seeing" directly and argues against its Eurocentric frame. Flyvbjerg opens with a sustained critique of Plato's hierarchy of knowledge – Mills' "sociological imagination" is the American version of the same argument.

**Cross-lineage**: Berger (seeing) connects to the making lineage through his art criticism – he wrote about how we see handmade things. Mills (seeing/method) connects to the craft lineage through his appendix "On Intellectual Craftsmanship," which is literally about the craft of thinking.

## 3. The Making Lineage

Embodied knowledge, craft, and the hand-head connection.

```
Michael Polanyi, The Tacit Dimension (1966)
  ├─→ Richard Sennett, The Craftsman (2008)
  │    └─→ Peter Korn, Why We Make Things (2013)
  └─→ Tim Ingold, Making (2013)

[craft-practice branch]
Christopher Schwarz, The Anarchist's Tool Chest (2011)
Christopher Alexander, The Timeless Way of Building (1979)
```

**The thread**: Polanyi established that we know more than we can tell – tacit knowledge is real, irreducible to rules. Sennett built on this with a social theory of craft: the hand teaches the head through repetitive practice, and this has political implications (good craftwork requires autonomy). Ingold, coming from anthropology, argued that making is a way of knowing – not applying knowledge to materials, but discovering knowledge through materials. Korn wrote a memoir that embodies Sennett's and Polanyi's ideas in practice: in the workshop, wishing won't make it so.

**The craft-practice branch**: Schwarz and Alexander don't cite Polanyi directly but live in the same intellectual space. Schwarz's "build the chest first" is practical Polanyi – learn by doing. Alexander's "quality without a name" is an attempt to point at what Polanyi called tacit knowledge in the context of architecture.

**Evidence of connection**: Sennett cites Polanyi extensively. Ingold engages with both Sennett and Polanyi, disagreeing about the role of hylomorphism (imposing form on matter). Korn doesn't cite anyone – he's a craftsman, not an academic – but his book reads as Sennett's theory lived.

**Cross-lineage**: This lineage connects to the seeing lineage through Polanyi (who also influenced Flyvbjerg's concept of phronesis) and to the maintenance lineage through Sennett's argument that craft requires institutional support (echoing Franklin's distinction between holistic and prescriptive).

## 4. The Development Critique Lineage

What happens when wealthy institutions try to help.

```
James Ferguson, The Anti-Politics Machine (1990)
  ├─→ Anna Tsing, Friction (2004)
  │    └─→ related: Didier Fassin, Humanitarian Reason (2011)
  └─→ Randall Packard, A History of Global Health (2016)
       └─→ Vincanne Adams, Metrics (2016)

[organizational branch]
Monika Krause, The Good Project (2014)
Donald Sull, "Why Good Companies Go Bad" (1999)
Otto von Busch & Karl Palmås, The Corruption of Co-Design (2023)
```

**The thread**: Ferguson showed that a development project in Lesotho "failed" at its stated goals but "succeeded" at extending state power – development depoliticizes. Tsing showed that global connections are always mediated by local "friction" – there's no smooth transfer of anything. Packard documented the recurring pattern in global health: identify a magic bullet, deploy it at scale, wonder why it didn't work, repeat. Adams showed how metrics reshape health priorities – what gets counted gets funded, regardless of need.

**The organizational branch**: Krause analyzed NGOs as organizations with their own logic (media companies that produce narratives of suffering), not just vehicles for doing good. Sull's "active inertia" explains why organizations keep doing what stopped working. Von Busch and Palmås argue that participatory design has been corrupted into a legitimation tool.

**Evidence of connection**: This is Lee's professional world. Ferguson is the foundational text for critical development studies. Tsing, Fassin, and Adams all work in the post-Ferguson tradition. Packard's history synthesizes decades of critique. These authors cite each other frequently and appear on the same conference panels.

**The moral imagination sub-thread**: Benatar's "Moral Imagination" (2005) in PLoS Medicine argued that global health lacks moral imagination – it has technical capacity but not the will to imagine different distributions of health. Rindova & Martins (2023) picked up "moral imagination" as a concept for organizational strategy – "the collective desirable." This is a thin but deliberate chain on Lee's shelf.

## 5. The Web/Design Lineage

How digital tools and the web should be thought about.

```
Bret Victor, "Magic Ink" (2006) / "Ladder of Abstraction" (2011)
  └─→ [influence on] Craig Mod, "Fast Software" (undated)
       └─→ [influence on] Frank Chimero, "The Web's Grain" (2015)

Don Norman, The Design of Everyday Things (1988)
  └─→ [critique] Sara Hendren, What Can a Body Do? (2020)

Christopher Alexander, The Timeless Way of Building (1979)
  └─→ [applied to software] pattern language movement
       └─→ [in web context] Gordon Brander's pattern library
```

**The thread**: Victor demonstrated that information software could be radically better – not iteratively, but conceptually. His essays showed, not told. Mod brought this sensibility to publishing and the web. Chimero asked what design means when the web has its own material properties ("the grain"). Norman codified human-centered design; Hendren challenged its ableist assumptions.

**The stream sites as a living lineage**: The 18 stream sites (Ink & Switch, Hundred Rabbits, A Working Library, Logic(s) Magazine, etc.) form a loose network of people thinking about technology, design, and the web in ways that connect to the shelf. They're not a lineage in the academic sense – they're a scene.

## 7. Tensions and Arguments

The shelf is not a consensus. These items disagree:

**Alexander vs. von Busch/Palmås**: Alexander assumed that participatory design reveals a shared "quality without a name." Von Busch and Palmås argue participation can be captured, corrupted, weaponized. Alexander is utopian about process; von Busch/Palmås are realist.

**Berry vs. Victor**: Berry refuses the computer. Victor builds the most beautiful computer interfaces ever made. Both are on the shelf. The reconciliation: Berry's values (care, locality, appropriate scale) can be held inside Victor's medium (interactive, responsive, immediate).

**Flyvbjerg vs. Law**: Flyvbjerg wants social science to matter more by doing phronesis. Law says the methods themselves are the problem – you can't clean up the mess. Both diagnose the same disease; they disagree about whether treatment is possible.

**Sull vs. the development critiques**: Sull's "active inertia" is an organizational diagnosis from a business school. Ferguson/Tsing/Packard diagnose the same phenomenon (organizations doing what doesn't work) from a critical-theory perspective. Same observation, different politics.

**Labatut vs. everyone**: Labatut is the only quasi-fictional work on the shelf. It occupies a position no other item does: the emotional, narrative, non-argumentative treatment of knowing too much. "Not a soul who gets it" is Lee's note – and it applies to the book's position on the shelf as well.
