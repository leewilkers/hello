## I. THE DECLARATIVE – Moral Clarity, No Hedging

Authors who state what is the case and let the reader deal with it. The sentences do work. No warming up.

### Wendell Berry – "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer" (1987)

> What would a computer cost me? More money, for one thing, than I can afford, and more than I wish to pay to people whom I do not admire. But the cost would not be just monetary. It is well understood that technological innovation always requires the discarding of the "old model" – the "old model" in this case being not just our old Royal standard, but my wife, my critic, my closest reader, my fellow worker. Thus (and I think this is typical of present-day technological innovation), what would be superseded would be not only something, but somebody. In order to be technologically up-to-date as a writer, I would have to sacrifice an association that I am dependent upon and that I treasure.

> I can only conclude that I have scratched the skin of a technological fundamentalism that, like other fundamentalisms, wishes to monopolize a whole society and, therefore, cannot tolerate the smallest difference of opinion. At the slightest hint of a threat to their complacency, they repeat, like a chorus of toads, the notes sounded by their leaders in industry.

Source: [PDF](https://classes.matthewjbrown.net/teaching-files/philtech/berry-computer.pdf)
*Why these passages*: The "old model" turn is devastating – the personal made structural in one sentence. "Chorus of toads" is the kind of phrase you remember for decades.

### Solomon Benatar – "Moral Imagination" (2005)

> While it is entirely appropriate to consider scientific and technological advances and economic growth as necessary for social progress, it is arguable that these will not be sufficient to ensure movement towards a more just world in which the health of whole populations could be improved.

Source: [PLoS Medicine (open access)](https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371/journal.pmed.0020400)
*Why this passage*: The quiet power of "necessary" vs. "sufficient." Benatar doesn't reject the technical apparatus – he names its insufficiency. The gap is moral, not technical.

---

### Ursula K. Le Guin – "A Rant About 'Technology'" (undated)

> We have been so desensitized by a hundred and fifty years of ceaselessly expanding technical prowess that we think nothing less complex and showy than a computer or a jet bomber deserves to be called "technology" at all.

> As if linen were the same thing as flax – as if paper, ink, wheels, knives, clocks, chairs, aspirin pills, were natural objects, born with us like our teeth and fingers – as if steel saucepans with copper bottoms and fleece vests spun from recycled glass grew on trees, and we just picked them when they were ripe ...

Source: [ursulakleguin.com](https://www.ursulakleguin.com/a-rant-about-technology)
*Why these passages*: The "as if" cascade building to absurdity. She makes the definitional error (technology = high technology) feel genuinely ridiculous, which is harder than it sounds.

### Alan Jacobs – "From Tech Critique to Ways of Living" (2021)

> But the number of people who are even open to following this logic is vanishingly small. For all its cogency, the SCT is utterly powerless to slow our technosocial momentum, much less to alter its direction.

> A rational assessment of the current moment must begin with the recognition that the forces against which Illich, Franklin, Postman, and Borgmann contended ... have progressed with dramatic speed in the past forty years.

Source: [The New Atlantis](https://www.thenewatlantis.com/publications/from-tech-critique-to-ways-of-living)
*Why these passages*: Jacobs concedes that the entire tradition of technology critique – however intellectually correct – has failed to change anything. This forces the question of what comes after critique.

## II. THE ANALYTICAL-PLAYFUL – Conceptual Invention, Precise but Light

Authors who build new concepts with visible pleasure. Serious ideas, worn lightly.

### Ian Bogost – "The Cathedral of Computation" (2015)

> Here's an exercise: The next time you hear someone talking about algorithms, replace the term with "God" and ask yourself if the meaning changes. Our supposedly algorithmic culture is not a material phenomenon so much as a devotional one, a supplication made to the computers people have allowed to replace gods in their minds, even as they simultaneously claim that science has made us impervious to religion.

> "The Google search algorithm" names something with an initial coherence that quickly scurries away once you really look for it. Googling isn't a matter of invoking a programmatic subroutine – not on its own, anyway. Google is a monstrosity. It's a confluence of physical, virtual, computational, and non-computational stuffs – electricity, data centers, servers, air conditioners, security guards, financial markets.

Source: [The Atlantic](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2015/01/the-cathedral-of-computation/384300/)
*Why these passages*: The God/algorithm substitution test is a one-sentence diagnostic you can apply anywhere. The second dissolves "the algorithm" into messy material reality.

### Venkat Rao – "A Big Little Idea Called Legibility" (2010)

> The more I examined these efforts at sedentarization, the more I came to see them as a state's attempt to make a society legible, to arrange the population in ways that simplified the classic state functions of taxation, conscription, and prevention of rebellion.

> High-modernist (think Bauhaus and Le Corbusier) aesthetics necessarily lead to simplification, since a reality that serves many purposes presents itself as illegible to a vision informed by a singular purpose.

Source: [Ribbonfarm](https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2010/07/26/a-big-little-idea-called-legibility/)
*Why these passages*: The first captures Scott's core insight via Rao's compression. The second links modernist aesthetics to political simplification – the "illegibility" of multipurpose reality.

### Donald Sull – "Why Good Companies Go Bad" (1999)

> Inertia is usually associated with inaction – picture a billiard ball at rest on a table – but physicists also use the term to describe a moving object's tendency to persist in its current trajectory. Active inertia is an organization's tendency to follow established patterns of behavior – even in response to dramatic environmental shifts.

Source: [HBR](https://hbr.org/1999/07/why-good-companies-go-bad)
*Why this passage*: "Active inertia" reframes organizational failure as momentum, not paralysis. The problem isn't that institutions do nothing – it's that they keep doing the same thing harder.

---

### Sarah Perry – "Tendrils of Mess in our Brains" (2017)

> Mess is only perceptible because it produces in our minds an imaginary order that is missing. It is as if objects and artifacts send out invisible tendrils into space, saying, "the matter around me should be ordered in some particular way." The stronger the claim, and the more the claims of component pieces conflict, the more there is mess. It is these invisible, tangled tendrils of incompatible orders that we are "seeing" when we see mess. They are cryptosalient: at once invisible and obvious.

Source: [Ribbonfarm](https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2017/01/05/tendrils-of-mess-in-our-brains/)
*Why this passage*: The ribbonfarm voice distilled – taking an utterly mundane observation (mess) and excavating its conceptual structure with invented vocabulary ("cryptosalient"). Precise, never decorative.

---

### Sarah Perry – "Ritual and the Consciousness Monoculture" (2015)

> Rituals are what help us navigate the necessary ambiguities of social life, into which rational codification can never fully penetrate.

> Rather than dismissing these behaviors as meaningless, look instead at how they elaborate meaning. And, perhaps more importantly, enjoy them without the sneaking suspicion that you're not being authentic enough when you perform them.

Source: [Ribbonfarm](https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2015/01/08/ritual-and-the-consciousness-monoculture/)
*Why these passages*: The first names the limit of rationalism in one clause. The second identifies the specific modern pathology: we can't do rituals without monitoring ourselves for authenticity.

### Sarah Perry – "Deep Laziness" (2018)

> This is the laziest way to do things, so that is how the universe does it.

> Clouds are beautiful and never a mess because they are products of structure-preserving processes.

Source: [Ribbonfarm](https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2018/04/06/deep-laziness/)
*Why these passages*: Laziness inverted from vice to cosmic principle. The clouds sentence links beauty to minimal effort with startling economy.

## III. THE CRAFT VOICE – Directness About Making

Authors who write about how things work with the clarity of someone who has actually built things.

### Bret Victor – "Magic Ink" (2006)

> Most of the time, a person sits down at her personal computer not to create, but to read, observe, study, explore, make cognitive connections, and ultimately come to an understanding.

> For information software, all interaction is essentially navigation around a data space... Unless it is enjoyable or educational in and of itself, interaction is an essentially negative aspect of information software.

Source: [worrydream.com](https://worrydream.com/MagicInk/)
*Why these passages*: The first reframes what computers are FOR – not creation, but understanding. The second is Victor's core indictment: interaction signals design failure.

---

### Craig Mod – "Fast Software, the Best Software" (undated)

> A typewriter is an excellent tool because, even though it's slow in a relative sense, every aspect of the machine itself operates as quickly as the user can move... The best software inches ever closer to the physical directness of something like a typewriter.

> Just swipe from the right and – *sloop* – an app float-over appears... You move your finger and – instantly – there is the tiny app under your finger... to feel tactile is to move without latency.

Source: [craigmod.com](https://craigmod.com/essays/fast_software/)
*Why these passages*: Mod makes the abstraction of code tangible. "Sloop" – onomatopoeia in a software essay. Speed isn't technical; it's physical.

---

### Frank Chimero – "The Web's Grain" (2015)

> Most of the solidified techniques about our practice come from the natural ways of the web that have been there since the start. The answer is right there in front of us, in the website itself, and each step we take away from its intentions makes our creations weaker.

> What would happen if we stopped treating the web like a blank canvas to paint on, and instead like a material to build *with*?

Source: [frankchimero.com](https://frankchimero.com/blog/2015/the-webs-grain/)
*Why these passages*: The canvas/material distinction is the essay's gift to web design. Chimero's grace is in making the argument feel obvious once stated.

---

### Chase McCoy – "We Could All Be Archivists" (2025)

> Physical collections benefit from their form: by taking up space in the real world they demand attention and care. Digital collections more easily fall into the trap of "out of sight, out of mind."

> If an average consumer wanted to organize information like they might in Notion while maintaining ownership and storing their data locally, I literally do not know of a solution that doesn't involve administrating a database.

Source: [chsmc.org](https://chsmc.org/2025/01/archivists/)
*Why these passages*: The first names why physical objects survive and digital ones don't – they take up space and demand attention. The second is a confession that the tools don't exist yet.

## IV. THE CRITICAL-CULTURAL – Politics of Representation

Authors who name who benefits, who's excluded, what's hidden.

### Hito Steyerl – "In Defense of the Poor Image" (2009)

> The poor image is a rag or a rip; an AVI or a JPEG, a lumpen proletarian in the class society of appearances, ranked and valued according to its resolution. The poor image has been uploaded, downloaded, shared, reformatted, and reedited. It transforms quality into accessibility, exhibition value into cult value, films into clips, contemplation into distraction.

> In a way, the poor image is subject to a similar tension. On the one hand, it operates against the fetish value of high resolution. On the other hand, this is precisely why it also ends up being perfectly integrated into an information capitalism thriving on compressed attention spans, on intensity rather than contemplation, on previews rather than screenings.

Source: [e-flux journal](https://www.e-flux.com/journal/10/61362/in-defense-of-the-poor-image/)
*Why these passages*: "Lumpen proletarian in the class society of appearances" is Marxist vocabulary repurposed for image culture with total precision. The second captures the essay's central tension: the poor image is simultaneously resistant and perfectly co-opted.

---

### Maggie Appleton – "Tools for Thought as Cultural Practices" (undated)

> Most of the examples I listed above are cultural practices and techniques. They are primary ways of doing; specific ways of thinking and acting that result in greater cognitive abilities. Ones that people pass down from generation to generation through culture.

> This gives "tools for thought" a distinctly computer-oriented, male, American, middle-class flavour. The term has always been used in relation to a dream that is deeply intertwined with digital machines, white-collar knowledge work, and bold American optimism.

Source: [maggieappleton.com](https://maggieappleton.com/tools-for-thought)
*Why these passages*: The first reframes the conversation – from apps to practices, from technology to culture. The second exposes whose thinking the field was designed for.

## V. THE HERETICAL – Contrarian by Conviction, Not Performance

Authors who say something most readers will resist, and mean it.

### Sasha Chapin – "Notes Against Note-Taking Systems" (undated)

> Most heart-stopping writing comes from synthesizing the previously unarticulated in the moment. Rather than reaching for your database, try channeling what's in the air at this very second.

> I am waiting for any evidence that our most provocative thinkers and writers are those who rely on elaborate, systematic note-taking systems.

Source: [sashachapin.substack.com](https://sashachapin.substack.com/p/notes-against-note-taking-systems)
*Why these passages*: Creation happens now, not from archives. The second is a challenge no one in the note-taking community has answered.

---

### Tara Isabella Burton – "Rational Magic" (undated)

> In a way, you can summarise post rationality, as, like, "how do you get the sacred without violence."

> There's a sense where modernity is kind of great, because sacredness leads to irrationality, problems, wars etc. That maybe trading off a bit of guaranteed meaning for more choice, warm homes and less violence ... is actually a pretty good deal. At the same time, [postrationalists] are basically all mourning the loss of enchantment.

Source: [The New Atlantis](https://www.thenewatlantis.com/publications/rational-magic)
*Why these passages*: The informality carries the philosophical weight. Modernity's trade-off was real (meaning for safety), and the project is trying to get both.

## Reading This Document

The tonal registers map roughly to the shelf lanes:
- **Declarative** → technology + intervention (Berry, Jacobs, Benatar, Le Guin)
- **Analytical-playful** → perception (Bogost, Rao, Sull, Perry)
- **Craft voice** → craft (Victor, Mod, Chimero, McCoy)
- **Critical-cultural** → perception + intervention (Steyerl, Appleton)
- **Heretical** → stream (Chapin, Burton)

But the registers cross lanes. Berry is declarative AND craft. Victor is craft AND analytical. Perry is analytical AND heretical. The interesting authors resist a single register – and so does Lee.
