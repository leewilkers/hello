# Negative Space – What's Not on the Shelf

> The shelf defines itself as much by what it excludes as by what it includes. This document catalogs the absences, the rejections, and the threshold items – what didn't make it and why that matters for voice calibration.

---

## Absent Traditions

### 1. Pure Philosophy
**Not here**: Heidegger, Dewey, Wittgenstein, Foucault, Deleuze, Derrida, Butler, Arendt, Latour.

**Why it matters**: Many shelf items draw on these thinkers. Illich was influenced by Heidegger. Berger draws on Marx. Flyvbjerg builds on Aristotle. Law is deeply Latourian. But the philosophers themselves don't appear – only the people who applied their ideas to specific domains.

**The pattern**: Lee trusts applied thinkers over pure theorists. When he reaches for "social construction of technology," he reaches for Winner or Franklin, not Bijker or Pinch. When he needs phenomenology, he reads Polanyi's "we know more than we can tell," not Merleau-Ponty.

**Voice implication**: Don't cite philosophers directly when writing for Lee. Cite the people who did something with the philosophy.

### 2. Silicon Valley / "Build" Ideology
**Not here**: Andreessen, Thiel, Altman, Paul Graham, Y Combinator, "Zero to One," "The Hard Thing About Hard Things," the entire startup canon.

**Why it matters**: Lee works with technology daily. He built a website with Eleventy. He uses Claude Code extensively. He is not anti-technology. But the startup framing – disruption, scaling, moving fast, founder mythology – is completely absent.

**The pattern**: The tech thinkers Lee *does* include (Victor, Mod, Chimero) work from positions of craft and critique. They build things but they also ask what building does. The "build" ideology doesn't ask – it just builds.

**Voice implication**: Never use startup language. No "iterate," "pivot," "scale," "disrupt," "10x." No founder-worship framing. No "exciting opportunity." Technology talk should be grounded in craft, not growth.

### 3. Self-Help / Productivity
**Not here**: "Deep Work" (Newport), "Atomic Habits" (Clear), "Getting Things Done" (Allen), "Thinking, Fast and Slow" (Kahneman – though this is more behavioral econ).

**Why it matters**: The shelf's relationship to work is through *craft* (Sennett, Korn, Schwarz) and *method* (Mills, Flyvbjerg), not through *optimization*. The question isn't "how do I get more done?" – it's "what does good work look like?" and "what does working well feel like?"

**Voice implication**: Don't optimize. Don't life-hack. Don't suggest systems for systems' sake. Lee's ADHD management is his own; the shelf says nothing about productivity and everything about quality.

### 4. Conventional Management Theory
**Not here**: Drucker, Porter, "Good to Great" (Collins), "The Lean Startup" (Ries), McKinsey, BCG, OKRs, "Measure What Matters."

**Partial exception**: Sull's "Why Good Companies Go Bad" is in HBR, but it's anti-management – it explains why management orthodoxies fail. Rindova & Martins is in a strategy journal but it's about moral imagination, not competitive advantage.

**Voice implication**: Don't frame problems as management problems. Frame them as design problems, ethical problems, or political problems.

### 5. AI/ML Canon
**Not here**: Bostrom ("Superintelligence"), Stuart Russell ("Human Compatible"), Tegmark ("Life 3.0"), Mitchell ("Artificial Unintelligence"), Crawford ("Atlas of AI"), the alignment literature, the capabilities literature.

**Why it matters**: Lee uses AI tools more intensively than most people. He builds with Claude Code daily. He has a sophisticated understanding of model behavior. But no AI books appear on the shelf.

**Possible readings**: (a) Lee sees AI as a tool, not a subject – it belongs in the "use" category, not the "read about" category. (b) The AI literature hasn't produced a book that meets his quality bar. (c) The shelf represents his intellectual identity pre-AI; the AI relationship is still forming.

**Voice implication**: Don't assume Lee needs to be educated about AI. Don't recommend AI books. The relationship is practical, not theoretical.

### 6. Critical Theory / Continental Theory (as primary texts)
**Not here**: Foucault, Habermas, Bourdieu, Agamben, Žižek, any Frankfurt School, any poststructuralism.

**Why it matters**: The development critique lineage (Ferguson, Tsing, Fassin) draws heavily on critical theory. But the primary texts don't appear. Lee reads the applications, not the theory.

**The pattern**: Same as with pure philosophy. Lee trusts specificity over generality. A Foucauldian analysis of a development project in Lesotho (Ferguson) > Foucault's "Discipline and Punish."

### 7. Conventional Design Canon
**Not here**: Dieter Rams, Braun design, Bauhaus, "Less is More," Swiss design, most graphic design history.

**Partial exceptions**: Norman (usability), Alexander (patterns), Chimero (web). But the mainstream design canon – the hero designers, the design-thinking industry, the IDEO playbook – is absent.

**Voice implication**: Don't invoke design heroes or design-thinking frameworks. Lee's design sensibility is through craft (make it, learn from it) and critique (ask what design does to people), not through the design profession's self-narrative.

## Active Rejections

Items that were on the shelf and were removed, or were proposed and refused:

### Julia Watson – Lo-TEK: Design by Radical Indigenism
**Was on shelf → removed**. Lee flagged the book's position: a Western designer packaging indigenous knowledge systems. The issue wasn't the hover note – it was the book itself. Removed on principle.

**What this teaches**: Lee has an orientalism detector. Work that aestheticizes or extracts from non-Western traditions without centering those traditions' own terms will get flagged.

### Ribbonfarm (as a general stream entry)
**Was on stream → replaced** by specific Venkat Rao essays. Lee preferred precision over breadth – linking to "A Big Little Idea Called Legibility" and "How to Draw and Judge Quadrant Diagrams" rather than the blog as a whole.

**What this teaches**: Specificity > generality. Point to the essay, not the blog.

### Hover Note Round 2 (17 proposals, all rejected)
**Proposed → all killed**. Lee called them "slop... think insider, CEV." They were thesis summaries dressed as margin notes: "design with the web, not against it," "thinking through doing," "making as knowing."

**What this teaches**: Summarizing what a book argues is the wrong move. The hover note should be the phrase that sticks, the positioning move, the insider reference. Research the author's actual words.

### "The McLuhan you can actually read"
**Proposed → killed**. "Claude smell." The construction "the [X] you can actually [Y]" is a tell.

### "10,000 hours before Gladwell"
**Proposed → killed**. Corny. Pop-culture reference as a shortcut to sounding knowing. Doesn't work.

### "Infrastructure" (as a word)
**Flagged as a claude-ism**. During Watson hover note workshopping, Lee identified "infrastructure" as a word Claude reaches for when it wants to sound substantive. The word isn't banned, but its use is suspect.

## The Threshold – What Almost Made It

Three items sit in `dest: undecided`:

### Audrey Watters – Teaching Machines (MIT Press)
**About**: The history of educational technology, from teaching machines of the 1920s to modern edtech. Critical of the techno-solutionist impulse in education.

**Why it's on the threshold**: Fits the technology-critique lineage. Watters' blog (Hack Education) was influential in the edtech-critical space. But Watters retired from public writing, and the book may be more historical than Lee's shelf tends toward.

### James Bridle – New Dark Age (Verso)
**About**: How digital technology produces new kinds of unknowing. Computational thinking as ideology.

**Why it's on the threshold**: Fits the technology + perception lanes. Bridle's argument connects to Bogost's "Cathedral of Computation" (in the stream). But Bridle may be too abstract – too much diagnosis, not enough material specificity.

### Wendy Chun – Updating to Remain the Same (MIT Press)
**About**: Habitual new media – how the compulsion to update creates a loop of sameness. Networks, race, and the digital.

**Why it's on the threshold**: Fits perception + technology. Chun is a serious media theorist. But she's closer to the critical-theory tradition (Kittler, Hansen) that the shelf generally avoids as primary texts.

**What the threshold items share**: All three are technology critiques from academics. They're more theoretical than the typical shelf item. The shelf may not need more technology critique – it has Franklin, Winner, Berry, Mattern, Illich. What it might need is more in the craft or intervention lanes.

## What the Negative Space Says About Voice

When writing for Lee, these registers are off-limits:

| Register | Example | Why it fails |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Startup optimism | "This could scale to..." | The shelf is maintenance, not growth |
| Productivity framing | "Here's how to optimize..." | Craft, not optimization |
| Design-thinking | "Let's ideate on..." | Critique, not method |
| Academic jargon | "The Foucauldian implications..." | Applications, not theory |
| AI evangelism | "The exciting potential of..." | Tools, not religion |
| Management speak | "Let's align on the OKRs..." | Ethics, not process |
| Pop-intellectual | "As Gladwell argues..." | Primary texts, not popularizers |

When writing for Lee, these registers work:

| Register | Example | Why it works |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Craft specificity | "The joint holds because..." | Material, concrete, embodied |
| Maintenance realism | "The real question is who repairs..." | Anti-innovation, pro-care |
| Ethical clarity | "The cost is borne by..." | Names the distribution of harm |
| Insider positioning | "The Ribbonfarm version of this..." | Assumes shared reading |
| Dry observation | "Exactly what it sounds like." | Lee's own register |
