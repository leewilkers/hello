## Sarah Perry -- "Ritual and the Consciousness Monoculture" (2015)

### The move

Our form of consciousness -- literate, time-bound, self-burdened -- is not the human default but a particular strain that swept populations like a gene sweep, erasing previous variety. Perry calls it "scholastic-industrial consciousness." It produces a uniquely painful selfhood: the irreconcilable gap between first-person specialness and third-person averageness (Rochat), made worse by the proliferation of "mirrors" (grades, photography, social media) and the recruitment of the self as the primary locus of meaning after religion and tradition erode (Baumeister). The participatory group rituals that once offered escape from this overburdened self have been replaced by spectacle -- watching rather than dancing, listening rather than making. The essay argues that rhythmic, participatory ritual is a "social vitamin" we are starving without.

### Passages that stuck

1. "People have always had selves, but selves have not always had to carry the burden of supplying meaning to life in such a far-reaching fashion." (Baumeister) [the overburdened self -- why the modern condition is structurally painful, not just hard]

2. "To be an atomized individual self, whose actions are under one's rational control and express one's sincere, authentic intent at all times is a peculiar, modern way to be human. Ritual is the normal way to be human." [inverts the default: authenticity is the aberration, ritual is the norm]

3. "If he believed he was a transmitter, rather than a creator, then we'd be able to see what his talents really were." (Johnstone) [the Eskimo carver waiting for the bone's shape vs. the modern artist burdened with self-expression]

4. "The one trend in confraternal statutes more eye-catching than the decrease in ritual devotions is the increase in procedures to regulate and reduce internal discord." (Barnes) [the institutional diagnosis: more rules, less ritual]

5. "The rebirth of ritual is the most deadly serious play." [the final sentence -- honest about the danger, not prescriptive]

### Where it connects

- **Perry, "Deep Laziness" (2018)**: The overburdened self is the same self that scheduled time torments. Ritual offers collective, occasional escape through dissolution; deep laziness offers individual, ongoing escape through fit. The confraternity's collapse -- replacing ritual with regulation -- is Alexander's fabricated structure replacing generated structure.

- **Perry, "Tendrils of Mess" (2017)**: Masking in "Tendrils" (covering incompatible orders so something can be perceived) is the spatial version of what ritual does to the self: masking the painful third-person view so the group experience can emerge. Politeness as social masking appears in both essays.

- **Sennett, *The Craftsman* (shelf)**: Johnstone's transmitter/creator maps onto Sennett's hand-teaching-head. The Eskimo carver is Polanyi's tacit knowledge in practice -- receptive, structure-preserving. Scholastic-industrial consciousness reframes making as self-expression, which is the burden Perry diagnoses.

- **Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (shelf)**: "There is something important in rhythmic rituals that cannot be fully captured in scholastic-industrial rational thought; it must be experienced to be understood." The ritual state as collective tacit knowledge.

- **Berger, *Ways of Seeing* (shelf)**: Both argue that what we experience is conditioned -- by images (Berger) or by culture and ritual (Perry). Berger's "seeing comes before words" and Rochat's "awareness of oneself from the imagined perspective of other minds" locate the fundamental human faculty in social perception, not individual cognition.

### What Lee does with it

DRAFT -- "More rules, less ritual" as a three-word diagnosis of institutional decay. The sincerity ideology -- "authentic leadership," "bring your whole self to work," "radical transparency" -- reframed as a historically specific cultural product, not a universal good. Ritual (structured check-ins, defined roles, regular handoffs) may be more humane than demanded authenticity. The Maria Sabina parable for any extractive engagement with others' practices: wellness tourism, corporate mindfulness, imported design thinking workshops.

### What it does NOT say

No politics of ritual -- who controls access, whose rituals are protected vs. extracted vs. criminalized. The self-flagellation case is thin (one historical example carrying a structural claim). No engagement with ritual traditions that survived modernity and what enabled their persistence. No prescription -- deliberately diagnostic, ending with a warning rather than a program.
