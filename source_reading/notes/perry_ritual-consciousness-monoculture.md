## The Move

Modern consciousness -- literate, self-conscious, time-bound, identity-heavy -- is not *the* human consciousness but *a* particular strain that swept across populations the way a beneficial gene sweeps a genome. Perry calls it "scholastic-industrial consciousness." It produces a novel and painful experience of selfhood (the irreconcilable gap between first-person specialness and third-person averageness), and the participatory group rituals that once provided escape from the overburdened self have been systematically replaced by spectacle. The essay is a diagnosis of this deprivation and a cautious argument that rhythmic, participatory ritual -- not sincerity, not authenticity, not self-expression -- is the missing nutrient.

## Section-by-Section Notes

### Opening (Selective sweep as analogy)

- Argument: The gene-sweep analogy frames consciousness as subject to the same competitive dynamics as languages and alleles. One form of consciousness can outcompete others not because it is better for human flourishing but because it is more contagious or more compatible with institutional power.
- Key passage: "I simply argue that there are and have been many forms of human consciousness, varying in particular ways, that we retain the 'hardware' capability for many forms of consciousness, and that humans are constrained into particular mental states by their cultures, especially through group ritual (or lack thereof)."
- Concepts: **Consciousness monoculture** -- the reduction of variety in subjective experience, analogous to genetic or linguistic monoculture. Not a metaphor -- Perry means it literally.

### Scholastic-Industrial Consciousness

- Argument: Names the specific form of consciousness that swept. It provides particular experiences of time, identity, self, others, reality, and the divine. It is literate, accelerated by printing, and reaches its ultimate form (with subversion) in the internet. "Scholastic-industrial" because transmitted by schooling and selected for by industrialization.
- Key passage: "Since it appears to be transmitted by schooling and since it is the form of consciousness most conducive to industrialization, it may be thought of as scholastic-industrial consciousness."
- Concepts: Via Sorenson -- consciousness varies along sense-of-name, sense-of-space, sense-of-number, sense-of-truth, and sense-of-emotion. Perry treats these as real axes, not metaphors.

### Axes of Variation: Time

- Argument: The time-rate wage (Szabo) made standard time a cognitive requirement. Punctuality is not natural -- it is a sacrifice of a previous way of being in time. The trade-off made sense when human labor was irreplaceably valuable; it is less defensible now.
- Key passage: "Coordinated punctuality comes at the cost of each person living most of his hours aware of the wider world's standard time, which is a novel way of being human."
- Concepts: **Standard time as consciousness constraint** -- clock time is not a neutral measuring device but a restructuring of subjective experience. Mumford's "the clock, not the steam engine" via a different route.

### Axes of Variation: Identity

- Argument: Fixed legal names, permanent records, accumulated identity-information -- all novel. Sorenson on New Guinea naming practices: names were "improvised, not formally bestowed," often "whimsical." Everett on the Piraha trading names with spirits. The internet briefly promised anonymity; surveillance capital foreclosed that.
- Key passage: "In these preconquest regions of New Guinea names were rarely binding. What one was called varied according to time, place, mood, and setting." (Sorenson)
- Concepts: **Identity fixity** as a feature of scholastic-industrial consciousness, not human nature. The volume of information attached to a modern identity is without precedent.

### Axes of Variation: The Self

- Argument: The longest and densest section. Rochat's core insight: the irreconcilable gap between the beloved first-person self and the neutrally-evaluated third-person self. Mirrors (common only since the 19th century) are one "source of accurate (which is to say painful) information about the self." School, photography, social networks are all mirrors. Baumeister's argument: as religion, shared morality, and tradition eroded as sources of meaning, the self was left holding the entire burden of value. Self-deception is the normal coping mechanism; "seeing things as they really are is associated with depression and low self-esteem."
- Key passage: "People have always had selves, but selves have not always had to carry the burden of supplying meaning to life in such a far-reaching fashion. The reason for the modern fascination with the self, then, is that the self has been made into a fundamental and powerful source of value in modern life." (Baumeister)
- Key passage: The Biamis' reaction to mirrors -- "the tribal terror of self-recognition" (Carpenter via Rochat). Paralyzed, covering mouths, hiding heads. "Only their stomach muscles betraying great tension."
- Concepts:
  - **The overburdened self** -- the self recruited as the primary locus of meaning once other sources erode. Not narcissism but necessity.
  - **Mirrors (literal and social)** -- any source of accurate third-person information about the self. Grades, erotic ranking (Collins), photography, social media. All deflating.
  - **Self-deception as adaptation** -- healthy people maintain unrealistically positive self-images. Realism correlates with depression. The modern environment overwhelms this defense with too many mirrors.

### Constrained Toward Consciousness

- Argument: Rochat again -- human consciousness is specifically the awareness of oneself from the imagined perspective of other minds. Others *constrain* us into this form of consciousness. Groups therefore have enormous power over mental states, including the power to induce ritual states.
- Key passage: "We are forced, by others, to be aware of ourselves from their perspective -- to have others in mind."

### Hiving and Rhythmic Entrainment

- Argument: Haidt's "hive hypothesis" -- people need to lose themselves in emergent social organisms to reach the highest levels of flourishing. Rhythmic entrainment (synchronized motion to a beat) is the mechanism: it eases pain, facilitates cooperation, produces bravery, and (darker) enables aggression against out-groups. Humans are among the only animals capable of it. The Piraha dance for three days at a time.
- Key passage: "The hive hypothesis states that 'people need to lose themselves occasionally by becoming part of an emergent social organism in order to reach the highest levels of human flourishing.'" (Haidt)
- Concepts: **Hiving** -- the dissolution of individual selfhood into group identity through synchronized rhythm. Not metaphor but mechanism: decreased DLPFC activation, increased social bonding.

### Authenticity, Sincerity, and the Suspicion of Ritual

- Argument: The critique of ritual-as-empty is itself a feature of scholastic-industrial consciousness. Seligman and Weller trace the elevation of sincerity and disdain for ritual to Kant's focus on personal intent. To be "an atomized individual self, whose actions are under one's rational control and express one's sincere, authentic intent at all times is a peculiar, modern way to be human. Ritual is the normal way to be human."
- Key passage: "Our disdain for 'empty rituals' is our loss: rituals are what help us navigate the necessary ambiguities of social life, into which rational codification can never fully penetrate."
- Concepts: **Sincerity as ideology** -- the elevation of authenticity over ritual performance is not a universal human value but a specific historical product. Hugging replacing handshakes, then the 20-second hug -- an escalation of required intimacy in the space of a greeting.

### Distracting the Watcher at the Gates

- Argument: Johnstone's "watcher at the gates of the mind" (via Schiller) -- the censorious self-consciousness that kills spontaneity. Perry locates it in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC). Rappers freestyling and jazz musicians improvising both show decreased DLPFC activation. Dietrich's "transient hypofrontality hypothesis": dreaming, endurance running, meditation, and drug states share DLPFC inhibition. Rhythmic ritual does this in a social context.
- Key passage: "If he believed he was a transmitter, rather than a creator, then we'd be able to see what his talents really were." (Johnstone)
- Key passage: "Once we believe that art is self-expression, then the individual can be criticised not only for his skill or lack of skill, but simply for being what he is." (Johnstone)
- Concepts:
  - **The watcher** -- internalized self-surveillance that gates creative and spontaneous behavior. The DLPFC as its neural address.
  - **Transmitter vs. creator** -- Johnstone's distinction. The Eskimo carver waiting for the shape inside the bone vs. the modern artist burdened with "self-expression." A pre-scholastic relationship to making.
  - **Transient hypofrontality** -- the shared neural signature of altered states. Rhythmic ritual as a social technology for inducing it.

### Self-Flagellation as Institutional Glue

- Argument: Historical case study. French penitential confraternities spread rapidly when built around the rhythmic ritual of self-flagellation; collapsed when they replaced flagellation with meditation and community service. They diagnosed their problem as "internal discord" and prescribed more regulation -- exactly the wrong fix.
- Key passage: "It can be argued that the rituals performed by confraternities early in their existence were crucial for the maintenance of group and organizational cohesion." (Barnes)
- Key passage: "The one trend in confraternal statutes more eye-catching than the decrease in ritual devotions is the increase in procedures to regulate and reduce internal discord."
- Concepts: **Regulation as failed substitute for ritual** -- when organizations lose their binding rituals, they try to replace cohesion with rules. The rules address symptoms (discord) while accelerating the cause (loss of shared ritual experience).

### Cautions

- Argument: Ritual as vitamin -- necessary but dangerous in wrong doses. Chicken-egg problem: ritual requires community, community requires ritual. Outsiders can destroy rituals by extracting them from context (Maria Sabina's mushroom ceremony overrun by hippies). Ritual states are contagious -- Goodman's accidental glossolalia. "The rebirth of ritual is the most deadly serious play."
- Key passage: "In Maria Sabina's words, the mushrooms began to lose their power after the coming of the foreigners."
- Concepts: **Ritual as vitamin / ritual as drug** -- both analogies simultaneously. Necessary, powerful, dangerous, context-dependent.

## Vocabulary

- **Scholastic-industrial consciousness** -- the literate, time-bound, identity-heavy, self-burdened form of consciousness transmitted by schooling and selected for by industrialization
- **Consciousness monoculture** -- the loss of subjective variety as one form of consciousness outcompetes others
- **The overburdened self** -- the self pressed into service as the primary locus of meaning after religion, morality, and tradition erode
- **Mirrors** -- any source of accurate third-person information about the self (literal mirrors, grades, erotic ranking, photography, social media)
- **Hiving** -- dissolution of individual selfhood into group identity through synchronized rhythm (Haidt)
- **Rhythmic entrainment** -- synchronized motion to a musical beat; the mechanism of hiving
- **Transient hypofrontality** -- decreased prefrontal cortex activation shared by dreaming, endurance running, meditation, improvisation, and ritual (Dietrich)
- **The watcher at the gates** -- internalized self-surveillance that gates spontaneity; lives in the DLPFC (Johnstone via Schiller)
- **Sincerity as ideology** -- the historically specific elevation of authenticity and personal intent over ritual performance (Seligman & Weller, tracing to Kant)
- **Social vitamins** -- rituals as necessary nutrients provided by ancestral social "diets" but absent from modern life

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Perry, "Tendrils of Mess in our Brains" (2017)**: "Ritual" provides the social and phenomenological theory that "Tendrils" applies to visual perception. Masking in "Tendrils" -- covering aspects of reality so others can be perceived -- is the spatial version of what ritual does to the self: masking the painful third-person view so the group experience can be felt. Perry's concept of politeness as social masking (in "Ritual") becomes the formal theory in "Tendrils."

2. **Perry, "Deep Laziness" (2018)**: The self-flagellation case study is the institutional version of what "Deep Laziness" describes individually. When the confraternities lost their rhythmic ritual, they replaced it with regulation -- scheduled time (legible, top-down) instead of elaborated time (emergent, ritual). The confraternity's decay mirrors Alexander's distinction between generated and fabricated structure: the ritual was generated (emergent, structure-preserving); the replacement statutes were fabricated (imposed, legible).

3. **Sennett, *The Craftsman* (shelf)**: Johnstone's "transmitter vs. creator" distinction maps onto Sennett's argument that the hand teaches the head. The Eskimo carver waiting for the bone's shape is Polanyi's tacit knowledge in practice -- the maker as receiver, not imposer. Scholastic-industrial consciousness reframes making as self-expression, which is exactly the burden Perry diagnoses.

4. **Berger, *Ways of Seeing* (shelf)**: Berger's argument that images condition perception parallels Perry's argument that culture conditions consciousness. Both are about what we are *constrained* to experience. Berger's "seeing comes before words" operates in the same territory as Rochat's "awareness of oneself from the imagined perspective of other minds" -- both locate the fundamental human capacity in a social, not individual, faculty.

5. **Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (shelf)**: "There is something important in rhythmic rituals that cannot be fully captured in scholastic-industrial rational thought; it must be experienced to be understood." This is Polanyi's "we know more than we can tell," applied to embodied group practice rather than individual skill. The ritual state is tacit knowledge of a collective.

## Limits

- Perry is cautious about who should do what rituals, but the essay does not address power. Who controls access to ritual? Whose rituals get protected, whose get extracted (Maria Sabina), whose get criminalized? The hippie problem is treated as a cautionary tale about context, not as a question about cultural power.
- The self-flagellation case is thin. One historical example from one context, mediated through Barnes' interpretation. It does the work Perry needs -- demonstrating that rhythmic ritual generates cohesion that meditation and community service don't -- but it's a single data point carrying a structural claim.
- The essay leaves the "what to do" question deliberately open. No recommendations, no program, no examples of successful modern ritual recovery. This is honest -- Perry says we "must be very cautious" -- but it means the essay is almost entirely diagnostic. The closest to a prescription is the final sentence: "the rebirth of ritual is the most deadly serious play."
- No engagement with ritual traditions that *survived* modernity (liturgical Christianity, Orthodox Judaism, monastic practice, martial arts) and what their survival conditions were. The framing is all loss, which obscures the question of what enables persistence.
