## Ursula Franklin -- *The Real World of Technology* (1989)

### The move

Franklin defines technology as practice -- "ways of doing something" -- and distinguishes holistic technologies (where the doer controls the process) from prescriptive technologies (where the process controls the doer). Prescriptive technologies, dominant since Chinese bronze foundries and turbocharged by the Industrial Revolution, produce a "culture of compliance" that now structures not just factories but governance, education, and daily life. The six Massey Lectures build from this distinction to an analysis of how prescriptive systems eliminate reciprocity, generate infrastructures that serve private gain at public expense, turn citizens into "plannees," and follow a lifecycle from liberation rhetoric to dependency. The positive program is thin by design -- Franklin refuses to offer "a blueprint to cure the faults of all other blueprints" -- but the diagnostic framework is the most usable technology-critique tool on the shelf.

### Passages that stuck

1. "In political terms, prescriptive technologies are designs for compliance." (p. 23) [diagnostic -- the sentence that carries the book]

2. "Whenever human activities incorporate machines or rigidly prescribed procedures, the modes of human interaction change. In general, technical arrangements reduce or eliminate reciprocity." (p. 48) [structural -- the mechanism by which technology changes relationships]

3. "What turns the promised liberation into enslavement are not the products of technology per se -- the car, the computer, or the sewing machine -- but the structures and infrastructures that are put in place to facilitate the use of these products and to develop dependency on them." (p. 102) [structural -- why the problem is never the device]

4. "If somebody robs a store, it's a crime... But if somebody steals from the commons and from the future, it's seen as entrepreneurial activity and the state cheers and gives them tax concessions." (p. 122) [ethical-clarity -- justice reframed]

5. "Social change will not come to us like an avalanche down the mountain. Social change will come through seeds growing in well prepared soil -- and it is we, like the earthworms, who prepare the soil." (p. 121) [dry -- the image that persists]

6. "Whenever someone talks to you about the benefits and costs of a particular project, don't ask 'What benefits?' ask 'Whose benefits and whose costs?'" (p. 124) [practical -- the question to carry into every room]

### Where it connects

1. **Illich -- Tools for Conviviality**: Franklin's holistic/prescriptive is Illich's convivial/manipulatory analyzed at the level of the division of labour. She cites him directly on "shadow work" and "vernacular values." The lineage runs Illich -> Franklin -> Russell & Vinsel.

2. **Berry -- "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer"**: Berry's nine standards are Franklin's holistic-technology criteria restated as a personal checklist. Both writing in the late 1980s, apparently independently. Berry's "technological fundamentalism" is Franklin's culture of compliance seen from the dissenter's side.

3. **Russell & Vinsel -- "Hail the Maintainers"**: Franklin is the explicit precursor. Her mechanical-bride lifecycle (liberation -> standardization -> captivity) is what they'll diagnose as the innovation delusion. Her invisible developers (telephone operators, secretaries) anticipate Mattern's maintenance arguments by decades.

4. **Ferguson -- The Anti-Politics Machine**: Franklin's argument that technological infrastructures absorb political decisions into technical settings runs parallel to Ferguson's depoliticization diagnosis. Both: power exercised through design rather than declaration.

5. **Winner -- The Whale and the Reactor**: Cited directly. The Robert Moses overpass example is shared territory. Franklin extends Winner from artifacts to the infrastructures that normalize them.

### What Lee does with it

[DRAFT] The holistic/prescriptive distinction is the most-used framework from Lee's shelf in consulting. It shows up whenever a program that looked good on paper produces compliance instead of engagement -- when a training protocol flattens situated judgment, when a reporting system displaces the relationships it was supposed to serve, when standardizing a process across contexts kills what made it work in each one. "Designs for compliance" names the political content of a process design without requiring anyone to have intended it. In global health ethics consulting, where prescriptive protocols are the medium of the work, the question "Is this holistic or prescriptive?" is a diagnostic that opens conversations nothing else can. "Whose benefits and whose costs?" is the question Lee carries into every review.

### What it does NOT say

Positive program is deliberately thin -- earthworm theory is a posture, not a strategy; "redemptive technologies" is a category gesture, not a developed concept. Gender analysis is present but underdeveloped. No Global South beyond passing mentions. The 1989 computing discussion is pre-internet; the framework (liberation -> dependency -> captivity) applies more forcefully to 2026 than to anything Franklin could have foreseen, but the examples need updating.
