## James Ferguson -- *The Anti-Politics Machine: "Development," Depoliticization, and Bureaucratic Power in Lesotho* (1990)

### The move

Development projects fail at their stated goals but succeed at something else entirely: extending state bureaucratic power into resistant regions while stripping poverty of its political dimensions. Ferguson's question is not "why do development projects fail?" but "what do they actually do?" The development apparatus in Lesotho is "not a machine for eliminating poverty that is incidentally involved with state bureaucracy; it is a machine for reinforcing and expanding the exercise of bureaucratic state power, which incidentally takes 'poverty' as its point of entry." Two instrument-effects: depoliticization (poverty rendered technical, political causes erased) and expansion of state control (government presence established in previously ungoverned opposition territory). Neither was intended by the planners. Both were produced by the machine.

### Passages that stuck

1. "it is a machine for reinforcing and expanding the exercise of bureaucratic state power, which incidentally takes 'poverty' as its point of entry -- launching an intervention that may have no effect on the poverty but does in fact have other concrete effects." [structural -- the title concept; what the machine actually produces]

2. "Like goodness itself, development in our time is a value so firmly entrenched that it seems almost impossible to question it or to refer it to any standard beyond its own." [diagnostic -- why the machine is hard to challenge; moral immunity]

3. "The thoughts and actions of 'development' bureaucrats are powerfully shaped by the world of acceptable statements and utterances within which they live; and what they do and do not do is a product not only of the interests of various nations, classes, or international agencies, but also and at the same time, of a working out of this complex structure of knowledge." [mechanism -- discourse as the operating system; planners are not dumb, they are embedded]

4. "It is perhaps reasonable to suggest that it may even be because development projects turn out to have such uses, even if they are in some sense unforeseen, that they continue to attract so much interest and support." [explanatory -- why "failed" projects get replicated; the uses of failure]

5. "70 percent of the average household income in the project area was from wage labor in South Africa, with only 6 percent from farming." [empirical -- the single figure that demolishes the World Bank's subsistence-agriculture framing of Lesotho]

### Where it connects

- **Packard, *History of Global Health***: Packard documents across a century the pattern Ferguson identifies in one project -- magic-bullet intervention, failure, renewed optimism, repeat. Ferguson provides the mechanism (discourse produces the intervention); Packard provides the historical record.

- **Krause, *The Good Project***: Krause opens the machine and shows the gears -- NGO organizational logic, donor narratives, projectification. Ferguson sees the machine from outside; Krause from inside. Together: the machine has its own logic, and individual agency within it is constrained.

- **Tsing, *Friction***: Tsing generalizes Ferguson's Lesotho finding. Global connections are always mediated by local friction. The development machine arrives with its discourse; the Basotho cattle economy is the friction that reveals what the discourse cannot see.

- **Franklin, *Real World of Technology***: Ferguson's development apparatus is a prescriptive technology. The system controls the planner, the discourse controls the outcome, the machine produces its outputs regardless of intention. Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction is the theoretical frame Ferguson illustrates empirically.

- **Benatar, "Moral Imagination"**: Ferguson showed one project depoliticizing; Benatar shows the entire aid system doing it at the global level. Benatar's "eclipse" -- aid visibility obscuring extraction -- is the system-level anti-politics machine.

### What Lee does with it

DRAFT -- The foundational frame for the intervention lane. Three consulting applications: (1) the depoliticization diagnosis -- when an institution renders a problem as technical, ask what political dimensions the framing erases; (2) the instrument-effects lens -- when a programme fails at stated goals, ask what it succeeded at instead (what capacity was built, what populations came under new oversight); (3) the "behind the backs" principle -- intentions are analytically irrelevant, good people running a bad machine produce the machine's outputs. The book also anchors the entire dev-critique lineage: Tsing, Fassin, Packard, Adams, Krause all work in the space Ferguson opened.

### What it does NOT say

No programme for doing development better -- Ferguson explicitly refuses prescription. No account of individual agency within the machine -- development workers appear as gears, not actors (the strongest critique, from Mosse, Li, de Vries). Fieldwork is from 1982--83 in one small landlocked country with unique features (near-total dependence on South Africa, migrant labor economy). Whether the machine operates identically in larger, more complex settings is an empirical question Ferguson acknowledges but cannot answer.
