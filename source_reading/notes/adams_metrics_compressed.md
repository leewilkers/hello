## Vincanne Adams (ed.) -- *Metrics: What Counts in Global Health* (2016)

### The move

An edited collection showing that global health metrics are not neutral measurement tools but political technologies. The DALY "fiscalizes" life -- converts quality of life into a monetary-instrument-like entity for resource allocation. The RCT becomes "the new Lord Chancellor" -- the one metric to rule them all, determining what gets funded, studied, and scaled. Eight ethnographic chapters show the ground-level consequences: maternal deaths erased for political survival in Malawi and Nigeria, a data retention strike in Senegal, Yup'ik healing forced into logic models, and nonprofits warped by the demand for countable "widgets." Adams's framing argument: metrics claim political neutrality and thereby become a form of politics in their own right, authorizing new conduits between profit-seeking and health delivery while displacing other forms of evidence and care.

### Passages that stuck

1. "There is a very fine line between using RCTs to do the sort of health work we want to do and doing only the sort of work that RCTs can be designed to do." [structural -- the epistemological trap: the tool for measuring effectiveness becomes the gatekeeper for action]

2. "The DALY abstracts quality of life and turns it into a fiscally meaningful form -- that is, into an ontological entity that stabilizes messy complexities of 'living,' that turns life into an object that behaves the way a monetary instrument would behave." [definitional -- the fiscalization of life, the DALY's real achievement]

3. "Other kinds of evidence may be more reliable than numbers. In this sense, quantification strategies and the metrics we rely on to avoid politics often do not avoid politics at all; they become a form of politics in their own right." [diagnostic -- the neutrality claim as political act]

4. "Three of the four numbers used to calculate paradoxically precise-looking uncertainty intervals were based mostly on our own intuition, as appropriate empirical evidence is lacking." [Wendland -- the "wobbly numbers" that look like facts but rest on guesswork]

5. "Since the hospital had only had one other death that month, adding Blessing's death would have doubled the maternal mortality rate. But because she had left the police clinic still breathing, she would not be added to their death toll." [Oni-Orisan -- the mechanism: a woman's death erased because counting it would double the rate]

6. "These days, lacking data may be even more tragic than lacking health." [Adams on the World Bank's "data deprivation" -- the logical endpoint of metrics ideology]

### Where it connects

1. **Ferguson, *Anti-Politics Machine*** -- Ferguson showed one development project depoliticizing through the apparatus of aid. Adams shows metrics as the contemporary depoliticization mechanism: numbers claim neutrality and thereby do political work invisibly. Oni-Orisan's Nigeria is Ferguson's Lesotho running through health statistics.

2. **Packard, *History of Global Health*** -- Packard documented the recurring magic-bullet cycle. Adams shows the latest iteration: the RCT as the magic bullet for knowing what works. Same structural blindness, new instrument.

3. **Krause, *The Good Project*** -- Krause showed NGO logic fragmenting reason. Adams's volume shows the same fragmentation through metrics: problems siloed to fit measurement, relational work translated into widgets, missions reshaped to match funder demands.

4. **Tsing, *Friction*** -- The metrics aspire to smooth, universal comparability. Each chapter is a friction story: local conditions resist, subvert, or weaponize the demand for numbers. Senegalese nurses withholding data is political friction made deliberate.

5. **Benatar, "Moral Imagination"** -- Benatar's "silo approaches" are what metrics logic produces. Adams gives the mechanism; Benatar names the moral deficit.

### What Lee does with it

[DRAFT] The consulting frame that lives in this book: when an institution foregrounds its evidence base, the Adams question is -- what does the evidence framework make invisible? What can't be counted, and what happens to it? The volume is strongest on the *mechanism* by which metrics reshape practice: not just bad numbers but the cascade from counting regime to funding conduit to clinical distortion. The DALY-to-RCT-to-investment-fund pipeline in chapter 1 is the most important argument -- the one that connects Blessing's erased death in Nigeria to JPMorgan Chase's Global Health Investment Fund. Pairs with Ferguson for structural analysis of how the apparatus works, with Krause for organizational-level distortion, and with Packard for the recurring cycle that never learns from itself. The Hales chapter on Yup'ik sovereignty is the most emotionally acute -- metrics as a colonization mechanism, requiring indigenous programs to prove themselves in the colonizer's epistemological terms to receive the funding they need to exist. "Healing doesn't need to be structured that way" is the sentence from this volume that sticks.

### What it does NOT say

No chapter addresses the metrics producers' perspective -- the epidemiologists and health economists who build the models. No engagement with cases where metrics regimes genuinely improved health outcomes. The "alternatives" in the epilogue ("slow research," "scaling across") are gestured at but not demonstrated -- the book is much stronger on diagnosis than prescription. All cases are from the 2010--2015 period; the SDGs and digital health data explosion since 2016 have accelerated the trends but also introduced new dynamics. The volume does not reckon with the possibility that some forms of counting are better than no counting at all -- Adams acknowledges this in the epilogue but the case-study chapters uniformly present dysfunction.
