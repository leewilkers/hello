## The Move

Global connections are never smooth -- they gain traction through the grip of worldly encounter. Tsing takes "friction" as both metaphor and method: a wheel turns because of its encounter with the road surface; spinning in air it goes nowhere. The book is an ethnography of how universals (prosperity, knowledge, freedom) actually work in practice -- not as self-fulfilling abstractions but as "engaged universals" that travel across difference and are charged and changed by their travels. The site is the rainforests of South Kalimantan, Indonesia, in the 1980s and 1990s, but the argument reaches into Japanese trading companies and the stock market, Brazilian rubber tappers, UN environmental conferences, and the democratic movement that overthrew Suharto. Tsing refuses both the programmatic globalization story ("everything becomes integrated") and the pure-local-resistance story ("the margins absorb and transform"). Instead: heterogeneous, unequal encounters produce culture and power, and neither the global nor the local exists prior to the encounter.

## Section-by-Section Notes

### Preface

- **Argument**: Indonesia under Suharto (1966--1998) appeared bustling and quiet. When the country "fell apart" around 2000, the disasters built directly on the preceding thirty years of imagined peace and progress. The book is about the cultural processes by which predatory business practices and local empowerment struggles came to characterize the rainforests.
- **Key passage**: "I focus on zones of awkward engagement, where words mean something different across a divide even as people agree to speak."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Zones of awkward engagement** -- the transient sites where words mean different things across a divide but people agree to speak. These are where culture is produced in friction. Tsing's ethnographic method: "patchwork and haphazard."
  - **The forest is social** -- Meratus Dayaks showed Tsing that forest landscapes are terrains of personal biography and community history. House posts resprout into trees. Fruits and rattans are planted in growing forest. "Almost all scholarship and policy continues to portray forests as wild, natural spaces outside society."

### Introduction

- **Argument**: The theoretical spine. Universal claims (capitalism, science, liberal rights) do not make everything everywhere the same -- they gain "grip" through friction with particular situations. The book develops a "portfolio of methods" to study this productive friction.
- **Key passage**: "A wheel turns because of its encounter with the surface of the road; spinning in the air it goes nowhere. Rubbing two sticks together produces heat and light; one stick alone is just a stick."
- **Key passage**: "Friction is not just about slowing things down. Friction is required to keep global power in motion."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Engaged universals** -- "Universals are effective within particular historical conjunctures that give them content and force." They must travel across difference and be charged and changed by their travels. They never fulfill their promises of universality. "Even in transcending localities, they don't take over the world."
  - **Friction as method** -- not a synonym for resistance. "Hegemony is made as well as unmade with friction." Friction inflects historical trajectories, "enabling, excluding, and particularizing."
  - **The duality of universals** -- "Universalism is implicated in both imperial schemes to control the world and liberatory mobilizations for justice and empowerment." Protest extends the reach of the forms of power it protests, even as it gives voice to anger and hope. Balibar's "normalization" and "insurrection" are equally inspired by universals.

### Part I: Prosperity (Chapters 1--2)

#### Chapter 1: Frontiers of Capitalism

- **Argument**: The "resource frontier" in Kalimantan is not a place but an imaginative project that molds places and processes. Legal and illegal enterprise collaborate to undermine pre-existing property rights, transforming the countryside into a free-for-all. The frontier is made in the "shifting terrain between legality and illegality, public and private ownership, brutal rape and passionate charisma, ethnic collaboration and hostility, violence and law, restoration and extermination."
- **Key passage**: "Frontiers are not just edges; they are particular kinds of edges where the expansive nature of extraction comes into its own. Built from historical models of European conquest, frontiers create wildness so that some -- and not others -- may reap its rewards."
- **Key passage**: Uma Adang: "Better you had brought me a bomb, so I could blow this place up."
- **Concepts**:
  - **The salvage frontier** -- "where making, saving, and destroying resources are utterly mixed up." Plantations are introduced to "restore" deforested land; the land is then deforested to make way for the plantations. Bob Hasan hosts a 10K "Run for the Rainforest" while running the timber cartel.
  - **Proliferation** -- "always already out of control." Capitalist expansion at frontiers is not primitive accumulation (archaic) but savage -- an intensification that lurches forward in a hall of mirrors, "becoming showy parodies of themselves."
  - **Legal/illegal symbiosis** -- wild loggers fill out economies of scale for legal companies. Together they "overwhelm local residents, who generally have been unable to defend their lands and resources against this combination." Either alone could be challenged; together they create the frontier.
  - **Public/private confusion** -- "The bureaucracy was the market... The military was the market." The fluidity between public and private was the fertile space for the economy itself.

#### Chapter 2: (on scale-making and entrepreneurship)

- Extended analysis of how Japanese sogo shosha trading companies and Indonesian politicians created a new way of seeing the forest -- only dipterocarps, the rest as waste, including the human inhabitants. The forest simplification became a model for resource management and nation-building. APKINDO mimicked the sogo shosha, flooded world markets with cheap plywood.

### Part II: Knowledge (Chapters 3--5)

- **Argument**: Knowledge of nature is produced through collaboration, but collaboration is not shared understanding -- it is working together across difference. Competing environmental perspectives don't converge; they create productive frictions.
- **Key concepts**: Generalization as a collaborative achievement; the gap between globally circulating scientific knowledge and local environmental knowledge; the Meratus forest as a social landscape (fruit trees, rattan plantings, honey trees as markers of biography and community history, not "wild nature").

### Part III: Freedom (Chapters 6--7)

#### Chapter 6: The Indonesian Environmental Movement

- **Argument**: In the 1980s and 1990s under Suharto's repression, environmentalism was essentially the *only* pluralist social justice movement that flourished across Indonesia. It served as a vehicle for democratic opposition by using the language of science and modernity -- languages the authoritarian state itself endorsed. "To make global movement matter for the nation: this was the goal."
- **Key passage**: "Reading a roster of environmental groups in the late 1980s it was difficult to distinguish the radical critics from the president's cronies."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Using liberalism against itself** -- activists used the state's own liberal-universalist pretensions to challenge it. Lawsuits were "an arena in which victory meant raising national consciousness rather than winning in court."
  - **Translation as nation-making** -- the translation of "indigenous" into Indonesian (*masyarakat adat*, customary society) rather than *pribumi* (which excludes Chinese Indonesians). The term brought colonial legal heritage back into play, reminding the public that the state was violating its own rules.
  - **Ad hoc coalition politics** -- the Kedung Ombo dam campaign brought villagers, students, and transnational NGOs together around divergent goals (compensation, democracy, stopping ecological destruction). "The creativity of the movement also flourished through these differences."

#### Chapter 7: Collaborative Survival (Manggur)

- **Argument**: The environmental "success" at Manggur village depended on an alliance among village leaders, provincial nature lovers (Kompas Borneo), and national activists (SKEPHI/WALHI) -- who all told completely different stories of what happened. "I became fascinated by the success of an alliance based on rather complete incomprehensions of collaborators' points of view."
- **Key passage**: "Close collaborators have such different stories" -- the village head timed the victory before the environmentalist meeting; the Jakarta organizers barely remembered the details; Kompas Borneo saw the forest as wild nature to be recorded; the village leaders saw it as a social network of claimed trees and ancestral orchards.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Productive misunderstanding** -- collaborators don't need to agree to work together effectively. "These misunderstandings -- far from producing conflict -- had allowed them to work together!"
  - **The grassroots alliance as hierarchy reproduction** -- the environmental movement's three-tiered structure (village/province/capital) reproduced the New Order's administrative hierarchy even while contesting its policies. "A counter-discourse... the government's administrative hierarchy was reproduced in these reversals."
  - **Trees as social networks** -- for Meratus Dayaks, the forest is not managed *by* social networks; it simultaneously *is* the social network. Claimed trees pass to descendants. Community is constituted in overlapping histories of planting, foraging, and harvesting. The "community-managed forest" that environmentalists desired was produced through the alliance itself -- it didn't pre-exist the encounter.

### Coda

- **Argument**: "The future is clear and well-known. Only the past is worth studying, because only the past still has mystery." Hope is Gramscian -- optimism of the will in the face of likely destruction. "Tiny cracks might yet break open the dam; contingent openings are sites of unexpected force -- for better or worse."
- **Concepts**: Fragment over system. "Globalization is not delivered whole and round like a pizza... Global connections are made in fragments -- although some fragments are more powerful than others."

## Vocabulary

- **Friction** -- the grip of worldly encounter. Not resistance, not slowdown -- "Friction is required to keep global power in motion." It enables, excludes, and particularizes.
- **Engaged universals** -- universals that travel across difference and are charged by their travels. Effective within particular historical conjunctures, never fulfilling their own dreams of universality.
- **Zones of awkward engagement** -- transient sites where words mean something different across a divide even as people agree to speak. Where culture is produced.
- **The salvage frontier** -- where making, saving, and destroying resources are mixed up. Conservation, production, and sacrifice overlap.
- **Proliferation** -- capitalist expansion at the frontier, "always already out of control." Not linear progress but a hall-of-mirrors intensification.
- **Productive misunderstanding** -- collaborators succeeding precisely through incompatible comprehensions of what they are doing together.
- **Masyarakat adat** -- "customary society," the Indonesian translation of "indigenous people" that carries colonial legal heritage and became a tool for contesting state resource seizures.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Ferguson, *The Anti-Politics Machine*** -- Ferguson showed one development project depoliticizing in Lesotho. Tsing extends the frame: it's not just that development depoliticizes -- the entire chain of global connections (Japanese traders, Indonesian generals, international conservation funding) produces effects that none of the participants intended or controlled. Ferguson's diagnostic is a single case; Tsing provides the theory for why these cases proliferate.

2. **Adams, *Metrics*** -- Adams's volume is a catalogue of the frictions Tsing theorizes. Health metrics aspire to smooth universal comparability; every chapter in Adams shows the grip of local encounter resisting, subverting, or weaponizing that aspiration. Tsing provides the conceptual vocabulary; Adams provides the contemporary evidence.

3. **Fassin, *Humanitarian Reason*** -- Fassin's "politics of pity" and Tsing's "zones of awkward engagement" describe the same phenomenon from different angles. Fassin asks what affective structures govern who gets helped; Tsing asks what happens when different conceptions of help, justice, and nature encounter each other in practice.

4. **Krause, *The Good Project*** -- Krause shows NGO logic fragmenting reason. Tsing shows the same fragmentation operating through translation -- "indigenous" becomes *masyarakat adat*, and in the translation, new possibilities and new constraints emerge. The environmental movement's hierarchy (village/province/capital) reproduces the state's hierarchy even while opposing the state.

5. **Packard, *A History of Global Health*** -- Packard documents the recurring pattern: identify a magic bullet, deploy at scale, fail, repeat. Tsing's "salvage frontier" is the environmental version: destroy the forest, plant a plantation to "restore" it, then destroy that too. Same cycle, different domain.

6. **Illich, *Tools for Conviviality*** -- Illich argued that tools should serve human-scale agency. The sogo shosha trading model, adopted by Indonesia's APKINDO, is an anti-convivial tool par excellence -- it forces all actors into a single trading chain, eliminates alternatives, and concentrates power. Tsing shows what Illich's analysis looks like at the scale of a national economy.

7. **Scott, *Seeing Like a State*** -- Tsing engages Scott explicitly. The forest simplification -- seeing only dipterocarps where there was biodiversity -- is a legibility project. But Tsing goes further: the legibility doesn't just come from the state; it comes from the encounter between Japanese trading companies and Indonesian politicians. The "seeing" is collaborative.

## Limits

- The book is dense and intentionally nonlinear. The theoretical framework (universals, friction, engagement) is powerful but can feel repetitive across chapters.
- The Meratus Dayak material is ethnographically rich but the other sites (Japanese trading companies, international conservation) are sketched rather than fully ethnographed. Tsing acknowledges this -- her knowledge is "variously ethnographic, journalistic, and archival."
- The environmental movement is presented sympathetically, even romantically, in chapters 6 and 7. Tsing flags this ("I'm not sure these initiatives will work") but the critique of the movement's internal contradictions -- its class biases, its reproduction of state hierarchy, its dependence on foreign funding -- could go further.
- Written about the 1980s--1990s, published 2004. The post-Suharto period (decentralization, continued deforestation, palm oil expansion) has transformed the landscape in ways the book only hints at. The theoretical framework holds but the empirical ground has shifted.
- Nothing about health or health systems specifically -- the intervention-lane connection is structural (how global connections produce local dispossession) rather than sector-specific.
