## The Move

Sacasas reframes technology ethics from "how do we prevent bad actors?" to "what does this tool do to the person using it?" The title nods at Heidegger but the method is closer to Illich: 41 questions that treat every technology as morally active -- not neutral until misused. The questions were drafted in 2016 in response to Om Malik's suggestion that tech companies should "add an emotional and moral dimension to products." Sacasas' correction: the moral dimension is already there. The only question is what kind.

## Section-by-Section Notes

### The Framing (paragraphs 1-6)

- **Argument**: Opens with a recurring observation -- people in tech express "bewilderment" when their tools are used destructively. Sacasas identifies three possible explanations: naivete, hubris, recklessness. But the deeper problem isn't malevolent use; it's the failure to ask moral questions about *normal* use.
- **Key passage**: The Cathy O'Neil point -- "people find it hard to 'think like an *-hole' ... because most aren't." Sacasas appreciates the generosity but finds it insufficient. History should correct our limited perspectives. And worst-case scenarios aren't the only moral dimension worth examining.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Artifacts have ethics** -- Sacasas' extension of Winner's "Do artifacts have politics?" The answer is the same: yes. The moral character of a tool is not added by the user; it is embedded in the design.
  - **"Add an emotional and moral dimension"** -- Malik's phrase, which Sacasas corrects. You don't add a moral dimension to technology; you notice the one that's already there. The framing mistake: treating morality as an optional feature, not a structural property.

### The 41 Questions

- **Argument**: Not an analytical framework but a diagnostic instrument. The questions don't answer anything -- they surface what goes unexamined. Organized roughly from the personal (what kind of person does this make me?) to the structural (what systems does this depend on? are they just?).
- **Key passages** (grouped by register):

  **Formation questions** (1-2, 7-8): What sort of person will this make of me? What habits, what practices cultivated, what practices displaced? These treat the user as someone being formed by the tool, not merely operating it.

  **Perception questions** (3-4, 9-10): How will this affect my experience of time, place? What will it encourage me to notice, to ignore? These are McLuhan questions -- the medium reshaping the sensorium.

  **Cost-distribution questions** (11-13, 16): What was required of other human beings, other creatures, the earth? At whose expense am I empowered? These are Berry questions -- who pays for my convenience?

  **Desire questions** (22-23, 26): What desires does this generate, dissipate? How does it shape my vision of a good life? These go past behavior to formation -- the tool reshaping what you want, not just what you do.

  **Structural questions** (34, 37-38): Upon what systems does my use depend? Are they just? What risks for others, and have they consented? The jump from personal use to systemic complicity.

  **Accountability questions** (39-41): Can the consequences be undone? Does this make it easier to live as if I had no responsibilities? Can I be held responsible -- and would I feel better if I couldn't? The last question is the sharpest: it surfaces the desire to be irresponsible as a feature, not a bug.

- **Concepts**:
  - **Technology as moral formation** -- Questions 1-2 and 7-8 treat tool use as character formation, not task completion. The question isn't "does this tool work?" but "what kind of person does using it make me?"
  - **Distributed cost** -- Questions 11-13 require the user to trace the supply chain backward. Not abstract -- "what was required of other human beings so that I might be able to use this technology?" This is Berry's "direct dependence" in question form.
  - **The consent question** (#38) -- "What risks will my use of this technology entail for others? Have they consented?" Moves technology ethics from consumer choice to imposed risk. The person affected didn't agree to be affected.
  - **The desire to be unaccountable** (#41) -- "Can I be held responsible for the actions which this technology empowers? Would I feel better if I couldn't?" The final question names what automation and abstraction actually promise: moral distance.

### The Meta-Commentary

- **Argument**: Sacasas distances himself from the mainstream "tech ethics" movement while defending the need to think about technology's moral dimensions. His reservation: tech ethics advocacy often reduces to "use data responsibly" and "make terms of service transparent" -- policy fixes that don't ask the formation questions.
- **Key passage**: "I've generally come at the matter from a different place and have expressed my own reservations with the shape more recent tech ethics advocacy has taken."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Tech ethics vs. technology ethics** -- An implicit distinction. "Tech ethics" (the movement) concerns corporate behavior, data privacy, algorithmic fairness. Sacasas' "technology ethics" concerns what tools do to persons, relationships, and moral perception. The questions operate at the second level.

## Vocabulary

- **Artifacts have ethics** -- Sacasas' coinage, extending Winner. Not "artifacts can be used unethically" but "artifacts carry ethical properties in their design."
- **Technological fundamentalism** -- Not Sacasas' term (it's Berry's), but the essay is responding to the same phenomenon: the inability to tolerate the suggestion that a technology might be refused or questioned.
- **The questions** -- Sacasas refers to them as a set, a tool. They are meant to be printed out, posted, used in situ -- not read as an argument.
- **Moral dimension** -- What Malik wants to "add" and what Sacasas insists is already present. The language of addition vs. recognition is the essay's key distinction.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Winner's "Do Artifacts Have Politics?"** -- Sacasas explicitly builds on Winner. Winner asked whether technologies embed political choices; Sacasas extends the claim to ethics. The 41 questions are an applied toolkit for Winner's theoretical insight: if artifacts have politics, then every adoption is a political act, and here are the questions that surface the politics.

2. **Berry's nine standards** -- Berry's standards for technological innovation and Sacasas' 41 questions are the same project in different registers. Berry is declarative (the tool *should* be cheaper, repairable, non-displacing); Sacasas is interrogative (what does the tool cost, who repairs it, what does it displace?). Berry's Standard 9 ("should not replace or disrupt anything good that already exists") maps directly to Sacasas' questions 7-8 (what practices will this cultivate, displace?).

3. **Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction** -- Sacasas' formation questions (1-2, 7-8) are asking whether a technology is holistic or prescriptive in Franklin's sense. A holistic technology lets the user control the process and develop skill; a prescriptive technology controls the user and displaces skill. "What sort of person will the use of this technology make of me?" is Franklin's question stated personally.

4. **Illich's convivial tools** -- The newsletter is named *The Convivial Society*. Sacasas' project is Illich updated for networked technologies. Illich's test: does the tool serve human-scale agency or institutional control? Sacasas' 41 questions are 41 ways of operationalizing that test.

5. **McLuhan's "the medium is the message"** -- Questions 3-4 and 9-10 (time, place, notice, ignore) are McLuhan's insight turned into a diagnostic. The medium reshapes perception before you choose what to do with it.

## Limits

- The questions are all addressed to the individual user. No questions about collective refusal, regulatory intervention, or institutional design. This is Sacasas' Illichian inheritance: the locus of moral action is the person, not the institution. But questions 34 and 37-38 gesture at systems -- they could be extended toward structural analysis and aren't.
- No prioritization. All 41 questions carry equal weight. In practice, some matter more for some technologies than others. A list this long risks becoming either overwhelming or a pick-what-suits-you buffet, which is the opposite of moral rigor.
- The essay reposted questions from 2016 with minimal revision. Six years of "tech ethics" discourse -- fairness, accountability, transparency, surveillance capitalism -- doesn't visibly alter the list. Either the questions were ahead of their time or they're too general to be affected by specifics.
