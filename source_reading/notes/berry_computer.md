## The Move

Berry refuses a specific technological upgrade -- pencil and typewriter to computer -- and uses the refusal to surface a general principle: that consumption is a moral act, and that every tool adoption displaces something (or someone) that already works. The essay's real argument arrives in the reply to his critics, where the personal refusal becomes a critique of "technological fundamentalism" and the conservation movement's blind spot about consumption.

## Section-by-Section Notes

### The Original Essay

- **Argument**: Opens with a working arrangement -- pencil, Royal typewriter (1956), wife as typist and editor -- and defends it against the assumption that it needs upgrading. Each reason for refusal names a different cost: energy dependence, corporate complicity, the displacement of a person by a machine, and the unproven claim that computers produce better writing.
- **Key passage**: "what would be superseded would be not only something, but somebody. In order to be technologically up-to-date as a writer, I would have to sacrifice an association that I am dependent upon and that I treasure."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Direct dependence on strip-mined coal** -- Berry's measure of complicity is not abstract "carbon footprint" but a named extraction: strip-mined coal in Appalachia. The chain from wall outlet to mountaintop removal is the moral unit.
  - **The "old model" as person** -- Innovation discourse treats what it replaces as an object ("the old model"). Berry names what actually gets replaced: a relationship, a collaborator, a judgment. "Not only something, but somebody."
  - **Literary cottage industry** -- Not nostalgia. A working system that produces good output, employs two people, and runs on daylight. The question isn't whether it's quaint but whether it works.

### Standards for Technological Innovation (the nine criteria)

- **Argument**: A concrete decision framework for tool adoption. Not anti-technology -- anti-default-adoption. Each criterion names a specific cost that "upgrade" rhetoric hides.
- **Key passage**: The nine standards themselves (numbered 1-9 in the text).
- **Concepts**:
  - **Cost inversion** -- Standards 1, 4, 5, 7, 8 all point the same direction: the new tool should demand *less* from the world, not more. Innovation's default promise is "more capability"; Berry's test is "less extraction."
  - **Repairability near home** -- Standards 6, 7, 8 concern locality and repair. The tool should be fixable "by a person of ordinary intelligence" and purchasable "as near to home as possible." This is Franklin's holistic technology in a checklist.
  - **"Should not replace or disrupt anything good that already exists, including family and community relationships"** (Standard 9) -- The only standard that names social relations as things a tool can damage. This is where the essay becomes more than Luddism.

### Reader Letters (Harper's responses)

- **Argument**: Five letters, four hostile, all missing the point in instructive ways. They demonstrate exactly what Berry will diagnose in his reply: technological fundamentalism that cannot tolerate dissent.
- **Key passage**: Inkeles' satirical reduction of Berry's wife to "Wife -- a low-tech energy-saving device" -- meant as feminist critique but actually proving Berry's point about treating persons as mechanisms.
- **Concepts**:
  - **The clear conscience problem** -- Rhoads and Borenstein both claim clear consciences about their computer use. They separate production (bad, someone else's fault) from consumption (neutral, personal choice). Berry will demolish this in the reply.
  - **Feminist reading as deflection** -- Inkeles and Koosman attack Berry for using his wife's labor. The move: reframe a critique of technology as a gender issue, thereby avoiding the technology question entirely. Berry notes they "stereotype and insult, on the basis of one fact, a woman they do not know."
  - **The Sierra Club reductio** -- Borenstein's argument that Berry's logic would require the Sierra Club to hand-copy its mailings. A slippery-slope move that equates "limit where you can" with "eliminate all technology." Berry never said that; his essay opens "like almost everybody else, I am hooked to the energy corporations."

### Berry's Reply

- **Argument**: The strongest section. Berry identifies "technological fundamentalism" -- a belief system that "wishes to monopolize a whole society and, therefore, cannot tolerate the smallest difference of opinion." The angry responses to a mild personal essay prove the diagnosis. He then turns to the conservation movement's structural failure: it opposes production harms while protecting consumption habits.
- **Key passage**: "consumers become salesmen, and the world is made safer for corporations."
- **Key passage**: "To the conservation movement, it is only production that causes environmental degradation; the consumption that supports the production is rarely acknowledged to be at fault."
- **Key passage**: "If the use of a computer is a new idea, then a newer idea is not to use one."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Technological fundamentalism** -- "like other fundamentalisms, wishes to monopolize a whole society and, therefore, cannot tolerate the smallest difference of opinion." Not a claim that technology is bad but that the *compulsion* to adopt is a fundamentalism -- a closed belief system that produces defensiveness when questioned.
  - **Consumers become salesmen** -- The mechanism by which market logic colonizes personal identity. Satisfied consumers defend their purchases with the arguments of the manufacturers. "They repeat, like a chorus of toads, the notes sounded by their leaders in industry."
  - **The conservation movement's production/consumption split** -- Environmentalists oppose strip mining but plug in their computers with a clear conscience. Berry: "If Rhoads can do so, that does not mean that his conscience is clear; it means that his conscience is not working." The structural critique: conservation that only targets production while protecting consumption is self-defeating.

## Vocabulary

- **Technological fundamentalism** -- Berry's coinage. A belief system that requires universal adoption and reacts to dissent with "the intensity of the feelings" usually reserved for religious challenges.
- **Cottage industry** -- Used literally, not metaphorically. Berry and his wife's writing operation.
- **Direct dependence** -- Berry's measure of complicity. Not "carbon footprint" (abstract, aggregate) but the specific chain: my wall outlet -> this power plant -> that strip mine.
- **The old model** -- Innovation's euphemism for what gets discarded. Berry's point: it might be a person.
- **Clear conscience** -- Used sarcastically. The self-deception that lets consumers oppose extraction while participating in it.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Franklin's holistic vs. prescriptive technologies** -- Berry's nine standards are a practitioner's version of Franklin's distinction. A holistic technology keeps the worker in control of the process; Berry's standards (repairable, small-scale, locally sourced, non-displacing) describe what that looks like at the tool level. Standard 9 ("should not replace or disrupt anything good that already exists") is Franklin's holistic criterion stated as a personal test.

2. **Illich's convivial tools** -- Berry's refusal is a specific instance of Illich's general argument that tools should serve human-scale agency. Berry's nine standards could be an appendix to *Tools for Conviviality*. The convergence: both locate the problem not in technology per se but in the compulsion to adopt, the loss of choice, the displacement of competence.

3. **Krause's "all NGOs are media companies" / the conservation movement's structural failure** -- Berry's critique of environmentalists who "impose restraints upon production without limiting consumption" maps onto Krause's analysis of how organizational logic fragments reason. The conservation movement, like Krause's NGOs, has an operational logic (fundraise, lobby, alert) that prevents it from questioning its own consumption. The "electronic bulletin board" Borenstein is so proud of is Krause's "good project" -- activity that feels like progress but doesn't touch the structure.

4. **Russell & Vinsel's maintenance vs. innovation** -- Berry's essay is a maintenance argument avant la lettre. His working system (pencil, typewriter, wife-as-editor) doesn't need upgrading because it works. The demand to upgrade is the innovation ideology that Russell & Vinsel will diagnose thirty years later: the assumption that the new is better, the old is obsolete, and anyone who resists is "on the losing side, a side already relegated to the dustbin of history."

## Limits

- Berry does not address the question of *access*. His refusal depends on a stable domestic arrangement, rural self-sufficiency, and an established writing career. The pencil-and-typewriter model is not available to a single mother in an apartment or a writer without a collaborator. He would probably agree -- the essay is about *his* practice, not a prescription -- but the letters expose a real vulnerability he doesn't fully answer.
- The essay does not distinguish between types of computing. A word processor and a networked surveillance machine are treated as the same object. Berry's 1987 computer is a standalone word processor; his critique (corporate complicity, energy dependence, displacement) applies much more forcefully to the 2026 internet than to what his correspondents were actually proposing.
- Nothing here about collective action, institutional reform, or structural change beyond personal refusal. Berry knows this: "I do not think that the government and the conservation organizations alone will ever make us a conserving society." But the alternative he offers is individual conscience, which has obvious scale limits he doesn't reckon with.
