## The Move

Polanyi starts from "we can know more than we can tell" and works out a four-part structure of tacit knowing (functional, phenomenal, semantic, ontological) that applies from face recognition to scientific discovery. He then extends this structure outward: into biology (the hierarchy of levels in living things), into evolution (emergence as the creation of new levels not derivable from lower ones), and into the social organization of science (a "society of explorers" whose coherence depends on tacit transmission). The book's deepest claim is that formalization is parasitic on tacit knowing, never the reverse -- and therefore any program to make all knowledge explicit is self-defeating.

## Chapter Notes

### Ch 1: Tacit Knowing

**Argument**: We attend *from* subsidiary particulars *to* a focal whole -- this is the from-to structure of all knowing. A blind man's stick, a diagnostic skill, a recognized face: in each case we rely on clues we cannot specify in order to attend to what they jointly mean. This structure has four aspects (functional, phenomenal, semantic, ontological) and it applies to perception, skill, language, tool use, and scientific discovery alike. The chapter ends by solving Plato's Meno paradox: we can search for what we don't yet know because tacit knowing lets us have "an intimation of the coherence of hitherto not comprehended particulars" (p. 21).

**Key concepts**:
- **Tacit knowing**: "we can know more than we can tell" (p. 4). Not a special or marginal kind of knowledge -- the structure underlying all knowledge.
- **From-to structure**: "we attend from something for attending to something else; namely, from the first term to the second term of the tacit relation" (p. 10). The proximal (near, subsidiary) term is attended from; the distal (far, focal) term is attended to.
- **Proximal and distal terms**: "Using the language of anatomy, we may call the first term proximal, and the second term distal" (p. 10). Proximal = the clues, particulars, subsidiaries. Distal = the focal meaning they jointly constitute.
- **Four aspects of tacit knowing**: Functional (we rely on the first term to attend to the second), phenomenal (we are aware of the proximal in the appearance of the distal), semantic (meaning is displaced away from us), ontological (tacit knowing is the understanding of a comprehensive entity).
- **Indwelling**: "it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning" (p. 18). When we make something function as a proximal term, we incorporate it into our body -- we dwell in it.
- **Interiorization**: "To rely on a theory for understanding nature is to interiorize it" (p. 17). The theory becomes a tool we attend from, not a proposition we attend to.
- **Subception**: The experimental demonstration of tacit knowing -- subjects learn to anticipate electric shocks associated with particular syllables without being able to identify which syllables are the shock syllables (Lazarus and McCleary, 1949).

**Key passages**:
1. "we can know more than we can tell" (p. 4) [anchor -- the sentence that launches everything]
2. "we know the first term only by relying on our awareness of it for attending to the second" (p. 10) [structural -- the from-to relation defined]
3. "it is not by looking at things, but by dwelling in them, that we understand their joint meaning" (p. 18) [epistemological -- indwelling as the mode of understanding]
4. "Repeat a word several times, attending carefully to the motion of your tongue and lips, and to the sound you make, and soon the word will sound hollow and eventually lose its meaning" (p. 18) [diagnostic -- how focal attention to subsidiaries destroys meaning]
5. "the process of formalizing all knowledge to the exclusion of any tacit knowing is self-defeating" (p. 20) [polemical -- the central argument against positivism]
6. "to see a problem is to see something that is hidden. It is to have an intimation of the coherence of hitherto not comprehended particulars" (pp. 21--22) [the Meno solution]
7. "the kind of lucidity which destroys its subject matter" (p. 18/25) [compression -- Polanyi's phrase for what happens when you try to make the tacit explicit]

**Vocabulary**: tacit knowing, from-to structure, proximal/distal, subsidiary awareness, focal awareness, indwelling, interiorization, subception, comprehensive entity, ostensive definition

**Connects to**:
- Sennett, *The Craftsman* -- "the hand teaches the head" is an elaboration of Polanyi's from-to structure applied to craft. Sennett cites Polanyi extensively.
- Ingold, *Making* -- making as a way of knowing is Polanyi's indwelling applied to materials.
- Korn, *Why We Make Things* -- "in the workshop, wishing won't make it so" is practical Polanyi: tacit knowledge that resists articulation.
- Flyvbjerg, *Making Social Science Matter* -- phronesis (practical wisdom) is the methodological application of Polanyi's argument that not all knowledge can be formalized.
- Rao, "Legibility" -- the "eye on the ground" that comprehends the forest by walking in it is Polanyi's indwelling. The failure mode Rao describes -- projecting incomprehension onto the object -- is Polanyi's destructive lucidity.

### Ch 2: Emergence

**Argument**: The from-to structure of tacit knowing maps onto the structure of reality itself. Comprehensive entities (a skill, a game of chess, a speech, a living organism) consist of two levels: a lower one governed by its own laws, and a higher one that controls the boundary conditions left open by the lower level. The operations of the higher level cannot be derived from the laws of the lower one. This is the "principle of marginal control." Applied to biology: living functions rely on physics and chemistry but are not reducible to them. The chapter culminates in the claim that evolution itself is a series of emergences -- each producing a level whose organizing principles cannot be accounted for by the level beneath it.

**Key concepts**:
- **Dual control / marginal control**: "each level is subject to dual control; first, by the laws that apply to its elements in themselves and, second, by the laws that control the comprehensive entity formed by them" (p. 36). The higher level governs the boundary conditions left undetermined by the lower level.
- **The hierarchy of speech**: Five levels -- voice production, words, sentences, style, literary composition -- each governed by its own rules (phonetics, lexicography, grammar, stylistics, literary criticism) and each leaving boundary conditions open for the next level above (p. 36).
- **Operational principles of machines**: "Engineering and physics are two different sciences" (p. 40). A complete physical description of a machine would not tell you whether it is a machine, how it works, or what it's for. Operational principles govern boundary conditions left open by physics.
- **Organismic processes**: Non-machinelike processes in living beings, exemplified by Driesch's sea urchin embryo -- any cell or combination of cells can regenerate the whole organism (pp. 42--43). Distinct from mechanical processes but combined with them in development.
- **Emergence**: "no level can gain control over its own boundary conditions and hence cannot bring into existence a higher level" (p. 45). Each higher level requires a principle not manifest in the lower level.
- **Reality as depth**: "minds and problems possess a deeper reality than cobblestones" (p. 33) -- things that can yet reveal themselves in unexpected ways are more real than things that cannot.

**Key passages**:
1. "the operations of a higher level cannot be accounted for by the laws governing its particulars forming the lower level" (p. 36) [structural -- the irreducibility thesis]
2. "You cannot derive a vocabulary from phonetics; you cannot derive the grammar of a language from its vocabulary; a correct use of grammar does not account for good style; and a good style does not provide the content of a piece of prose" (p. 36) [demonstration -- the speech hierarchy]
3. "Engineering and physics are two different sciences" (p. 40) [compression -- the machine argument in one line]
4. "The most striking feature of our own existence is our sentience. The laws of physics and chemistry include no conception of sentience, and any system wholly determined by these laws must be insentient" (p. 38) [ontological -- the hard limit of reductionism]
5. "minds and problems possess a deeper reality than cobblestones, although cobblestones are admittedly more real in the sense of being tangible" (p. 33) [ontological -- his reclassification of what counts as real]

**Vocabulary**: dual control, marginal control, boundary conditions, comprehensive entity, operational principles, organismic, equipotential, morphogenetic regulation, ideogenesis (vs. phylogenesis)

**Connects to**:
- Franklin, *Real World of Technology* -- Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction is a practical expression of Polanyi's dual control. Holistic technologies operate at the level of the whole; prescriptive technologies fix the boundary conditions and eliminate the higher-level agency of the worker.
- Alexander, *The Timeless Way of Building* -- Alexander's "quality without a name" is an attempt to point at what Polanyi calls the comprehensive entity whose meaning cannot be captured by specifying its parts.
- Beer, *Designing Freedom* -- Beer's cybernetics operates within the same conceptual space: systems whose behavior is not reducible to the behavior of their components. Beer comes at it from operations research; Polanyi from epistemology.

### Ch 3: A Society of Explorers

**Argument**: Three-part structure. First, Polanyi diagnoses the modern crisis: scientific skepticism fused with moral perfectionism has produced two destructive hybrids -- existentialist anti-moralism (personal) and Marxist fanaticism (political). Second, he shows that science itself depends on authority, tradition, and tacit transmission -- not on the explicit verification positivism claims. The "principle of mutual control" (overlapping neighborhoods of competent judges) maintains standards across a domain no single person can survey. Third, he argues that responsible judgment in science is the model for responsible judgment everywhere: the scientist is neither self-determining (existentialism) nor determined (Marxism) but "committed to the anticipation of a hidden reality." The chapter ends with a sketch of evolution as a field of potentialities that evokes action -- "the image of humanity immersed in potential thought."

**Key concepts**:
- **The destructive fusion**: "moral doubt is frenzied by moral fury and moral fury is armed by scientific nihilism" (p. 60). Scientific skepticism + moral perfectionism = either existentialist despair or Marxist totalitarianism.
- **Mutual control**: "scientists keep watch over each other... their personal fields will form chains of overlapping neighborhoods extending over the entire range of science" (p. 72). Standards are maintained not by central authority but by adjacent competence.
- **Universal intent**: "To claim validity for a statement merely declares that it ought to be accepted by all" (p. 78). Not established universality but the claim that one's discovery *should* be recognized as true by anyone properly equipped.
- **Freedom as service**: "Originality is commanded at every stage by a sense of responsibility for advancing the growth of truth in men's minds. Its freedom is perfect service" (p. 77). The scientist's freedom is not self-determination but obedience to a hidden reality.
- **Society of explorers**: "man is in thought. Man the explorer is placed in the midst of potential discoveries" (p. 83). A society whose coherence comes from each member's independent pursuit of truth within overlapping domains.
- **The master proves himself by his restraint**: (Goethe, cited p. 80). A scientist should choose problems commensurate with his capacities -- not too small, not too large.
- **Tradition as prerequisite for originality**: "Such a tradition assures the independence of its followers by transmitting the conviction that thought has intrinsic powers" (p. 82). You cannot be original without first submitting to what exists.

**Key passages**:
1. "moral doubt is frenzied by moral fury and moral fury is armed by scientific nihilism" (p. 60) [diagnostic -- the modern crisis in one sentence]
2. "the kind of lucidity which destroys its subject matter" (p. 25, echoed p. 61) [anchor -- the recurring compression]
3. "Originality is commanded at every stage by a sense of responsibility for advancing the growth of truth in men's minds. Its freedom is perfect service" (p. 77) [moral -- his alternative to both existentialism and positivism]
4. "scientists keep watch over each other... their personal fields will form chains of overlapping neighborhoods extending over the entire range of science" (p. 72) [structural -- mutual control defined]
5. "It is the image of humanity immersed in potential thought that I find revealing for the problems of our day" (p. 91) [cosmic -- the final vision]
6. "Men need a purpose which bears on eternity. Truth does that; our ideals do it" (p. 92) [existential -- the closing claim]

**Vocabulary**: society of explorers, mutual control, overlapping neighborhoods, universal intent, active inertia (of tradition), self-co-ordination, plausibility (as gatekeeper), scientific value (exactitude + systematic importance + intrinsic interest of subject matter)

**Connects to**:
- Mills, *The Sociological Imagination* -- Mills' attack on "abstracted empiricism" and "grand theory" is the sociological version of Polanyi's attack on positivism. Both argue that formalized method cannot replace judgment.
- Flyvbjerg, *Making Social Science Matter* -- Flyvbjerg's phronesis is Polanyi's tacit knowing applied to social science. The argument that practical wisdom can't be reduced to rules is direct Polanyi.
- Krause, *The Good Project* -- Krause's analysis of NGO logic (the organization's need to produce legible outputs) is Polanyi's destructive lucidity applied to humanitarian work.
- Sull, "Why Good Companies Go Bad" -- Sull's "active inertia" is the organizational failure of tradition *without* the tacit renewal Polanyi describes. The organization keeps its commitments but loses the capacity to revise them.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Sennett, *The Craftsman***: Sennett's central argument -- that the hand teaches the head through repetitive practice, and that this has political implications (good craftwork requires autonomy) -- is Polanyi with a sociological extension. Sennett cites Polanyi extensively. Polanyi provides the epistemology; Sennett provides the politics.

2. **Franklin, *The Real World of Technology***: Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction is Polanyi's dual control applied to labor. In a holistic technology, the worker operates at the higher level (controlling boundary conditions); in a prescriptive technology, the process is fixed from above and the worker is reduced to the lower level. Franklin doesn't cite Polanyi, but the structural parallel is exact.

3. **Flyvbjerg, *Making Social Science Matter***: Flyvbjerg's phronesis (practical wisdom that cannot be reduced to episteme) is the methodological application of Polanyi's tacit knowing. Both argue that formalization cannot replace judgment, and both locate the failure of social science in the attempt to do so. The lineage connection is through Aristotle, but the modern articulation is Polanyi's.

4. **Mills, *The Sociological Imagination* (appendix: "On Intellectual Craftsmanship")**: Mills' insistence that the practice of social science is a craft -- that it involves the whole person, that it cannot be reduced to method -- echoes Polanyi. Mills' appendix is the practical manual for what Polanyi theorizes. Different vocabularies, same conviction: knowing is an activity of the whole person, not the application of a procedure.

5. **Rao, "A Big Little Idea Called Legibility"**: Rao's failure mode -- the state/institution that can't read a complex reality, blames the reality, and destroys it with simplification -- is what happens when Polanyi's "destructive lucidity" is wielded by power. The "eye on the ground" vs. "eye in the sky" is indwelling vs. detached formalization.

6. **Ingold, *Making***: Ingold's argument that making is a way of knowing -- not applying preconceived form to passive matter -- is Polanyi's indwelling applied to the practice of materials. Ingold explicitly engages Polanyi through Sennett.

7. **Alexander, *The Timeless Way of Building***: Alexander's "quality without a name" is an attempt to point at what Polanyi calls tacit knowledge in architecture. Both are trying to articulate something they agree cannot be fully articulated. Alexander's pattern language is an effort at what Polanyi calls explicit integration -- useful but never a replacement for the tacit.

## Limits

- The book's third chapter pivots from epistemology into political philosophy and theology with a speed that leaves the argument underdeveloped. The diagnosis of "skepticism fused with moral perfectionism" is powerful but historically specific to Cold War liberalism -- it doesn't obviously map onto post-2000 conditions.
- Polanyi's evolutionary claims (emergence as creative agency, "a field of potentialities which evoke action") are speculative and not well supported by the biology he invokes. The analogy between tacit knowing and biological emergence is suggestive but not argued -- it is asserted on grounds of structural similarity.
- The book says almost nothing about power. Who gets to dwell? Who is denied the apprenticeship that transmits tacit knowledge? The "society of explorers" is an idealized scientific community -- it has no account of exclusion, gatekeeping by class or race, or the politics of whose tacit knowledge counts.
- The argument for authority and tradition as prerequisites for originality is real but needs the counterweight of asking whose traditions and which authorities. Polanyi's "mutual control" works as a description of how scientific communities regulate themselves, but it doesn't address what happens when those communities systematically exclude certain voices.
- The relationship between the book and *Personal Knowledge* (1958) is underdeveloped for anyone reading *The Tacit Dimension* alone -- Polanyi frequently refers back to positions he developed there without restating them.
