## The Move

An edited collection that treats global health metrics not as neutral measurement tools but as political technologies -- instruments that reshape what counts as health, who gets helped, and who profits. Adams's framing argument (chapters 1 and the introduction) carries the volume: the shift from international health to global health is inseparable from the rise of DALYs, RCTs, and cost-effectiveness rubrics, and these metrics do not merely *measure* health outcomes -- they become the passage points through which sovereignty, finance, and clinical practice are reorganized. The individual chapters are ethnographic case studies showing what this reorganization looks like on the ground: maternal mortality statistics fabricated for political survival in Malawi and Nigeria, a data retention strike in Senegal, Yup'ik behavioral health programs forced into evidence frameworks that cannot capture what they do, nonprofits warped by the demand for "widgets," and the entanglement of RCT infrastructure with for-profit investment.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Notes

### Introduction (Adams)

- **Argument**: Maps the colonial-to-postcolonial-to-global transition in health metrics. The key claim: global health is not just "new bottles for old wine" -- the metrics themselves constitute a new regime of sovereignty. The demand for counting creates forms of knowledge that claim political neutrality while being deeply political.
- **Key passage**: "Other kinds of evidence may be more reliable than numbers. In this sense, quantification strategies and the metrics we rely on to avoid politics often do not avoid politics at all; they become a form of politics in their own right."
- **Concepts**: Metrics as storytelling -- numbers tell stories about what their producers care about most, coded into which variables matter and which are excluded.

### Chapter 1: Metrics of the Global Sovereign (Adams)

- **Argument**: The longest and most theoretically dense chapter. Traces the lineage from colonial universalism through DALYs to RCTs to the "win-win" scenario where global health becomes a for-profit investment opportunity. Adams's central move: the DALY "fiscalizes" life -- turns it into an entity that behaves like a monetary instrument. The RCT then becomes "the new Lord Chancellor," the metric that rules all others, determining not just what works but what gets funded and studied at all.
- **Key passage**: "There is a very fine line between using RCTs to do the sort of health work we want to do and doing only the sort of work that RCTs can be designed to do."
- **Key passage**: "These days, lacking data may be even more tragic than lacking health." (on the World Bank's concept of "data deprivation")
- **Concepts**:
  - **Fiscalization of life** -- the DALY abstracts quality of life into a fiscally meaningful form, turning life into an object that behaves like a monetary instrument. The move from QALY (Global North, counting cost of keeping people alive) to DALY (Global South, justifying expenditures in relation to death and disease burdens) is not neutral -- it produces the Global Burden of Disease index, which makes countries that look nothing alike metrically comparable.
  - **The new Lord Chancellor** -- Adams's recurring metaphor. Bill Gates compares health metrics to the micrometer that enabled the steam engine. Adams asks: what else does this Lord Chancellor do? The RCT creates silos of exclusion around what counts as evidence. "How did we ever know anything for sure before the RCT?"
  - **Database determinism** -- "If you've got it, use it." Metrics compel a kind of utility that might not otherwise be seen.
  - **Storied metrics** -- the numbers need stories to back them up. The suffering stranger narrative does the legitimacy work that raw numbers cannot. Stories carry "a different kind of credibility because they make people feel something quite different from what they feel when they see compelling numbers."
  - **The global sovereign** -- "a flexible assemblage of data production, number crunching, and scale-up profit sourcing" that orchestrates health interventions within capitalism's terms.

### Chapter 2: Estimating Death (Wendland)

- **Argument**: A forensic close reading of how maternal mortality statistics are produced in Malawi. The WHO regression equation for estimating maternal mortality uses three covariates -- GDP, general fertility rate, and "skilled attendance at birth" -- and every number in the equation is itself an estimate. For 27 countries with no data at all, the model is a tautology: Equatorial Guinea shows declining maternal mortality because its GDP rose with oil prices, not because fewer women died.
- **Key passage**: "All existing estimates of levels and trends in maternal mortality provide no more than an image of a reality that may have been."
- **Key passage**: A colleague at Queens Hospital in Malawi: "They are saying we will meet the Millennium Development Goal. But I can't believe it. If it's that low, why are we still seeing this kind of thing every day?"
- **Concepts**:
  - **Wobbly numbers** -- Wendland's term for estimates built on estimates built on intuitions. "Three of the four numbers used to calculate paradoxically precise-looking uncertainty intervals were based mostly on our own intuition, as appropriate empirical evidence is lacking."
  - **The model as tautology** -- When no real data exist, the model's own assumptions produce the "results." GDP goes up, so maternal mortality goes down -- by construction, not observation.
  - **Numbers as fetishes** -- President Banda claimed declining maternal mortality based on survey data collected before she took office, comparing incompatible numbers (a survey estimate from 2006 with a model extrapolation for 2010). "The number-as-fetish is particularly evident."

### Chapter 3: The Obligation to Count (Oni-Orisan)

- **Argument**: In a southwestern Nigerian state, the governor builds his political career around a maternal health program (HMHB) funded by the World Bank and Gates Foundation. Deaths that would mar the statistics are erased -- Blessing dies in transit and is not counted because "she would have been an unnecessary mortality." Polio vaccination numbers become instruments for winning Gates Foundation competitions and reelection campaigns. Young doctors recognize the distortion but are powerless against it.
- **Key passage**: "The hospital is politically motivated."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Numbers as political instruments** -- not neutral data points but tools for governance, career advancement, and aid flows. "Political careers are made and destroyed by way of health statistics."
  - **Performing good data** -- the compulsion to produce numbers showing improvement constrains clinical care. Record-keeping protocols outweigh triage; a woman with exposed bowels waits while paperwork is completed.

### Chapter 4: The Power of Data (Tichenor)

- **Argument**: Senegalese health workers withheld routine patient data from the Ministry of Health for nearly three years (2010--2013) while continuing to provide care. The strike exposed the entire architecture of performance-based funding: without locally produced data, the Global Fund could not assess Senegal's malaria program and nearly cut funding. Data production is citizenship performance -- and withholding it is a novel form of political resistance.
- **Key passage**: The ICP (head nurse) at Sinthiou Mamadou is the only permanent staff at a rural health post: nurse, midwife, pharmacist, data collector. "It is simply too much for one person."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Data for funding** -- the implicit reciprocal trade relationship. Countries produce data; donors produce funding. Without the data, the entire architecture of global health governance collapses.
  - **The normal emergency** -- rural health posts where the system's demands for data collection overwhelm the capacity to provide care.

### Chapter 5: Native Sovereignty by the Numbers (Hales)

- **Argument**: Yup'ik behavioral health programs in Alaska are forced to adopt evidence-based metrics frameworks that cannot capture what healing means for them. "Healing doesn't need to be structured that way." The grant that funds Healing Our Families now requires a five-step data-driven procedure, logic models, and "fidelity checklists." Program directors must spend an entire year on data collection before implementing a curriculum they developed over six years.
- **Key passage**: "People that want to come back, or would recommend it to their friends and family, or who want to bring it to their community. And just through those three examples, I would see it as a success."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Metrics and sovereignty** -- recognition from the state depends on producing the right kinds of quantifiable evidence. Without metrics, Healing Our Families cannot achieve legitimacy, and without legitimacy, Yup'ik claims to manage their own child welfare are dismissed.
  - **Fidelity to the model vs. fidelity to healing** -- the granting agency's "pretty bright line" against implementing anything before the data-driven process is complete, even when local leaders know what works.

### Chapters 6--9 (Erikson, Walkover, Smith-Morris, Minn)

- **Erikson (ch. 6)**: Maps the financial architecture -- Global Health Investment Fund, S&P Dow Jones co-branded indexes, social impact bonds. Profit can be made "entirely on the promise of scaling up," whether or not health improves.
- **Walkover (ch. 7)**: Hesperian Health Guides (*Where There Is No Doctor*) has arguably made one of the largest impacts in global health through grassroots, bottom-up work that doesn't lend itself to statistical counting. The demand for "widgets" -- countable deliverables -- undermines the practices that made these organizations successful.
- **Smith-Morris (ch. 8)**: RCTs in the VA system fail to capture what actually makes programs work for veterans with spinal cord injuries. "Fidelity" to the research protocol is treated as evidence of program success even when the protocol misses what matters.
- **Minn (ch. 9)**: Konbit Sante in Haiti explicitly eschews clinical interventions and "parallel health systems," emphasizing relational work. The demand for "widgets" -- quantified deliverables -- forces the organization to translate its relational work into the language of metrics, a "double-edged sword."

### Epilogue (Adams)

- **Argument**: Not a call to abandon metrics but a call to recognize their limitations and explore alternatives. "Slow research" -- keeping efforts local, pluralizing strategies, "scaling across rather than scaling up." The alternative to the tyranny of numbers is not innumeracy but epistemological pluralism.

## Vocabulary

- **Fiscalization of life** -- the DALY's conversion of quality of life into a monetary-instrument-like entity, enabling market logics to govern health resource allocation.
- **The new Lord Chancellor** -- the RCT as the universal standard by which all other forms of evidence are judged and found wanting.
- **Wobbly numbers** -- (Wendland) estimates of estimates, with uncertainty ranges "based mostly on our own intuition."
- **Database determinism** -- (Brenneis, via Adams) "if you've got it, use it" -- data sets compel their own utilization regardless of fitness.
- **Storied metrics** -- the affective narratives (suffering stranger stories) that legitimize and give credibility to numbers that, left alone, lack persuasive force.
- **Performing good data** -- (Erikson/Oni-Orisan) producing numbers that show improvement regardless of underlying reality, because the political and financial consequences of bad data are worse than the consequences of bad health.
- **The global sovereign** -- Adams's term for the assemblage of data production, number crunching, and profit sourcing that governs through metrics while claiming political neutrality.
- **Widgets** -- (Minn) quantified deliverables demanded by funders. Organizations whose work is relational and qualitative must translate into widget-language or lose funding.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Ferguson, *The Anti-Politics Machine*** -- Ferguson showed how a development project in Lesotho depoliticized by extending state power through the apparatus of "development." Adams extends the diagnosis: metrics are the contemporary mechanism of depoliticization. By claiming political neutrality, numbers do political work invisibly. Oni-Orisan's Nigerian case is Ferguson's anti-politics machine running through health statistics instead of livestock surveys.

2. **Packard, *A History of Global Health*** -- Packard documents the recurring "magic bullet" cycle. Adams shows the latest iteration: RCTs as the magic bullet for knowing what works, with the same structural blindness. The DALY is the metric version of the magic bullet -- a single instrument promising to cut through complexity.

3. **Krause, *The Good Project*** -- Krause showed how NGO organizational logic fragments reason. Adams's volume shows the same fragmentation through metrics: health problems are carved into measurable silos, relational work is translated into widgets, and organizations reshape their missions to fit funder demands for countable outcomes. Walkover's chapter on Hesperian is Krause's good-project problem in miniature.

4. **Tsing, *Friction*** -- Tsing showed that global connections are always mediated by local friction. Adams's chapters are a catalogue of those frictions: health workers in Malawi fabricating numbers to serve political masters; Senegalese nurses withholding data as political resistance; Yup'ik healers trying to fit subsistence fishing into a logic model. The metrics aspire to smooth, universal comparability; the ground resists.

5. **Benatar, "Moral Imagination"** -- Benatar's "silo approaches" are what you get when Adams's metrics logic meets health: each disease gets its own program, its own metrics, its own funding stream. Adams gives the mechanism; Benatar names the moral failure.

6. **Scott, *Seeing Like a State*** -- Adams cites Scott directly. The metrics of global health are legibility projects: they render complex social phenomena comparable and countable, and in doing so they make certain things visible and other things -- the things that actually determine whether people live or die -- invisible.

## Limits

- The volume is ethnographically rich but theoretically front-loaded. Adams's framing chapters (intro + ch. 1) carry most of the conceptual weight; some case-study chapters are more descriptive than analytical.
- No chapter addresses the perspective of the metrics producers themselves -- the epidemiologists, health economists, and data scientists who build the models. The volume critiques from outside the counting room.
- The "alternatives" section in the epilogue is thin. "Slow research" and "scaling across" are gestured at but not demonstrated. The book is much stronger on diagnosis than prescription -- which Adams acknowledges.
- All cases are from the 2010--2015 period. The Sustainable Development Goals (replacing MDGs) and the explosion of digital health data since 2016 have accelerated the trends Adams identifies but also introduced new dynamics the volume doesn't address.
- The volume does not engage with the possibility that some metrics regimes genuinely improve health outcomes in ways the ethnographic cases don't capture. The selection of cases favors dysfunction, which is appropriate for critique but risks presenting metrics as inevitably destructive.
