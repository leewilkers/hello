## Ursula K. Le Guin -- "A Rant About 'Technology'" (2005)

### The move

Reclaims the word "technology" from its captured meaning -- computers, jets, the complex and spectacular -- and restores the full sense: "the active human interface with the material world." Everything made is technology. Fishhooks, shoes, matches, linen, aspirin. The captured definition renders most technologies invisible, which is why a reviewer could say Le Guin's fiction "carefully avoided" technology when it's saturated with it. The reviewer's mistake is the culture's mistake.

### Passages that stuck

1. "Technology is the active human interface with the material world." [definitional -- the sentence that does the work. One line, and the hierarchy between "high" and "low" technology collapses.]

2. "But the word is consistently misused to mean only the enormously complex and specialised technologies of the past few decades, supported by massive exploitation both of natural and human resources." [diagnostic -- names the capture and its cost in one sentence]

3. "As if linen were the same thing as flax -- as if paper, ink, wheels, knives, clocks, chairs, aspirin pills, were natural objects, born with us like our teeth and fingers" [the naturalness illusion -- familiar technologies pass from "made" to "given"]

4. "I don't know how to build and power a refrigerator, or program a computer, but I don't know how to make a fishhook or a pair of shoes, either. I could learn. We all can learn. That's the neat thing about technologies. They're what we can learn to do." [the competence test and the reframe -- technology as learnable capability, not object class]

5. "Or maybe the hard stuff's inside, hidden -- like bones, as opposed to an exoskeleton...." [self-description and general principle -- the most important structures are the ones you don't see]

### Where it connects

1. **Franklin's holistic/prescriptive** -- Le Guin's definitional recovery restores the range Franklin's distinction needs. Holistic technologies (weaving, pottery, cooking) are exactly the technologies the captured definition makes invisible. Le Guin says they *are* technology; Franklin says they *work differently* from prescriptive ones. The two arguments need each other.

2. **Illich** -- Convivial tools span from hand tools to institutions. That range requires Le Guin's full definition. If "technology" means only computers and jets, then Illich's critique sounds like it applies to a subset. Le Guin: no, it's about everything made.

3. **Polanyi / Sennett / Ingold** -- "They're what we can learn to do" is the making lineage compressed into one clause. Technology as learnable capability, not object class. Polanyi: tacit knowledge acquired through practice. Sennett: the hand teaches the head. Ingold: making as knowing. Le Guin arrives at the same place from a poet's definition rather than a theorist's analysis.

4. **Berry** -- Arguing from opposite ends of the same position. Berry refuses a specific technology and names its costs. Le Guin insists technology is everything made, not just the complex and spectacular. Berry narrows; Le Guin widens. But the shared move: resist the cultural capture that equates "technology" with "the latest thing."

5. **Russell & Vinsel / Mattern** -- Maintenance is invisible because the technologies maintained are invisible. Le Guin's rant explains why: the captured definition of "technology" renders road surfaces, repair manuals, and public records sub-technological. Restore the word and the maintenance work becomes visible as what it is.

### What Lee does with it

[DRAFT] The definitional anchor for the technology lane. Le Guin's one sentence does the clearing work that makes Franklin, Illich, and the maintenance lineage legible. When Berry says he won't buy a computer, the implicit risk is that readers hear "anti-technology." Le Guin makes the correction: Berry isn't anti-technology. He already has technology -- a pencil, a typewriter, a wife who edits. He's refusing one technology in favor of others that already work. "The active human interface with the material world" is the sentence that unlocks the shelf's technology lane for anyone who starts with the assumption that technology = the latest thing.

### What it does NOT say

One move, undeveloped. Doesn't address why the word was captured -- what ideological work the narrowing does, who benefits from equating technology with complexity. Winner, Franklin, and Illich have answers; Le Guin stays at usage rather than power. The definition is a poet's definition, not an analytical one -- it includes nearly everything, which means you need Franklin's sub-distinctions to do real work with it. And no engagement with the gender politics of the hard/soft hierarchy, which Le Guin of all people could have pressed.
