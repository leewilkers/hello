## The Move

Mess is not entropy -- it is the visible collision of incompatible implied orders. Perry gives you a perceptual mechanism: objects project invisible "tendrils" of expected order into surrounding space, and mess is what you see when those tendrils conflict. After reading this, you can distinguish disorder (randomness, high entropy) from mess (competing legibilities), and you can see why highly ordered environments are more mess-prone than rough ones.

## Section-by-Section Notes

### Opening (Mess as intimate, not creative)

- Argument: Rejects the "mess is good for creativity" apologetics. Mess is stressful. The new thinking is wrong. But mess is not pure bad either -- Einstein's desk has *mono no aware*.
- Key passage: "All the new thinking about mess is apologetics: what if mess is good? Perhaps mess makes us more creative. Messiness is a sign of intelligence. All that. As a pathologically messy person, I cannot concur with this glorification of mess." (opening)
- Concepts: *picturesque mess* -- a mess rendered calming through external ordering devices (photographer's composition, monochrome palette). The photographer imposes order *on* the mess without resolving it.

### Alan Watts and the Cloud Problem

- Argument: Introduces the central puzzle via Watts. Clouds are not symmetrical, not cubes, not orderly -- yet they are not a mess. Why?
- Key passage: "When you look at the clouds they are not symmetrical. They do not form fours and they do not come along in cubes, but you know at once that they are not a mess." (Watts, *The Tao of Philosophy*)
- Concepts: The Watts observation is the fulcrum. It establishes that mess requires something beyond mere disorder -- it requires the *implication* of order.

### Mess Is Not Shannon Entropy

- Argument: The core analytical section. Three rectangles of pixels: pure black (max order, not mess), random noise (max entropy, not mess), overlapping text/logos (moderate entropy, definitely mess). The rectangle with *more* compressibility is messier. This disproves any information-theoretic account of mess.
- Key passage: "why are tableaux that are apparently more orderly (in the sense of compressibility in the data required to specify them) also more messy?"
- Concepts:
  - **Mess requires implied extreme order in component parts**: "in order for mess to appear, there must be in the component parts of the mess an implication of extreme order, the kind of highly regular order generally associated with human intention."
  - **Tendrils**: "It is as if objects and artifacts send out invisible tendrils into space, saying, 'the matter around me should be ordered in some particular way.' The stronger the claim, and the more the claims of component pieces conflict, the more there is mess."
  - **Cryptosalient**: "at once invisible and obvious" -- the tangled tendrils of incompatible orders that constitute what we "see" when we see mess.
  - **Mess as imaginary order**: "Mess is only perceptible because it produces in our minds an imaginary order that is missing."

### Friend definitions (interstitial)

- Sam Burnstein: "Messes are low-intentionality as a whole but high-intentionality in their component pieces."
- @allgebrah: "A mess is a decaying purpose."
- Chris Beiser: "incomplete aesthetic experience composed of a surplus of objects that produce aesthetic experiences... of vastly different types and durations, without a canonical ordering."
- Daniel Klein: "mess as matter deficient in side-effect-free interfaces."

### Buildings, garbage, and aesthetic exclusion

- Argument: Buildings project order onto their space, rendering some objects incompatible. Le Corbusier houses are maximally mess-prone because they project the strongest order. Garbage is "aesthetically excluded from almost every visible order."
- Key passage: "A *maison Corbu* strongly projects a particular order onto its space, such that the vast majority of human furnishings are aesthetically excluded, rendered mess. Living within these constraints is a discipline, and 'purifies the soul' in the sense of requiring you to live like you're on a camping trip all the time." (via Brand, *How Buildings Learn*)
- Concepts: **Aesthetic exclusion** -- objects are "mess" relative to the order of a particular space. The more austere the order, the larger the class of excluded objects. The bowerbird example: cognitive access to an *imaginary order* is not uniquely human.

### Feces and social masking

- Argument: Extends mess from the visual to the social. Feces exclusion as proto-mess-management. Pathogen avoidance as the origin of keeping "incompatible orders" separate. Politeness as the social equivalent of masking mess.
- Key passage: "In masking parts of reality, we need not become less ourselves; what is masked forms the background against which the form of what is not masked is clear and salient."

### Masking

- Argument: Snow on a city, black-and-white photography, strong composition -- all are masking operations that reduce mess by hiding conflicting orders. Alexander's ALCOVE pattern as architectural masking: the hobby-doer's mess is screened from observers.
- Key passage: "Covering certain aspects of reality is necessary to leave other aspects perceptible."
- Concepts: **Masking** as the primary mechanism for managing mess. Not eliminating competing orders but screening them from each other. Clothing as a portable alcove for the body.

### Orders of Strands (hair)

- Argument: Human hair as a case study in mess. Longest hair of any mammal. Its natural state is mess. Hair comes with an "order deficit" -- organizational effort required just to reach baseline. The tattered remains of a hairstyle are messier than merely unbrushed hair, because they imply a stronger competing order.
- Key passage: "Human hair, then, is a locus for the display of order. Its 'natural' state is mess, implying that hair comes with an order deficit, requiring organizational effort to come up to the level of acceptable human. Our minds and personalities are similar."

### The Order of Clouds (Alexander's Fifteen Properties)

- Argument: Watts's kaleidoscope transforms mess into non-mess by imposing local symmetries -- one of Alexander's Fifteen Fundamental Properties. The properties explain why natural things are never mess (structure-preserving processes at all scales) and why some human artifacts decay into mess while others don't.
- Key passage: "Being constantly rebuilt by independent entities and forces at all scales of time and space, constrained by each other, is how the order of nature never looks like a mess (except during tornado season)."
- Concepts: **Local symmetries** (Alexander) as the mechanism behind "the order of clouds." The car repair shop example: "'messy' order that has been created by the real everyday needs of the people who work there" vs. the shallow orderliness of a sterile storefront.

### Imaginary Orders

- Argument: Mess, humor, selfhood, and narrative are all products of conflicting or switching mental projections. Perry braids the threads: mess = incompatible projections in space; joke = incompatible projections switching in time; self = incompatible first-person and third-person projections; story = a projected model universe being continuously revised.
- Key passage: "As human beings, 'projecting and sharing stylized model worlds in mental space' is both our ancestral job and our favorite hobby. The world that we interact in is mostly imaginary, constructed by all of us out of fantasies and guesses."
- Concepts: **Imaginary orders** as the substrate of human reality. Nick Lowe's claim that narrative and game are "different cultural artifacts of a common underlying cognitive apparatus."

## Vocabulary

- **Tendrils** -- the invisible projections of expected order that objects send into surrounding space
- **Cryptosalient** -- "at once invisible and obvious"; what the tangled tendrils are
- **Imaginary order** -- the mentally projected arrangement that mess implies is missing
- **Aesthetic exclusion** -- the rendering of objects as "mess" by a space's projected order
- **Masking** -- covering incompatible orders to prevent mess (snow, composition, alcoves, clothing)
- **Mono no aware** -- used for the poignancy of Einstein's picturesque mess
- **Order deficit** -- the gap between a thing's natural state and acceptable order (hair, personality)
- **Local symmetries** -- Alexander's property that the kaleidoscope imposes; found in clouds and beautiful architecture
- **Structure-preserving transformation** -- (imported from Alexander/Perry's "Deep Laziness") processes that elaborate existing order rather than imposing new, competing order

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Rao, "A Big Little Idea Called Legibility" (2010)**: Perry's "tendrils" are the perceptual complement to Scott/Rao's legibility. Legibility is the *state's* need to impose readable order on complex reality; mess is what *perception* registers when imposed orders conflict. The Le Corbusier example appears in both -- Rao via Scott's high-modernist urbanism, Perry via Brand's interviews with Corbu residents. Same pathology, different entry point: Rao enters through governance, Perry through phenomenology.

2. **Perry, "Deep Laziness" (2018)**: "Tendrils" provides the theory of mess that "Deep Laziness" operationalizes. Structure-preserving transformations are exactly the process that avoids generating mess -- each elaboration strengthens existing order rather than introducing competing tendrils. The "decay reveals underlying levels of organization" claim in "Deep Laziness" depends on the "Tendrils" distinction between things that decay into mess (imposed orders) and things that don't (structure-preserving orders). The car repair shop in "Tendrils" is the architectural version of "elaborated time" in "Deep Laziness."

3. **Brand, *How Buildings Learn* (shelf)**: Perry cites Brand directly -- the Corbu house interview. Brand's shearing layers are a temporal theory of how buildings adapt; Perry's tendrils are a spatial theory of how buildings project order. The stone ruin that isn't a mess vs. the rectilinear room that is: the ruin was built by structure-preserving processes (Brand's "vernacular"), the room by imposed legible order.

4. **Alexander, *The Timeless Way of Building* / *The Nature of Order* (shelf)**: Perry is doing philosophy of Alexander. The Fifteen Fundamental Properties, local symmetries, "centers," and the ALCOVE pattern all appear. Perry's contribution is the negative case: Alexander describes what makes things *whole*; Perry describes what makes things *mess*. The two are the same theory applied to opposite poles.

## Limits

- No politics. Mess is analyzed as a perceptual and cognitive phenomenon, not a power relationship. Who gets to define what counts as mess, whose mess gets cleaned up, whose order gets imposed -- none of this is here. The Le Corbusier example gestures at it (the house "purifies the soul" by forcing ascetic discipline on residents) but Perry doesn't follow it to the political conclusion that Scott/Rao would.
- No account of mess as productive or generative. Perry rejects "mess is good for creativity" in the opening and never returns to it. There's no version of mess as compost, as the seedbed for new order, as the gap where something unexpected can happen. The essay is diagnostic, not therapeutic -- it explains what mess *is*, not what to do with it.
- The Alexander framework is taken as given. If you don't buy the Fifteen Fundamental Properties as a real description of natural order (rather than a projection of human aesthetics onto nature), the whole argument about "clouds are never a mess" becomes circular.
