## Wendell Berry -- "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer" (1987)

### The move

Berry refuses a computer, names nine standards any new tool should meet, and gets angry letters from Harper's readers who can't tolerate a mild personal dissent. The reply to those letters is the real essay: it identifies "technological fundamentalism" as a belief system that demands universal adoption, and it locates the conservation movement's structural failure in its refusal to connect consumption to the extraction it claims to oppose.

### Passages that stuck

1. "what would be superseded would be not only something, but somebody" [ethical-clarity -- names the human cost innovation rhetoric hides behind "the old model"]

2. "I have scratched the skin of a technological fundamentalism that, like other fundamentalisms, wishes to monopolize a whole society and, therefore, cannot tolerate the smallest difference of opinion" [diagnostic -- why a one-page essay about a typewriter provoked rage]

3. "consumers become salesmen, and the world is made safer for corporations" [structural -- the mechanism, in eight words]

4. "To the conservation movement, it is only production that causes environmental degradation; the consumption that supports the production is rarely acknowledged to be at fault" [intervention-critique -- applies directly to any organization that opposes harms it also funds]

5. "If the use of a computer is a new idea, then a newer idea is not to use one" [dry -- the last line, Berry's register at its best]

### Where it connects

1. **Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction** -- Berry's nine standards are what Franklin's holistic technology looks like as a personal checklist. Repairable, small-scale, non-displacing, locally sourced.

2. **Krause on NGO logic** -- Berry's conservation-movement critique (oppose production, protect consumption) is the same structural problem Krause finds in development NGOs: organizational logic that fragments the reasoning it claims to serve.

3. **Russell & Vinsel** -- The essay is a maintenance argument thirty years before "Hail the Maintainers." The working system doesn't need upgrading. The pressure to upgrade is ideological, not practical.

4. **Illich on convivial tools** -- Berry's refusal is a specific case of Illich's general principle. The nine standards could be an appendix to *Tools for Conviviality*.

### What Lee does with it

[DRAFT] The moral anchor of the shelf. Not because the refusal to buy a computer is the right move in 2026 -- it isn't, and Berry would be the first to say the specific case isn't the point. The move is: treat tool adoption as a moral question, not a productivity question. Name what gets displaced. Refuse the premise that the new is automatically better. The nine standards are a usable framework, not a historical curiosity. "Consumers become salesmen" is the single sharpest sentence on the shelf about how technology colonizes judgment -- and Lee is someone who uses AI tools more intensively than most people while holding that sentence in his head. That tension is the point.

### What it does NOT say

Nothing about collective action or institutional reform -- Berry's alternative to technological fundamentalism is personal conscience, which has obvious scale limits. Nothing about access: his refusal depends on a stable domestic arrangement and an established career. The essay is about *his* practice, and the letters expose a vulnerability (who gets to refuse?) that he doesn't fully address.
