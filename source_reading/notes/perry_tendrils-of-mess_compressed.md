## Sarah Perry -- "Tendrils of Mess in our Brains" (2017)

### The move

Mess is not disorder -- it is the collision of competing implied orders. Objects project invisible "tendrils" of expected arrangement into surrounding space; mess is what you perceive when those projections conflict. This means highly ordered environments are *more* mess-prone, not less, because they project stronger exclusionary demands onto everything around them.

### Passages that stuck

1. "It is as if objects and artifacts send out invisible tendrils into space, saying, 'the matter around me should be ordered in some particular way.' The stronger the claim, and the more the claims of component pieces conflict, the more there is mess." [core mechanism -- the image that carries the whole essay]

2. "In order for mess to appear, there must be in the component parts of the mess an implication of extreme order, the kind of highly regular order generally associated with human intention. Flat uniform surfaces and printed text imply, promise, or encode a particular kind of order. In mess, this promise is not kept." [the counterintuitive result -- more order = more mess potential]

3. "In masking parts of reality, we need not become less ourselves; what is masked forms the background against which the form of what is not masked is clear and salient." [reframes masking as structurally necessary, not dishonest]

4. "As human beings, 'projecting and sharing stylized model worlds in mental space' is both our ancestral job and our favorite hobby." [the final braid -- mess, humor, self, narrative as one apparatus]

### Where it connects

- **Rao, "Legibility"**: Legibility is the state's version of the same pathology. Perry gives you the perceptual mechanism Rao describes politically -- imposed order creating brittleness. The Le Corbusier example appears in both, via different routes (Brand for Perry, Scott for Rao).

- **Perry, "Deep Laziness"**: Structure-preserving transformations are the anti-mess method. "Tendrils" explains *why* things that grow through elaboration don't decay into mess -- no competing orders, just layers of the same one. The car repair shop here becomes "elaborated time" there.

- **Brand, *How Buildings Learn***: Directly cited. Perry's tendrils are spatial; Brand's shearing layers are temporal. The stone ruin that isn't a mess was built vernacularly (Brand's word); the rectilinear ruin that is mess was built by imposed design.

- **Alexander, *Timeless Way* / *Nature of Order***: Perry is doing Alexander's negative case. He describes what makes things whole; she describes what makes things mess. Same theory, opposite poles.

### What Lee does with it

DRAFT -- "A mess is a decaying purpose" is a three-word consulting diagnosis for institutional dysfunction. The tendrils framework explains why clean frameworks (logic models, M&E matrices, strategic plans) generate more perceived disorder than rough ones -- the stricter the template, the more everything outside it reads as failure. Also useful for the transparency question: not all masking is dishonest; some is structurally necessary for anything to be legible at all.

### What it does NOT say

No politics of mess -- who decides what counts as mess, whose order gets imposed, whose mess gets cleaned up. Perry stays in phenomenology where Scott/Rao would go to power. And no account of mess as generative -- the "mess is good for creativity" line gets dismissed in the first paragraph and never revisited.
