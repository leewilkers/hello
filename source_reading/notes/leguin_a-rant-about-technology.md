## The Move

Le Guin reclaims the word "technology" from its captured meaning ("hi tech," computers, jets) and restores its full sense: the active human interface with the material world. Everything made is technology -- fishhooks, shoes, matches, linen. The rant is triggered by a reviewer who said Le Guin's fiction "carefully avoided" technology because it isn't "hard" SF. Le Guin's correction: her fiction is saturated with technology, because every society she writes has ways of getting food, making clothes, building shelter, and healing the sick. The reviewer's mistake is the culture's mistake: equating "technology" with complexity and spectacle.

## Notes

### The Definitional Argument

- **Argument**: "Technology" has been captured. The word now means only the "enormously complex and specialised technologies of the past few decades," and everything else -- paper, wheels, knives, aspirin, saucepans -- gets treated as natural, as if it grew on trees. This is not a disagreement about science fiction. It is a disagreement about what counts as made.
- **Key passage**: "Technology is the active human interface with the material world."
- **Key passage**: "But the word is consistently misused to mean only the enormously complex and specialised technologies of the past few decades, supported by massive exploitation both of natural and human resources."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Technology as interface** -- Not a category of objects but a relationship: human agency meeting material reality. Everything from fire to refrigerators. The definition refuses the hierarchy that puts computers above fishhooks.
  - **The naturalness illusion** -- "As if linen were the same thing as flax -- as if paper, ink, wheels, knives, clocks, chairs, aspirin pills, were natural objects, born with us like our teeth and fingers." The illusion: familiar technologies become invisible as technologies. They pass from "made" to "given."

### The Competence Test

- **Argument**: One way to recover respect for "low" technologies is to ask whether you can make one. "Anybody who ever lighted a fire without matches has probably gained some proper respect for 'low' or 'primitive' or 'simple' technologies." The test isn't knowledge -- it's the recognition that all making is hard, and the label "simple" is applied by people who've never tried.
- **Key passage**: "I don't know how to build and power a refrigerator, or program a computer, but I don't know how to make a fishhook or a pair of shoes, either. I could learn. We all can learn. That's the neat thing about technologies. They're what we can learn to do."
- **Concepts**:
  - **"Do I know how to make one?"** -- The test. Applied honestly, it demolishes the hierarchy between "high" and "low" technology. Le Guin can't make a fishhook or a pair of shoes. Neither can the reviewer who thinks her fiction lacks technology.
  - **"They're what we can learn to do"** -- Technology defined as learnable human capability, not as a class of complex objects. This reframes the relationship: technology is not something that happens to us (the Winner/Franklin/Illich concern) but something we learn (the Polanyi/Sennett/Ingold concern). Both framings are true; Le Guin stakes out the second.

### The SF Argument (lighter)

- **Argument**: All science fiction is technological because every fictional society has a material culture. "Even when it's written by people who don't know what the word means." The hard/soft SF distinction is really about whether the technology is visible (engineering, machines) or structural (food, cloth, medicine, social arrangement).
- **Key passage**: "Maybe I write easy science fiction. Or maybe the hard stuff's inside, hidden -- like bones, as opposed to an exoskeleton...."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Hard stuff inside** -- Le Guin's self-description. The technology in her fiction is structural, not spectacle. "Like bones, as opposed to an exoskeleton" -- the load-bearing element that isn't on display.

## Vocabulary

- **Technology** -- "The active human interface with the material world." Le Guin's definition, reclaimed from its captured meaning.
- **Hi tech** -- What the word "technology" has been reduced to. Le Guin: not a synonym for "technology," just one extreme of a continuous range.
- **The material world** -- The other side of the interface. Food, clothing, shelter, power, medicine. Not "nature" (too abstract) -- the specific physical stuff that societies deal with.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Franklin's holistic vs. prescriptive technologies** -- Le Guin's definitional move recovers the full range that Franklin's distinction operates across. Franklin's "holistic technologies" (weaving, pottery, cooking) are exactly the technologies that the captured definition renders invisible. Le Guin: they are technology. Franklin: and they work differently from prescriptive ones. The two arguments need each other.

2. **Illich's Tools for Conviviality** -- Illich's argument requires that tools span the full range from hand tools to institutional systems. Le Guin's definition restores that range. If "technology" means only computers and jets, then Illich's critique of tools-as-institutional-control sounds like it's about a subset. Le Guin: no, it's about everything made, which is the point.

3. **Polanyi / Sennett / Ingold (the making lineage)** -- "They're what we can learn to do" is Polanyi's tacit dimension restated as a definition. Technology is not a thing; it is a capability. Sennett's craftsman acquires technology through practice. Ingold's maker discovers knowledge through materials. Le Guin's one-line definition ("the active human interface with the material world") is the making lineage's epistemology compressed into a sentence.

4. **Berry's "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer"** -- Berry and Le Guin are arguing from opposite ends of the same position. Berry refuses a specific technology and names the costs. Le Guin insists that technology is everything made, not just the complex and spectacular. Berry narrows; Le Guin widens. But the shared move is the same: resist the cultural capture that equates "technology" with "the latest thing." Berry's nine standards work precisely because they apply to all technologies, not just computers -- which requires Le Guin's definition.

5. **Mattern's repair and maintenance work** -- Mattern writes about road surfaces, repair manuals, public records -- technologies rendered invisible by the captured definition Le Guin diagnoses. If technology only means the complex and spectacular, then maintenance is sub-technological, mere upkeep. Le Guin's definition makes Mattern's subjects visible as what they are: technologies that require skill, knowledge, and care.

## Limits

- It's a rant, not an argument. Le Guin makes one move (the definitional recovery) and doesn't develop it. She doesn't address why the word was captured -- what ideological work the narrowing does, who benefits from equating technology with complexity. Winner, Franklin, and Illich all have answers; Le Guin stays at the level of usage rather than power.
- The "active human interface with the material world" is a poet's definition, not an analytical one. It includes everything humans make or do with materials, which is nearly everything. At that level of generality, "technology" loses discriminating power -- you need sub-distinctions (Franklin's holistic/prescriptive, Illich's convivial/industrial) to do analytical work.
- No engagement with the politics of who gets to call what "technology." The reviewer's dismissal of Le Guin's fiction as non-technological is also a gendered dismissal -- societies, cultures, food, clothing read as "soft" and feminine; machines, engineering, weapons read as "hard" and masculine. Le Guin names the hard/soft hierarchy but doesn't press on its gender politics, which she was perfectly capable of doing elsewhere.
