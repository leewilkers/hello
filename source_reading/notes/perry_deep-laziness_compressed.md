## Sarah Perry -- "Deep Laziness" (2018)

### The move

Deep laziness is not sloth but the generative method by which the universe (and people, when they're doing it right) produces beauty: find existing centers of structure, elaborate them in a way that strengthens rather than competes, repeat until done. Perry applies Christopher Alexander's theory of structure-preserving transformations -- normally about buildings and visual form -- to how a person spends time. Your behavioral repertoire is small ("about three to five things" for boredom). These few behaviors constitute your life. The method: find the ones that feel like reflections of your actual self (behavioral centers), elaborate them through structure-preserving changes, repair or release as circumstances shift. The result is "elaborated time" -- lazy, emergent, fitted -- as opposed to "scheduled time" -- legible, top-down, dread-producing.

### Passages that stuck

1. "If there is too much mess around you to see any structure, you can at least observe the pattern of your own soul, which is axiomatically not a mess, and try to find behaviors in the world that have the same nature." [the most emotionally direct line in all three Perry essays -- used without irony, anti-nihilist without optimism]

2. "Equivalents might be 'elaborated time' (lazy time, experienced as an unfolding and elaboration of behavioral centers) and 'scheduled time' (behaviors legibilized and organized top-down to satisfy a pre-existing image of proper behavior, OR the related dread that one has failed at this brutal form of organization)." [the sharpest distinction -- scheduled time doesn't just constrain, it produces the specific dread of self-failure]

3. "At the most profound level, the feeling itself is the most important part of the structure to preserve." [why the method can't be taught by prescription -- the test is felt, not analyzed]

4. "Clouds are beautiful and never a mess because they are products of structure-preserving processes. However, humans are capable of performing both structure-preserving and structure-destroying processes." [humans as the only agents that can generate mess]

### Where it connects

- **Alexander, *Timeless Way of Building* / *Nature of Order* (shelf)**: Perry is applying Alexander to time, behavior, and selfhood. Centers, structure-preserving transformations, generated vs. fabricated -- all Alexander. Perry's contribution: the behavioral turn. Alexander's buildings become Perry's routines.

- **Perry, "Tendrils of Mess" (2017)**: "Tendrils" is the negative case; "Deep Laziness" is the positive. Structure-destroying transformations produce mess (competing tendrils of implied order). Structure-preserving transformations produce beauty (coherent elaboration). The car repair shop in "Tendrils" -- "messy order created by real everyday needs" -- is the spatial version of elaborated time.

- **Perry, "Ritual and the Consciousness Monoculture" (2015)**: The confraternities that replaced rhythmic ritual with regulation were replacing elaborated time with scheduled time. The overburdened self from "Ritual" is what deep laziness addresses individually -- not through dissolution (hiving) but through fit (the self finds behaviors that have its own nature).

- **Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension* (shelf)**: "One person's ritual is all but useless to another, especially a really good ritual." The best behavioral centers are tacit -- adapted through feeling, incommunicable by prescription. Perry: "we barely know what feeling is, or how to feel, and if we managed to know, it would be impossible to communicate anyway."

- **Brand, *How Buildings Learn* (shelf)**: Shearing layers (site, structure, skin, services, space plan, stuff) at different rates of change -- behavioral centers have the same layered quality: some deep and slow ("old roots"), others seasonal (the treadmill that obsolesces in spring).

- **Illich, *Tools for Conviviality* (shelf)**: Elaborated time is convivial time -- the person controls the process. Scheduled time is prescriptive time -- the process controls the person. The institutional demands that prevent deep laziness are Illich's diagnostic of tools that have crossed the threshold from serving to dominating.

### What Lee does with it

DRAFT -- Elaborated vs. scheduled time as the clearest available distinction for the felt quality of work. Not about quantity (work less) but about mode (is the time generated from your centers or imposed from an image of proper behavior?). "The pattern of your own soul, which is axiomatically not a mess" as a bottom when everything is chaotic. The lab-rat-cage reframe: the question is not "how do I do more" but "what are my levers and are they the right ones?" -- and the heroin-lever cage as structural diagnosis, not personal failure.

### What it does NOT say

No structural analysis of who gets access to elaborated time -- the class dimension is acknowledged in one sentence ("we are effectively forbidden from doing so by the demands placed on us") and dropped. No engagement with collective or institutional deep laziness -- the method is entirely individual. Alexander's ontology (centers, structure-preserving transformations as features of reality, not aesthetic preference) is assumed, not argued.
