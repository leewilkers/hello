## The Move

Franklin treats technology not as a collection of artifacts but as practice -- "ways of doing something" -- and builds from that premise a distinction between holistic technologies (where the doer controls the process) and prescriptive technologies (where the process controls the doer). The prescriptive form, dominant since ancient China's bronze foundries and turbocharged by the Industrial Revolution, has produced a "culture of compliance" that now structures governance, education, health care, and daily life. The lectures amount to a political anatomy of how prescriptive technologies generate infrastructures, eliminate reciprocity, colonize planning, and turn citizens into plannees.

## Chapter Notes

### Lecture 1: Technology as Practice

**Argument**: Technology is not artifacts -- "the wheels and gears" -- but "a system" that "entails far more than its individual material components." Franklin defines technology as practice, links it to culture (shared practices and values), and introduces the book's central distinction: holistic technologies, where the artisan controls the process from beginning to finish, versus prescriptive technologies, where work is divided into steps and control moves to the organizer. Chinese bronze casting (1200 BC) is the extended example -- a prescriptive technology par excellence that required discipline, precision, and command, and whose social consequences ("acculturation into a milieu in which external control and internal compliance are seen as normal") Franklin argues shaped Chinese political thought. The lecture closes with growth models vs. production models and the argument that production models have colonized domains (education, health care, reproduction) where growth models are more appropriate.

**Key concepts**:
- **Technology as practice**: "one might think of technology as ways of doing something" (Boulding, via Franklin, p. 14). Not tools but formalized ways of working -- linking technology directly to culture.
- **Holistic technology**: Specialization by product. "Artisans, be they potters, weavers, metalsmiths, or cooks, control the process of their own work from beginning to finish" (p. 18). The doer makes situational decisions as the work proceeds.
- **Prescriptive technology**: Specialization by process. "The making or doing of something is broken down into clearly identifiable steps. Each step is carried out by a separate worker" (p. 19). "In political terms, prescriptive technologies are designs for compliance" (p. 23).
- **Culture of compliance**: "We live in a culture of compliance, that we are ever more conditioned to accept orthodoxy as normal, and to accept that there is only one way of doing 'it'" (p. 24).
- **Growth model vs. production model**: Growth is nurtured, not commanded; production is controlled and predictable. "Within a growth model, all that human intervention can do is to discover the best conditions for growth and then try to meet them" (p. 27). Production models discount context and externalities.

**Key passages**:
1. "Technology is not the sum of the artifacts, of the wheels and gears, of the rails and electronic transmitters. Technology is a system. It entails far more than its individual material components." (p. 12) [definitional]
2. "All craftsmen share a knowledge. They have held reality down, flattened to a bench; cut wood to their own purpose, compelled the growth of pattern with the patient shuttle. Control is theirs." (Sackville-West, quoted p. 19) [holistic ideal]
3. "In political terms, prescriptive technologies are designs for compliance." (p. 23) [diagnostic -- the sentence that carries the book]
4. "The unchallenged prevalence of the production model in the mindset and political discourse of our time, and the model's misapplication to blatantly inappropriate situations, seems to me an indication of just how far technology as practice has modified our culture." (p. 31) [structural]

**Vocabulary**: holistic technology, prescriptive technology, culture of compliance, growth model, production model, machine demography, work-related vs. control-related technologies

**Connects to**: Illich (tools for conviviality -- Franklin's holistic technologies are Illich's convivial tools, institutionally analyzed); Polanyi (tacit dimension -- holistic technology requires the kind of situational judgment Polanyi calls tacit knowledge); Sennett (craftsman -- Sennett's hand-teaches-head argument is Franklin's holistic technology described at the level of the body); Winner (whale and the reactor -- cited directly, p. 25)

### Lecture 2: Living and Working in the Real World

**Argument**: Technology has restructured every level of reality -- vernacular (everyday experience), extended (experience of others), constructed (fiction, advertising, propaganda), and projected (the future treated as present). Communications technologies produce "pseudorealities" that replace direct experience with manufactured images, creating "pseudocommunities" of viewers who share an event they never attended. The key concept is reciprocity: genuine communication requires interactive give-and-take, and most modern technologies eliminate it by design. Franklin distinguishes reciprocity sharply from feedback (feedback improves performance within a given design; reciprocity can alter the design itself).

**Key concepts**:
- **Vernacular reality**: "that nitty-gritty stuff, the direct action and immediate experience" (p. 36). Bread, work, shelter. Both private and political.
- **Pseudorealities**: Technologically produced images that create "a powerful illusion of presence in places and on occasions where the spectators, in fact, are not and have never been" (p. 42).
- **Reciprocity vs. feedback**: "Feedback is a particular technique of systems adjustment. It is designed to improve a specific performance... Reciprocity, on the other hand, is situationally based. It's a response to a given situation. It is neither designed into the system nor is it predictable." (p. 49)

**Key passages**:
1. "Whenever human activities incorporate machines or rigidly prescribed procedures, the modes of human interaction change. In general, technical arrangements reduce or eliminate reciprocity." (p. 48) [structural -- the mechanism]
2. "Media images seem to have a position of authority that is comparable to the authority that religious teaching used to have." (p. 43) [diagnostic]
3. "How come the right to change our mental environment -- to change the constructs of our minds and the sounds around us -- seems to have been given away without anybody's consent?" (p. 44) [political -- consent as the absent question]

**Vocabulary**: vernacular reality, extended reality, constructed reality, projected reality, pseudoreality, pseudocommunity, reciprocity, non-communications technologies

**Connects to**: Berger (ways of seeing -- Franklin's pseudorealities are Berger's "ways of seeing" applied to broadcast media); McLuhan (medium is the message -- Franklin's argument that the form of transmission restructures the content, but she's more politically specific than McLuhan); Beer (designing freedom -- Beer's cybernetic concern with feedback loops, but Franklin insists reciprocity is categorically different from feedback)

### Lecture 3: Technology as Catalyst for Control

**Argument**: Technologies exist in context, and tools tend to restructure the tasks they serve -- "any task tends to be structured by the available tools" (p. 56). Franklin traces how prescriptive technologies, well before the Industrial Revolution, provided the social soil for industrial control. Drawing on Foucault's *Discipline and Punish*, she shows how 17th- and 18th-century discipline (military drill, timed schooling, plague management) prepared society for the factory system: "the factory system, with its mechanical devices and machines, only augmented the patterns of control. The machinery did not create them" (p. 60). The lecture then turns to infrastructures -- roads, electrical grids, financial structures -- that governments build to support technology and that become invisible, unquestionable, and politically loaded. The concept of divisible vs. indivisible benefits: public resources increasingly fund private (divisible) gains while neglecting the commons (indivisible benefits like clean air, justice, safe roads).

**Key concepts**:
- **Tools restructure tasks**: "Any task tends to be structured by the available tools. It can appear that the available tools represent the best or even the only way to deal with a situation." (p. 56)
- **Prepared soil**: The factory system didn't create compliance; it grew in soil already prepared by centuries of prescriptive discipline. "It was into this socially and politically well prepared soil that the seeds of the Industrial Revolution fell" (p. 60).
- **Divisible vs. indivisible benefits**: Public institutions are supposed to safeguard indivisible benefits (justice, clean air, education). Technology has redirected public resources toward divisible benefits (private profit). "The public purse has provided the wherewithal from which the private sector derives the divisible benefits" (p. 70).
- **Technological decisions as hidden politics**: Robert Moses's low overpasses, designed to exclude buses (and therefore poor and Black riders) from parkways. "A technological expression of the same prejudice appeared to be all right" (p. 71).

**Key passages**:
1. "The factory system, with its mechanical devices and machines, only augmented the patterns of control. The machinery did not create them." (p. 60) [historical -- against technological determinism]
2. "The global environmental destruction that the world now has to face could not have happened without the evolution of infrastructures on behalf of technology and its divisible benefits, and corresponding eclipsing of governments' obligation to safeguard and protect the sources of indivisible benefits." (p. 70) [structural]

**Vocabulary**: divisible/indivisible benefits, infrastructure, prepared soil, resource base vs. habitat, radar-trap parable

**Connects to**: Foucault (discipline and punish -- cited extensively; Franklin uses Foucault's historical analysis but extends it into the technological present); Winner (artifacts have politics -- the Moses overpass example is Winner's canonical case, and Franklin cites Winner directly); Ferguson (anti-politics machine -- Ferguson's depoliticization through development echoes Franklin's depoliticization through infrastructure)

### Lecture 4: Planning and Planners

**Argument**: Planning is prescriptive technology applied to society. There are planners and "plannees" -- "those who plan and those who conform to what was arranged beforehand" (p. 81). Franklin distinguishes two planning strategies: maximizing gain (the production model, assumes stable context, transfers across situations) and minimizing disaster (respects context, treats every response as changing the situation, requires the experience of those affected). She gives two positive examples -- the Berger Inquiry into the Mackenzie Valley pipeline and the Science Council report *Canada as a Conserver Society* -- to show that minimizing-disaster planning is feasible. The lecture also addresses the military-industrial infrastructure: arms production requires a "credible long-term enemy" to justify public expenditure, and Franklin predicts (correctly, as the "war on drugs" addendum shows) that with the Cold War ending, the war machine will turn inward. The lecture closes with nature: Franklin argues "environment" is an egocentric term that treats nature as infrastructure, and wishes the Canadian government would treat nature "the way Canadian governments have always treated the United States of America -- with utmost respect and as a great power."

**Key concepts**:
- **Planners and plannees**: "There are those who plan and those who conform to what was arranged beforehand... The degree of effectiveness of participation by the plannees in long-term planning operations seems to me a true measure of democracy in the real world of technology." (p. 81)
- **Maximize gain vs. minimize disaster**: Two planning orientations. Gain-maximizing assumes stable context and predictable responses. Disaster-minimizing requires "recognition and a profound understanding of context" where "every response induces a counter-response which changes the situation" (p. 83).
- **The credible enemy**: "To keep the public funds flowing, justifications are needed. And this generates the need for a credible long-term enemy." (p. 78)
- **Nature as great power**: "I would therefore suggest to you that, in all processes of planning, nature should be considered as a strong and independent power. Ask, 'What will nature do?' before asking, 'What will the Americans do?'" (p. 88)

**Key passages**:
1. "At one point planning becomes prophecy -- not by what planning and structuring does, but by what the plans and structures prevent." (p. 79) [structural -- planning as foreclosure]
2. "What the real world of technology needs more than anything else are citizens with a sense of humility -- the humility of Kepler or Newton, who studied the universe but knew that they were not asked to run it." (p. 91) [ethical-clarity]

**Vocabulary**: plannees, maximize gain, minimize disaster, credible enemy, conserver society, domains of ignorance, redemptive technologies, nature as power

**Connects to**: Packard (history of global health -- the recurring pattern of planning that serves the planner, not the planned-for); Adams (metrics -- gain-maximizing requires metrics; Franklin's disaster-minimizing requires judgment); Tsing (friction -- Tsing's "zones of awkward engagement" are what Franklin calls context that planning protocols cannot transfer across)

### Lecture 5: The Lifecycle of Technologies

**Argument**: Technologies have an internal chronology: invention, growth, acceptance, standardization, stagnation. The social meaning shifts at each stage. In the early phase -- the "mechanical bride" phase -- users are involved, communities form around the technology, and the promise of liberation is credible. After standardization, users become captive supporters, alternatives are eliminated, and "a technology that had been perceived to liberate its users began to enslave them" (p. 97). Franklin traces this pattern through cars, audio equipment, computers, sewing machines, and telephones. The sewing machine example is devastating: marketed as liberation from drudgery, it became the instrument of sweatshop exploitation. Telephone operators -- women who served as "product-development engineers" making the technology useful -- were eliminated once the infrastructure was established. The computer's "mechanical bride" phase (1989) is compared to the introduction of processed food: gushy magazines, harmless domesticity, liberation rhetoric. "Behind that pink fluff one already sees the features of global restructuring" (p. 100).

**Key concepts**:
- **Three ages of a technology**: Youth (liberation, involvement, community), middle age (standardization, captivity, dependency), old age (stagnation, cosmetic change, ritualized competition).
- **Mechanical bride phase**: The early stage "in which various applications... were developed and tested" with active user involvement. Named after McLuhan. Users have "a feeling of control that gives them the impression that they are entirely free to accept or reject a particular technology" (p. 97).
- **Liberation-to-enslavement pattern**: "What turns the promised liberation into enslavement are not the products of technology per se... but the structures and infrastructures that are put in place to facilitate the use of these products and to develop dependency on them." (p. 102)
- **Logic of technology overpowers other logics**: "As more and more of daily life in the real world of technology is conducted via prescriptive technologies, the logic of technology begins to overpower and displace other types of social logic, such as the logic of compassion or the logic of obligation, the logic of ecological survival or the logic of linkages into nature." (p. 95)

**Key passages**:
1. "What turns the promised liberation into enslavement are not the products of technology per se -- the car, the computer, or the sewing machine -- but the structures and infrastructures that are put in place to facilitate the use of these products and to develop dependency on them." (p. 102) [structural -- the mechanism, named precisely]
2. "The logic of technology begins to overpower and displace other types of social logic, such as the logic of compassion or the logic of obligation." (p. 95) [diagnostic -- multiple logics, one winning]
3. "I urge you to look at some of the computer magazines from the point of view of set-up and style. You'll find columns on 'How I found ten more uses for Lotus 1-2-3'... These columns strongly remind me of columns like 'How I used a packaged cake mix and even my mother-in-law didn't notice it'." (p. 99) [dry -- registers the analogy without belaboring it]

**Vocabulary**: mechanical bride, logic of technology, liberation-to-enslavement, three ages of response, shadow work (Illich, cited)

**Connects to**: Illich (shadow work -- cited directly; prescriptive technologies require compliance from clients, not just workers); Russell & Vinsel (innovation delusion -- the mechanical-bride-to-captivity arc is what Russell & Vinsel will later call the innovation lifecycle); Krause (good project -- Franklin's sewing-machine-to-sweatshop pattern is Krause's "good project" logic applied to technology: the thing that looks like help becomes the instrument of extraction); Mattern (maintenance and care -- the telephone operators and secretaries who made technologies work and then were eliminated are the invisible maintainers Mattern will later champion)

### Lecture 6: Reflections on Change

**Argument**: The final lecture summarizes, then turns to what can be done. The crisis of technology is "actually a crisis of governance" -- "we have lost the institution of government in terms of responsibility and accountability to the people. We now have nothing but a bunch of managers, who run the country to make it safe for technology" (p. 120). Change will come not from leaders but from "movements from below" -- Franklin's "earthworm theory of social change." The practical agenda: an expanded concept of justice (stealing from the commons should be treated as crime, not entrepreneurship); a seven-point checklist for public projects (promotes justice, restores reciprocity, confers divisible or indivisible benefits, favours people over machines, minimizes disaster over maximizing gain, favours conservation over waste, favours the reversible over the irreversible); "redemptive technologies" developed from the receiving end of existing technologies; and three sets of books for any community decision (financial, social/human, environmental). The Peruvian copper-smelting example opens the lecture: technology is not preordained, and a different social structure produces a different technical process.

**Key concepts**:
- **Earthworm theory of social change**: "Social change will not come to us like an avalanche down the mountain. Social change will come through seeds growing in well prepared soil -- and it is we, like the earthworms, who prepare the soil." (p. 121)
- **Crisis of governance, not technology**: "I would like to suggest to you that the crisis of technology is actually a crisis of governance." (p. 120) "We now have nothing but a bunch of managers, who run the country to make it safe for technology." (p. 120)
- **Redemptive technologies**: Technologies developed to correct the harms of existing technologies -- redesigning industrial processes, developing from the needs of those at the receiving end, studying "things that work."
- **Seven-point checklist**: Promotes justice? Restores reciprocity? Divisible or indivisible benefits? Favours people over machines? Minimizes disaster or maximizes gain? Conservation over waste? Reversible over irreversible?
- **Stealing from the commons**: "If somebody robs a store, it's a crime and the state is all set and ready to nab the criminal. But if somebody steals from the commons and from the future, it's seen as entrepreneurial activity and the state cheers and gives them tax concessions." (p. 122)
- **Three sets of books**: Financial accounting, social/human impact accounting, environmental accounting. All three required for responsible decisions.

**Key passages**:
1. "Social change will not come to us like an avalanche down the mountain. Social change will come through seeds growing in well prepared soil -- and it is we, like the earthworms, who prepare the soil." (p. 121) [ethical-clarity -- the image that sticks]
2. "If somebody robs a store, it's a crime... But if somebody steals from the commons and from the future, it's seen as entrepreneurial activity and the state cheers and gives them tax concessions." (p. 122) [structural -- justice reframed]
3. "We now have nothing but a bunch of managers, who run the country to make it safe for technology." (p. 120) [diagnostic]
4. "Whenever someone talks to you about the benefits and costs of a particular project, don't ask 'What benefits?' ask 'Whose benefits and whose costs?'" (p. 124) [practical -- the question to carry]

**Vocabulary**: earthworm theory, redemptive technologies, domains of ignorance, new social contract, three sets of books, stealing from the commons

**Connects to**: Berry (why I am not going to buy a computer -- Berry's nine standards are an individual-scale version of Franklin's seven-point checklist; Berry's "technological fundamentalism" is what Franklin calls the culture of compliance seen from the dissenter's position); Schumacher (small is beautiful -- cited extensively; Franklin's "as if people mattered" is Schumacher's subtitle, and her disaster-minimizing planning is Schumacher's "appropriate technology" given a political framework); Illich (shadow work, research by people -- cited directly as source for "technology from the bottom up")

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Illich -- Tools for Conviviality**: Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction is Illich's convivial/manipulatory distinction analyzed at the level of the division of labour. Illich argues tools should serve human-scale agency; Franklin shows the historical mechanism by which they stopped doing so. She cites Illich explicitly on "shadow work" and "vernacular values." The lineage runs Illich -> Franklin -> Russell & Vinsel.

2. **Winner -- The Whale and the Reactor**: Cited directly (p. 25, on "digitalized footprints of social transactions"). Winner's "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" is the theoretical framework Franklin's Robert Moses overpass example illustrates. Franklin extends Winner by tracing the politics not just of artifacts but of the *infrastructures* that make artifacts normal.

3. **Berry -- "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer"**: Berry's nine standards for tool adoption are Franklin's holistic-technology criteria restated as a personal decision framework. Berry's "technological fundamentalism" is what Franklin calls the culture of compliance seen from the perspective of someone who refuses. Both were writing in the late 1980s, apparently independently.

4. **Russell & Vinsel -- "Hail the Maintainers" / The Innovation Delusion**: Franklin is the explicit precursor. Her "mechanical bride" lifecycle (liberation -> standardization -> captivity) is what Russell & Vinsel will diagnose as the innovation delusion. Her analysis of telephone operators and secretaries as invisible developers who are eliminated once the technology matures anticipates Mattern's and Vinsel's maintenance arguments by decades.

5. **Ferguson -- The Anti-Politics Machine**: Ferguson's argument that development projects depoliticize by translating political questions into technical ones runs parallel to Franklin's argument that technological infrastructures absorb political decisions into technical settings "far away from public scrutiny" (p. 71). Both describe the same mechanism: power exercised through design rather than declaration.

6. **Adams -- Metrics**: Franklin's production model -- predictable, controllable, context-free -- is the epistemological ground on which Adams's metrics operate. When Franklin says planning to maximize gain "assumes a stable context and predictable responses" (p. 83), she is describing the conditions under which metrics seem adequate. Her alternative (minimize disaster, respect context, consult the plannees) is the planning philosophy that would make Adams's critique unnecessary.

7. **Foucault -- Discipline and Punish**: Cited extensively in Lecture 3. Franklin uses Foucault's analysis of 17th/18th-century discipline as prepared soil for the factory system, but she pushes beyond Foucault into the technological present and toward practical alternatives he never offered.

8. **Mumford -- Condition of Man / Technics and Civilization**: Cited on the moral inversion ("the seven deadly sins became the seven cardinal virtues"). Franklin shares Mumford's historical method and his conviction that technological history is social history.

## What Lee Does With It

[DRAFT] The holistic/prescriptive distinction is the most-used framework from Lee's shelf in his consulting work. It shows up whenever he needs to diagnose why a program that looked good on paper produced compliance instead of engagement -- why a training protocol flattened situated judgment, why a reporting system displaced the relationships it was supposed to serve, why standardizing a process across contexts killed the thing that made it work in each one. The distinction is load-bearing because it doesn't blame technology or defend it; it asks *how the work is organized* and what that organization does to the people doing it. Franklin's "designs for compliance" is the phrase that sticks -- it names the political content of a process design without requiring anyone to have intended it. In global health and ethics consulting, where prescriptive protocols are the medium of the work, the question "Is this holistic or prescriptive?" is a diagnostic that opens conversations nothing else can. The seven-point checklist (Lecture 6) is less directly usable but the underlying principle -- "whose benefits and whose costs?" -- is the question Lee carries into every review.

## Limits

- The lectures are Canadian-specific in their examples (Ontario Hydro, Via Rail, the Mackenzie Valley pipeline, PCBs in Quebec) and Cold War-specific in their political analysis (the credible-enemy argument). The analytical framework transcends both contexts, but the applications date.
- Franklin's positive program (Lecture 6) is thin compared to her diagnosis. "Redemptive technologies" is a gesture toward a category, not a developed concept. The earthworm theory is a posture, not a strategy. She acknowledges this: "Don't expect from me a blueprint to cure the faults of all other blueprints."
- The gender analysis is present throughout but could be stronger. Franklin notes that prescriptive technologies are gendered and that women's historical work is holistic, but she does not fully develop the argument that the prescriptive/holistic distinction *is* a gender distinction -- that the displacement of holistic by prescriptive is, among other things, the displacement of women's modes of work by men's modes of control.
- Nothing here about the Global South beyond brief mentions of population policy and "Third World" hunger. The development-critique lineage on Lee's shelf (Ferguson, Tsing, Packard, Adams) fills this gap, but Franklin's framework could have been applied there and wasn't.
- The 1989 computing discussion is necessarily pre-internet. Franklin's analysis of the "mechanical bride" phase of personal computing was prescient, but she could not have foreseen networked surveillance, platform monopolies, or algorithmic control. The framework (liberation -> dependency -> captivity) applies more forcefully to 2026 than to 1989, but the specific examples need updating.
