## Solomon Benatar -- "Moral Imagination: The Missing Component in Global Health" (2005)

### The move

Global health failure is not a knowledge gap or a funding gap -- it is a moral imagination gap. The capacity to connect one's own comfort to others' deprivation has been systematically dulled. Aid is necessary but structurally insufficient because its high profile obscures the larger extraction: trade rules, debt service, and brain drain transfer more out of developing countries than aid puts in. The essay names the mechanism that keeps the failure loop turning: indomitable optimism focused on the next intervention prevents confrontation with upstream causes.

### Passages that stuck

1. "the problem is how the high profile given to relatively small amounts of aid eclipses recognition of the fact that financial, human, and other material resources are continuously being extracted from developing countries by wealthy nations striving for their own ongoing economic growth." [structural -- the eclipse metaphor; aid as cover story for extraction]

2. "there is generally more interest in doing research to acquire new knowledge than in using existing knowledge, unless it is commercially profitable -- illustrating how market forces are a more powerful influence on the practice of medicine than health needs." [diagnostic -- names the commercial-profitability filter on which knowledge gets applied]

3. "The ability to empathise with others requires the critical examination of our individual lives and of our nations' actions, the capacity to see ourselves as bound to all other human beings, and the sensitivity to imagine what it might be like to be a person living a very deprived and threatened life." [definitional -- the three components of moral imagination]

4. "obfuscation by politicians and indomitable optimism focused on economics, science, and human rights all promote continued hope for improving health in the developing world through market forces and new technologies." [mechanism -- optimism as a form of obfuscation, not a response to evidence]

5. "Because wealthy nations, and by association their citizens, are deeply implicated in the generation and maintenance of forces that perpetuate social injustice and poverty, they need to face their responsibilities to alleviate the lives of those most adversely affected." [ethical -- moves from passive indifference to active complicity]

### Where it connects

- **Ferguson, *Anti-Politics Machine***: Ferguson showed one project depoliticizing; Benatar shows the entire aid system doing it at scale. The "eclipse" is the macro version of the anti-politics machine -- aid visibility neutralizes the political question of extraction.

- **Packard, *History of Global Health***: Packard documented the recurring cycle of magic-bullet optimism; Benatar names the moral failure that keeps the cycle turning. Roll Back Malaria is one instance of the pattern Packard traces across a century.

- **Krause, *The Good Project***: Krause showed NGOs as media companies producing narratives of suffering. Benatar's "high profile of aid" is the system-level version -- the narrative is the product, and the product obscures the extraction.

- **Fassin, *Humanitarian Reason***: Fassin's "politics of pity" is the affective structure underneath Benatar's "transient concern for the anonymous poor." Both argue that the emotional architecture of compassion is part of the problem.

- **Rindova & Martins (2023)**: Picked up "moral imagination" as a concept for organizational strategy. Benatar is the global health origin; Rindova & Martins are the organizational theory extension. The lineage is thin but deliberate on the shelf.

### What Lee does with it

DRAFT -- The eclipse frame is the sharpest tool here. When an institution foregrounds its aid, philanthropy, or capacity-building, the Benatar question is: what does that visibility obscure? What flows in the other direction? The "indomitable optimism" diagnosis also applies directly to programme design -- when an organization is optimistic about the next initiative, ask what the optimism is protecting them from seeing. Moral imagination as a concept names what's missing when institutions have technical capacity but not the will to examine their own complicity. Pairs with Krause for organizational consulting; pairs with Ferguson for structural analysis.

### What it does NOT say

No programme for cultivating moral imagination -- the essay diagnoses its absence but never operationalizes its presence. No account of agency in the global South; developing countries appear as objects of extraction and recipients of aid, not as political actors. No engagement with the possibility that moral imagination might itself be structurally constrained (by media, by geography, by economic incentives) rather than merely "dulled." The economics are twenty years old. And the concept inherits empathy's individualist bias -- three individual cognitive acts (self-examination, recognition, identification) without a structural theory of how institutions might cultivate or suppress them.
