## The Move

Social science methods don't just describe realities -- they help produce them. Law argues that the world is messy, vague, ephemeral, and multiple in ways that standard methods (quantitative or qualitative) cannot catch. The "method assemblage" -- the full hinterland of instruments, skills, assumptions, funding priorities, and material arrangements behind any research act -- enacts particular realities while rendering others invisible or impossible. The radical claim: out-thereness is not prior to method but accomplished by it. Realities are effects of the apparatuses of inscription. This doesn't mean we can believe anything we like -- but it means that which realities get made is partly a political question, and methods that insist on clarity and definiteness are making a political choice to unmake the vague, the fluid, and the multiple.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Notes

### Chapter 1: After Method -- An Introduction

- **Argument**: The world is textured in ways that standard methods miss -- pains, pleasures, intuitions, things that slip and slide, emotions, the ephemeral. Research methods assume the world is "a set of fairly specific, determinate, and more or less identifiable processes." But much of reality is vague, diffuse, elusive, or multiple. The task: imagine methods that don't seek the definite and the repeatable.
- **Key passage**: "If this is an awful mess ... then would something less messy make a mess of describing it?"
- **Concepts**:
  - **The generative flux** -- The world is not a structure to be mapped but a maelstrom filled with currents, eddies, and unpredictable changes. Sometimes a chart works. Mostly it doesn't.
  - **Knowing otherwise** -- Embodiment, emotionality, deliberate imprecision, situated inquiry. "Perhaps the academy needs to think of other metaphors for its activities -- or imagine other activities."

### Chapter 2: Pinboards and Hinterlands

- **Argument**: Through Latour and Woolgar's *Laboratory Life*, Law shows how scientific facts are produced by comparing inscriptions -- traces generated by instruments. The "hinterland" of standardized packages (machines, skills, statements) produces realities while concealing its own work. "Out-thereness" is a consequence of scientific work rather than its cause. Reality is secreted through a feedback loop: yesterday's modalities become tomorrow's hinterland.
- **Key passage**: "'Out-there-ness' is the consequence of scientific work rather than its cause." (Latour & Woolgar)
- **Key passage**: "Reality is secreted."
- **Concepts**:
  - **The hinterland** -- The full ramification of relations that sustain a statement: instruments, skills, other statements, material arrangements, funding structures. Extends far beyond what "method" conventionally names. Porous, indefinite, stretching in every direction.
  - **Inscription devices** -- Not just instruments but any set of arrangements for "converting relations from non-trace-like to trace-like form." The special materiality of science.
  - **Deletion, splitting, inversion** -- How realities come to look independent and prior: the work of production is deleted, the statement splits from its object, then a causal inversion occurs so that reality becomes the cause of the statement rather than its product.
  - **Method assemblage** -- Law's neologism: "the enactment or crafting of a bundle of ramifying relations that generates presence, manifest absence and Otherness." Not rules for best practice but the full apparatus of reality-production including its tacit, invisible, and suppressed components.
  - **Five features of Euro-American out-thereness** -- Independence, anteriority, definiteness, singularity, and primitive out-thereness. Latour and Woolgar challenge all but the last.

### Chapter 3: Multiple Worlds

- **Argument**: Through Annemarie Mol's study of lower-limb atherosclerosis at "Hospital Z," Law shows that the same disease is different things in different sites. In the clinic, it's intermittent claudication (a patient's story + a surgeon's skill). In pathology, it's a thickened intima under a microscope. In radiology, it's lumen loss on an angiograph. In duplex, it's velocity ratios from ultrasound. In the operating theater, it's a white plaque pulled from an artery. These are not perspectives on one disease. They are different realities enacted by different method assemblages.
- **Key passage**: "Under a microscope." -- The pathologist's afterthought that localizes his reality-claim.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Multiplicity vs. perspectivalism** -- Perspectivalism assumes one object seen from multiple angles. Mol's multiplicity: different practices enact different objects. "Atherosclerosis in the clinic is a different thing to atherosclerosis in the pathology lab."
  - **Coordination without coherence** -- The different atheroscleroses don't always agree. They're held together not by underlying unity but by practical coordination -- referral pathways, medical conventions, organizational routines. Sometimes they align; sometimes they don't.
  - **Enactment** -- Realities are not discovered but enacted in practice. The verb matters: it implies activity, specificity, and the possibility that different practices enact different realities.

### Chapter 4: Arguments (and more on multiplicity)

- **Argument**: Extends the multiplicity argument through further cases. Realities can be multiple and still coexist -- they don't need to be resolved into singularity. Strategies for handling multiplicity: addition (combining perspectives), denial (insisting on one version), calibration (adjusting instruments until they agree), and prioritization (one version trumps another in decision-making).
- **Concepts**:
  - **Strategies of coordination** -- How multiple realities are made to hang together without being forced into coherence. Each strategy has political implications: which version wins, which gets silenced.

### Chapter 5: Making a Mess with Method

- **Argument**: The heart of the book's methodological proposal. If the world is multiple, vague, and messy, then methods need to be too. "Allegory" becomes central -- a mode of representation that holds multiple meanings simultaneously without resolving them. Against the "method police" who insist on clarity and rigor as the only standards.
- **Key passage**: "Simple clear descriptions don't work if what they are describing is not itself very coherent. The very attempt to be clear simply increases the mess."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Allegory** -- Not a literary device imposed on research but a recognition that research always says more than one thing at once. "Any ethnographic text, however apparently transparent, is also necessarily allegorical."
  - **Presence, manifest absence, Otherness** -- Method assemblages produce presence (what gets reported), manifest absence (what is visibly excluded), and Otherness (what is rendered unthinkable). The politics of method lies in these three categories.
  - **Mess as method** -- Not sloppiness but the deliberate refusal to clean up what isn't clean. "If we want to think about the messes of reality at all then we're going to have to teach ourselves to think, to practise, to relate, and to know in new ways."

### Chapter 6: The Euro-American Patchwork

- **Argument**: Euro-American assumptions about reality (independent, anterior, definite, singular) are not universal truths but historical accomplishments embedded in specific practices. Law examines how these assumptions have been naturalized and what happens when they're questioned.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Naturalized metaphysics** -- The assumptions of definiteness and singularity are so embedded in research practice that they're invisible. Questioning them feels like questioning reality itself -- which is the point.

### Chapter 7: Fluid and Fire

- **Argument**: Two further forms of out-thereness beyond the network metaphor: fluid spaces (where things change shape gradually without sharp boundaries) and fire spaces (where things appear and disappear, are present and absent simultaneously). Bush pumps in Zimbabwe as fluid technology. Aboriginal Australian fire as a topology of discontinuous presence.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Fluid topology** -- Objects that maintain their identity while gradually changing form. Unlike networks (where identity is maintained by fixed connections), fluids are adaptive, shape-shifting, resilient precisely because they're not rigid.
  - **Fire topology** -- Discontinuous presence and absence. Things that are simultaneously here and somewhere else, now and then, present and absent. A kind of reality that Euro-American methods particularly struggle with.

### Chapter 8: After Method

- **Argument**: Returns to the opening question: how might method deal with mess? Not by abandoning rigor but by expanding what counts as rigorous. If realities are enacted, then method is always political. The question becomes: what realities do we want to help bring into being?
- **Key passage**: "If we want to think about the messes of reality at all then we're going to have to teach ourselves to think, to practise, to relate, and to know in new ways."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Ontological politics** -- If methods enact realities, then choosing methods is choosing realities. This is a political act whether acknowledged or not.
  - **Modest sociology** -- Not a retreat from ambition but a recognition that any account of reality is partial, situated, and has consequences. The alternative to methodological imperialism.

## Vocabulary

- **Method assemblage** -- The full hinterland of material and social arrangements that produces research findings. Extends far beyond "methods" as usually taught: into funding structures, career incentives, tacit assumptions, material infrastructure.
- **Hinterland** -- The ramifying set of relations that sustains any statement or reality. Includes instruments, skills, previous findings (now black-boxed), organizational routines, and much that is invisible.
- **Enactment** -- The production of reality through practice. Different practices enact different realities. Preferred over "construction" because it implies ongoing activity rather than a one-time event.
- **Inscription device** -- Any arrangement for converting relations into traces. Not just instruments but the full apparatus of making things visible, countable, comparable.
- **Multiplicity** -- Different practices enact different objects. Not multiple perspectives on one thing but multiple things. The disease in the clinic is not the same disease as in the pathology lab.
- **Out-thereness** -- Reality. But specified into five features (independence, anteriority, definiteness, singularity, primitive out-thereness) that can be questioned individually.
- **Allegory** -- A mode of telling that holds multiple meanings without resolving them. Law's preferred alternative to "transparent" research accounts.
- **Otherness** -- What method assemblages render unthinkable. Not just excluded but made impossible to articulate within the given framework.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Flyvbjerg, *Making Social Science Matter*** -- Flyvbjerg wants social science to matter by doing phronesis (practical wisdom). Law says the methods themselves are the problem -- you can't clean up the mess. Both diagnose the same disease; they disagree about whether treatment is possible. Flyvbjerg keeps the patient on medication; Law says the medication is producing the symptoms.

2. **Mills, *The Sociological Imagination*** -- Mills argued sociology had become too abstract and too empiricist simultaneously. Law radicalizes this: the issue isn't just that sociology has lost its way but that its methods produce the reality they claim to study. Mills wanted to reconnect personal troubles and public issues; Law wants to reconnect method and ontology.

3. **Berger, *Ways of Seeing*** -- Berger showed how images condition perception -- who gets to see, how visibility is constructed. Law extends this into method: research instruments condition what can be seen, and the unseen isn't just hidden but actively unmade. "Ways of seeing" becomes "ways of enacting."

4. **Polanyi, *The Tacit Dimension*** -- "We know more than we can tell." Law's hinterland is largely tacit -- the embodied skills, unspoken assumptions, and black-boxed instruments that sustain research but never appear in the methods section. The tacit dimension is not a limitation but a condition of possibility.

5. **Franklin, holistic vs. prescriptive technologies** -- Method as prescriptive technology: the researcher follows rules that control the process and produce standardized outputs. Law's method assemblage is the holistic alternative -- the researcher stays in control of the process, responsive to the material, adapting to mess rather than eliminating it.

6. **Ferguson, *The Anti-Politics Machine*** -- Ferguson showed how development projects depoliticize. Law shows how research methods depoliticize by assuming that reality is independent, anterior, definite, and singular. The "method police" and the development apparatus perform the same structural function: naturalizing a particular version of reality.

## Limits

- The book is more convincing as critique than as construction. "After method" names what comes after but doesn't give practitioners much to do Monday morning. The alternative methods (allegory, deliberate imprecision, mess) are evocative but operationally thin.
- The reliance on STS (Science and Technology Studies) cases -- particularly Latour and Woolgar, Mol -- means the argument is strongest for laboratory and clinical settings. How the method assemblage framework applies to, say, qualitative interview research or participatory evaluation is left to the reader.
- Law's claim that "realities are enacted" risks collapsing into the very relativism he disclaims. The caveat ("believing something is never enough to make it true") does work, but the framework gives no clear criteria for distinguishing better and worse enactments beyond political preference.
- Nothing about quantitative research that works well -- the epidemiological link between smoking and lung cancer gets a paragraph of acknowledgment, then the book moves on. The positive cases for standard methods are mentioned but never engaged.
- The prose itself enacts the argument -- diffuse, recursive, deliberately imprecise -- which makes it powerful as philosophy but taxing as a reading experience. This is probably deliberate.
