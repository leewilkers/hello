## Sasha Chapin -- "Notes Against Note-Taking Systems"

### The move

Note-taking systems destroy what they claim to preserve. Your natural salience filter -- what sticks without effort -- is a better selector than any external system, because it reflects what's actually alive in your thinking rather than what "seems like it should be interesting." The system becomes the work, the preparation becomes the barrier, and originality dies in accurate retrieval. What Chapin calls "read/write errors" -- the fragmentary, distorted versions of ideas that survive in memory -- are the mechanism of original thought, not information loss.

### Passages that stuck

1. "Your natural salience filter is a great determinant of what's most alive to you. If you begin to rely on any other filter, you will increasingly record what *seems like it should be interesting according to some preexisting criteria* rather than what organically sticks to your mind." [epistemological -- the filter argument, and the tell: "seems like it should be"]

2. "you will never transmute into the perfectly prepared person that you think exists in the future. Unfortunately, you have to start with the person currently in this chair. That's all there ever is." [existential -- the avoidance mechanism named]

3. "These read/write errors are what we call *originality.*" [core reframe -- memory's distortions are features, not bugs]

4. "If your note-taking system is adapted to a specific context of use such as this, then you're working. If it's not, then you're LARPing." [diagnostic -- the only test that matters]

5. "Shun the useless adoption of the aesthetic of the useful. When something can be like work or like play, never make it work." [final line -- the aesthetic warning]

### Where it connects

1. **Polanyi's tacit dimension** -- The "natural salience filter" is tacit knowledge applied to reading. What sticks is what your embodied understanding found important, which exceeds what you can articulate. Chapin's argument against retrieval systems is Polanyi's argument against formalization: the most important knowledge resists capture.

2. **Mills' "On Intellectual Craftsmanship"** -- Mills kept a single file and advocated for exactly this kind of low-system, high-use practice. The difference: Mills assumed the thinker would make something with the notes. Chapin is writing for people who confuse the file with the work.

3. **Berry's refusal logic** -- Berry refuses a computer; Chapin refuses a system. Same structure: the new tool promises capability but displaces something that already works. Both locate the problem in the compulsion to adopt, not the technology itself.

4. **Perry's tendrils** -- A second brain is a highly ordered system that projects strong demands onto everything around it. When your actual thinking doesn't match the system's categories, the mismatch registers as mess. Perry explains why elaborate note systems eventually feel oppressive rather than liberating.

No engagement with how systems change thinking, not just recording (the tools-shape-users problem would complicate the clean filter-vs.-system distinction). The essay is right about the common case and wrong about the edge cases it waves away.
