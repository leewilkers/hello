## The Move

Humanitarian government -- the deployment of moral sentiments in contemporary politics -- has become the dominant mode of governing precarious lives. Fassin traces how compassion replaced justice as the operative language for managing the poor, the undocumented, the asylum seeker, the disaster victim. The book's real force is ethnographic: not what humanitarianism says about itself, but what it does when observed up close. In listening centers, immigration offices, allocations committees, refugee camps, war zones -- the same structure repeats. The sufferer must perform suffering to receive aid. The giver must evaluate the performance. The exchange is structurally unequal regardless of anyone's intentions. "Humanitarian reason pays more attention to the biological life of the destitute and unfortunate, the life in the name of which they are given aid, than to their biographical life, the life through which they could, independently, give a meaning to their own existence."

## Chapter-by-Chapter Notes

### Preface to the English Edition

- Sets up the dual meaning of "humanity": mankind (species membership, basis for rights) and humaneness (moral sentiment, basis for compassion). The tension between these two meanings structures the entire book.
- Key framing: "This morally driven, politically ambiguous, and deeply paradoxical strength of the weak I propose to call humanitarian reason."
- Haiti example: the earthquake erased memories of French and American exploitation of the island. "Our response to it signified the promise of reparation and the hope for reconciliation." Generosity as forgetting.
- The consensual force of humanitarianism: "it fugaciously and illusorily bridges the contradictions of our world, and makes the intolerableness of its injustices somewhat bearable."

### Introduction: Humanitarian Government

- Argument: Moral sentiments have become "an essential force in contemporary politics." The expression "humanitarian government" designates the deployment of these sentiments -- not limited to NGOs in the Third World but encompassing all governance of precarious lives.
- The central paradox: compassion is simultaneously a politics of inequality (directed from above to below) and a politics of solidarity (premised on recognizing the other as fellow). This tension is constitutive, not accidental.
- "Precarious lives" in the strongest Latin sense: lives "not guaranteed but bestowed in answer to prayer" -- defined by the relation to those who have power over them.
- Methodological position: Fassin sits between Bourdieu (who takes suffering as real but misses its construction) and Boltanski (who analyzes the rhetoric of pity but derealizes the political stakes). Ethnography is the corrective for both.
- Semantic shift: "Inequality is replaced by exclusion, domination is transformed into misfortune, injustice is articulated as suffering, violence is expressed in terms of trauma." The old vocabulary of social critique has not disappeared but is increasingly masked.
- Key concept: "moral economy" -- not in E.P. Thompson's sense of popular norms, but as "the production, distribution, circulation, and use of moral sentiments, emotions, and values, norms and obligations in social space."

### Chapter 1: Suffering Unveiled (Listening to the Excluded and the Marginalized)

- Setting: listening centers in France created to address "social suffering" -- the new category that emerged in the 1990s.
- The listening center translates structural problems (unemployment, housing, poverty) into psychological ones (suffering, depression, trauma). Social workers become listeners; political problems become therapeutic ones.
- Concepts: **Psychologization of the social** -- when the response to structural inequality is to offer someone who will listen to your pain, the problem is redefined as one of recognition rather than redistribution.

### Chapter 2: Pathetic Choice (Exposing the Misery of the Poor)

- Setting: The Social Emergency Fund in France, 1998. 150 million euros distributed to "the most disadvantaged" through allocations committees.
- Ethnographic core: how senior civil servants decided who got money. Applicants had to write statements describing their hardship. The "rest for living" -- income minus fixed costs divided by household members -- became the decisive threshold at 150 euros/person.
- Four rhetorical tropes in applicants' statements: need (factual accounting -- least effective), justice (vindication -- rare but effective when used), merit (dignified style, virtue register -- most persuasive), compassion (imploring style -- surprisingly ineffective due to routinization).
- Key finding: **compassion fatigue as structural feature**. The rate of favorable decisions fell from 75.1% in January-February to 62.5% in May-June. The average grant fell from 406 euros to 286 euros. "Over time, compassion became dulled through constant contact with the hardship of the applicants."
- The "message" committees tried to send recipients through precise grant amounts was invisible to recipients -- "the administrators were in fact talking to themselves."
- Concepts: **the arbitrary and the contingent** -- unjust decisions not from absence of norms but from excess of norms. Multiple competing criteria with no hierarchy.
- Closing frame: "the poor who must exhibit the stigmata of poverty" -- aid requires the performance of suffering.

### Chapter 3: Compassion Protocol (Legalizing Diseased Undocumented Immigrants)

- Setting: French immigration law, 1990s-2000s. Illness -- "provided it was sufficiently serious and treatment deemed inaccessible" in the home country -- became grounds for legalization.
- The body of the immigrant shifts from productive body (labor power, pre-1974) to pathological body (suffering, post-1997). "Useless to the political economy, it now finds its place in a new moral economy that values suffering over labor and compassion more than rights."
- Three periods: before 1990 (discretionary), 1990-1996 (dispensation -- tolerated but not entitled), 1997 onward (right enshrined in law).
- Medical officers as gatekeepers: wide variation across departments. In 1997-98, the proportion of favorable opinions ranged from 42.7% (Hauts-de-Seine) to 96.7% (Val-de-Marne).
- Three ideal types of medical officer: strict legalism, unconditional liberality, tempered generosity.
- Four figures of truthfulness: conformity (real name + real disease), usurpation (false name + real disease), simulation (real name + exaggerated disease), imposture (false name + false disease).
- Concepts: **compassion protocol** -- by analogy with end-of-life care. A procedure of last resort deriving from sympathy in the face of suffering. "It demands the right to keep alive individuals who have nothing except their mere existence."

### Chapter 4: Truth Ordeal (Attesting Violence for Asylum Seekers)

- Setting: medical certification of torture for asylum seekers in France.
- The asylum seeker's word is no longer sufficient. The body must testify. Medical certificates documenting scars "compatible with the account" have become the proxy for political truth.
- Key paradox: "it is at a moment when the practice of torture is developing toward increasingly invisible forms that a visibility of marks on the body is demanded."
- Support organizations torn: issue certificates (collaborating with a system they critique) or refuse (abandoning individual asylum seekers). COMEDE continued; Primo Levi Association repeatedly announced intention to stop.
- Evolution of certificates: early ones (1987) were long, narrative, included the physician's personal conviction. Later ones (2002+) were standardized, clinical, brief -- "the observations are compatible with the patient's statement." Dehistoricization of refugees.
- The case of Elanchelvan Rajendram: Sri Lankan Tamil whose account, documents, and medical certificate were all rejected. Deported. Shot sixteen times by Sri Lankan army months later. "I don't know what more I can do to make people believe me."

### Chapter 5: Ambivalent Hospitality (Governing the Unwanted)

- LIMEN section. Setting: Sangatte -- the Red Cross center near Calais where migrants gathered before attempting to cross to Britain.
- Sangatte as neither camp nor shelter but something between: "an ultimate haven where the minimalist hospitality related more to humanitarianism than to asylum -- ironically, a refugee camp whose residents were not considered refugees."
- Fassin against Agamben's thesis that the camp is "the fundamental biopolitical paradigm of the West." Posits degrees of exception and differentiations of function -- resists equivalence between Sangatte and Guantanamo.
- After closure: conditions worsened. "The jungle" replaced the center. The humanitarian problem became invisible rather than solved.

### Chapter 6: Massacre of the Innocents (Representing Childhood in the Age of AIDS)

- Setting: South Africa, the AIDS epidemic, early 2000s.
- Three figures of childhood: the suffering child (mother-to-child transmission), the abused child (the "virgin cure" myth), the orphan (future of the nation).
- Nkosi Johnson as icon: the innocent victim whose suffering could be publicly mourned without the moral complexity attached to adult infection. Children "bore the mark of innocence: they were born infected."
- The Treatment Action Campaign poster juxtaposing Hector Petersen (1976, apartheid) with Nkosi Johnson (2001, AIDS) -- collapsing political death and biological death into one frame.
- Concepts: **the economy of suffering** -- how the moral representation of AIDS shifted when children entered the frame, making compassion possible without the complication of blame.

### Chapter 7: Desire for Exception (Managing Disaster Victims)

- Setting: Venezuela, 1999 mudslide disaster ("Tragedia").
- Disaster as political theology -- the state of exception. Hugo Chavez declared it a "tragedy" and mobilized the military. The response revealed class structures: wealthy victims were rescued first; poor victims in shantytowns on hillsides were last.
- Humanitarian exception was used to bypass normal political processes. The disaster became an opportunity for Chavez to consolidate power.

### Chapter 8: Subjectivity without Subjects (Reinventing the Figure of the Witness)

- Setting: humanitarian psychiatry in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
- Three witness figures from philology: *testis* (third-party observer), *superstes* (survivor who has lived through), *martus* (one who testifies through suffering -- martyrdom).
- MSF's "chronicles" from Palestine: nominally clinical accounts of trauma that actually function as political testimony. "What thus emerges repeatedly in the notes of the mental health professionals is a political account of violence rather than a clinical account of trauma."
- Medecins du Monde's attempt at symmetry between Palestinian and Israeli victims -- producing one report on Israeli violations (juridical, factual) and another on Palestinian attacks (psychological, affective). The two reports use fundamentally different registers: facts vs. affects, justice vs. suffering.
- Concepts: **political subjectivation through trauma** -- humanitarian psychiatry tends to produce "a form of subjectivity devoid of historical subject." Palestinians appropriated this subjectivity precisely to demand political status.

### Chapter 9: Hierarchies of Humanity (Intervening in International Conflicts)

- Setting: MSF's Administration Board meeting, March 28, 2003, eight days after the Iraq invasion began.
- The question: should six MSF volunteers remain in Baghdad? The discussion "skirted the most painful truth -- the ontological inequality underlying this transaction in human lives, between those that are imperiled and those that are saved."
- Triple lifeline: sacrificeable lives of local populations vs. freely sacrificed lives of aid workers; sacred lives of Western soldiers vs. sacrificeable lives of local civilians; valued lives of expatriate volunteers vs. devalued lives of local staff.
- Concepts: **politics of life** (distinct from Foucault's biopolitics) -- politics that bring into play "differentiated meanings and values of human lives." Not the technologies of managing populations but the hierarchies of whose life counts.

### Conclusion: Critique of Humanitarian Reason

- Humanitarian reason as political theology: the sacralization of life (Christian genealogy) and the valorization of suffering (passion and redemption, secularized) are the two foundations.
- "Biolegitimacy" -- the recognition of life as the highest value. The paradigm shift: from the volunteer risking life alongside liberation movements to the humanitarian saving lives in pacified spaces. "What Walter Benjamin calls 'life itself,' or maybe more explicitly, the simple fact of being alive."
- The structural inequality: "the very gesture that appears to grant them recognition reduces them to what they are not -- and often refuse to be -- by reifying their condition of victimhood while ignoring their history and muting their words."
- Humanitarian government "saves something of our idea of ourselves" -- it is salvationary for the givers, not only the receivers.
- Final image: two Guinean boys, aged 14 and 15, found dead in a plane's undercarriage, a letter in one's hand addressed to "Your Excellencies, members, and officials of Europe" begging for help with education, health, and an end to war. "Do not forget that it is to you that we must complain about the weakness of our strength in Africa."

## Vocabulary

- **Humanitarian government** -- "the deployment of moral sentiments in contemporary politics"; the set of procedures for managing, regulating, and supporting the existence of precarious lives
- **Humanitarian reason** -- "this morally driven, politically ambiguous, and deeply paradoxical strength of the weak"; the moral sentiment that bridges inequality and solidarity
- **Moral economy** -- the production, distribution, and circulation of moral sentiments, values, and obligations in social space (Fassin's usage, distinct from E.P. Thompson)
- **Precarious lives** -- lives "not guaranteed but bestowed in answer to prayer"; defined not by absolute condition but by relation to those who hold power
- **Compassion protocol** -- by analogy with end-of-life medical protocols; a procedure of last resort that derives from sympathy in the face of suffering; applied specifically to the legalization of sick immigrants
- **Biolegitimacy** -- the recognition of biological life ("the simple fact of being alive") as the supreme value and the ultimate basis for claims on the political community
- **Politics of life** -- politics that bring into play differentiated meanings and values of human lives; distinct from Foucault's biopolitics (which concerns techniques of population management)
- **Politics of pity** -- the affective structure of compassion as political force; always directed from above to below

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Ferguson, *The Anti-Politics Machine* (1990)**: Ferguson showed how development depoliticizes through institutional mechanism; Fassin shows how humanitarianism depoliticizes through moral sentiment. Ferguson's machine works by reframing political problems as technical ones; Fassin's works by reframing political problems as emotional ones. Both identify the same structural move -- the evacuation of politics -- through different mechanisms.

2. **Krause, *The Good Project* (2014)**: Krause showed NGOs as organizations with their own production logic -- "all NGOs are media companies." Fassin provides the moral economy those organizations inhabit. Krause's "fragmentation of reason" is the organizational consequence of what Fassin diagnoses as the dominance of humanitarian reason. Fassin asks what compassion does to politics; Krause asks what the project form does to compassion.

3. **Packard, *A History of Global Health* (2016)**: Packard documents the recurring pattern of magic-bullet interventions in global health. Fassin explains the moral economy that sustains the pattern -- why it persists despite repeated failure. The "indomitable optimism" Benatar names is, in Fassin's terms, the consensual force of humanitarian reason: it makes the intolerable bearable.

4. **Adams, *Metrics* (2016)**: Adams showed how counting reshapes health priorities. Fassin shows how compassion reshapes political priorities. Both identify a mechanism by which the means of governance become ends in themselves -- metrics in Adams, moral sentiments in Fassin. The "rest for living" calculation in Chapter 2 is a micro-instance of Adams's argument about what measurement does.

5. **Tsing, *Friction* (2004)**: Tsing showed that global connections are always mediated by local friction. Fassin's ethnographic chapters are case studies of this friction -- the gap between humanitarian law and its local administration, between the principle of compassion and its bureaucratic implementation. The medical officers in Chapter 3, with their wildly varying rates of favorable opinions, are Tsing's friction made visible.

6. **Benatar, "Moral Imagination" (2005)**: Benatar argued that global health fails from a deficit of moral imagination. Fassin complicates this -- the problem is not insufficient compassion but the wrong kind. Humanitarian reason is precisely moral imagination operationalized, and it still reproduces inequality. The failure is structural, not attitudinal.

## Limits

- France-heavy. Part I is entirely French case studies, and the theoretical framework is built on French political context (the Chevènement Act, the Social Emergency Fund, the Seine-Saint-Denis *département*). The claim to generality rests on Part II's international cases, but these are thinner ethnographically.
- The conclusion's Christian genealogy argument -- that humanitarian reason is secularized political theology -- is provocative but underdeveloped. The claim that the sacralization of life and the valorization of suffering derive from Christian roots doesn't adequately address other traditions of compassion he himself names (Islamic, Confucian, Buddhist).
- No account of humanitarian reason from the perspective of those it governs. The ethnography observes administrators, medical officers, NGO board members -- not the unemployed, the undocumented, the asylum seekers themselves. Their words appear only as written texts submitted for evaluation. Fassin acknowledges this ("the life through which they could, independently, give a meaning to their own existence") but doesn't remedy it.
- The analytical framework treats humanitarianism as primarily a Western phenomenon even as it operates globally. The book doesn't engage with how humanitarian reason functions in non-Western contexts where local actors deploy it for their own purposes -- the kind of appropriation Tsing would insist on.
