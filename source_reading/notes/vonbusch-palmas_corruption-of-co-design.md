## The Move

Participatory design has been corrupted -- not by bad actors but by the structure of participation itself. Von Busch and Palmas argue that the Scandinavian co-design tradition, built on consensus and empathy, is ill-equipped for a world of power, betrayal, and zero-sum games. They introduce political realism (Machiavelli, Mouffe, Geuss) into design theory and propose "Realdesign" -- design practice that takes the recalcitrant, deceptive, and self-interested nature of social life as its starting material rather than wishing it away. The book's real force is in the case studies: participatory budgeting co-opted by the World Bank, microfinance destroying the social capital it was supposed to leverage, empathy deployed as a governance trap. Each chapter names a realist concept -- betrayal, corruption, cunning, hypocrisy -- and shows it operating inside processes designers thought were virtuous.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Notes

### Chapter 1: Introduction -- The Problems of Participatory Design

- **Argument**: Opens with seven design studio scenarios that all go wrong in predictable ways -- avatar worlds become cesspools, noticeboards fill with insults, alternative currencies enable trafficking. The social world "kicks back against designerly intentions." Design has moved from engineering-adjacent modesty to claiming it can "redesign cultures themselves," but lacks a language for power, competing interests, or the properties of social material.
- **Key passage**: "The human material kicks back against designerly intentions in ways that may undermine even the most righteous course of action."
- **Concepts**:
  - **The social as material** -- Koskinen's formulation: design uses "the social as its material." But unlike plywood or Styrofoam, this material is recalcitrant, self-interested, and fights back.
  - **Scandinavians mugged by reality** -- The 2001 Gothenburg protests as founding trauma. A bullet fired at a protester a block from the School of Design. The "consensual spirit" of Swedish participatory democracy shattered. The irony: the protest tactics of 2001 (Reclaim the Streets, guerilla gardening) are now standard planning tools. "Paris '68 slogans today sound like copy from the policy dossiers of planning authorities."
  - **The exodus that looped back** -- The post-2000 political strategy of building parallel institutions (hackerspaces, commons, peer-to-peer) has been recuperated by corporate hackathons and government crowdsourcing. "The exodus path seems to have meandered its way back into the status quo."

### Chapter 2: The Realist Challenge -- Power and Possibilities

- **Argument**: Design thought is structurally idealist -- it starts from intentions, assumes the world is malleable, and believes in progress. Political realism starts from outcomes, assumes self-interest, and fears regress. The chapter introduces Machiavelli not as a cynic but as a republican who understood that "the democratic order rests on a clear understanding of how power really operates." Four questions challenge design's idealism: Is change possible? How do we know "preferred" is better? Preferred by whom? Can design be universal?
- **Key passage**: "Realpolitik does not move in a foggy future but in the present's field of vision. It does not consider its task to consist in the realization of ideas, but in the attainment of concrete ends." (von Rochau)
- **Key passage**: "To think politically is to think about agency, power, and interests, and the relations between these." (Geuss)
- **Concepts**:
  - **Idealism vs. realism in design** -- Idealists privilege intentions and consensus; realists privilege outcomes and conflict. Simon's "changing existing situations into preferred ones" is the idealist charter. Machiavelli's virtú/fortuna/corruzione triad is the realist alternative.
  - **"Preferred by whom?"** -- Geuss unpacks Lenin's question "Who whom?" into "Who does what to whom for whose benefit?" Design's "form follows function" hides this: who decides what function is proper, and in whose interests?
  - **Real realpolitik** -- Von Rochau's original term (1853) was not Kissinger's unscrupulous power play but a practical tool for liberals disillusioned by failed revolution. "An enemy of all kinds of self-delusion." The four diagnostic questions: Who holds power? What economic conditions underlie the political system? What is the prevailing cultural context? What room is there for action?
  - **Fisher's reflexive impotence** -- Capitalist realism creates participants who "know things are bad, but more than that, they know they can't do anything about it." This is not passive observation but a self-fulfilling prophecy -- and it operates inside participatory design just as well.

### Chapter 3: Betrayal -- Post-Political Participation

- **Argument**: The authors participated in a Gothenburg citizen dialogue about harbor redevelopment. Their team proposed experimental governance structures, alternative currencies, citizen-run zones. What came through the process: "a swamp." The chapter uses actor-network theory to show how co-design protocols -- Post-it brainstorming, poster visualization, PowerPoint time slots, planning-office categories -- systematically filter out political content. The "Quisling" in the process is not a person but the protocol itself.
- **Key passage**: "All representations are betrayals of the thing represented."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Agonistic vs. post-political design** -- Mouffe's agonism (design should cultivate dissensus) is flipped: the real question is how co-design suppresses dissensus. "Participation is invariably mediated by power."
  - **Translation as betrayal** -- From ANT: every representation of citizens' ideas involves a "drift, a slippage, a displacement." Goals get translated through successive enrollments until original intentions are unrecognizable. This is not conspiracy but process.
  - **Protocol as agent** -- Galloway's definition: "a technique for achieving voluntary regulation within a contingent environment." The brainstorming rule to "defer judgment" filters out warnings. The poster foregrounds the visualizable and sidelines governance proposals. The planning office can only process "views directly applicable on a development plan." The protocol is the trap.
  - **Collaborateurs** -- Participants in co-design processes as wartime collaborators -- not the arch-traitor kind but the continuum from tactical collaboration to unwitting complicity. The designers became collaborateurs through the design of the process itself.

### Chapter 4: Corruption -- Design and Decay

- **Argument**: Two case studies of social innovations that decayed: participatory budgeting (PB) and microfinance. PB went from Porto Alegre's radical democratic experiment to a World Bank governance tool. Microfinance went from Yunus's Grameen Bank to interest rates of 120% and women used as social collateral. Both demonstrate Machiavelli's corruzione -- the tendency of institutions to corrupt over time -- and the failure of designers to prepare for corrosive environments.
- **Key passage**: "Superior 'alternative' designs are no doubt necessary but they are certainly far from sufficient in this context." (Peck & Theodore)
- **Concepts**:
  - **Mantenere lo stato** -- Machiavelli's overriding concern: maintain the state of things. Design is trained to pursue "preferred situations" but not to prevent making things worse. "While design interventions must always strive for a better world, designers must equally be careful not to recklessly leave the world in a worse situation than they found it."
  - **Innovation as destabilizer** -- Pre-Schumpeterian, innovation had negative connotations: "excessive novelty, without purpose or end." For Machiavelli, innovation serves to stabilize, not revolutionize. The innovating prince "may reintroduce the perilousness of fortuna."
  - **Anti-social innovation** -- Microfinance's leveraging of "social capital" as collateral corrodes the social bonds it exploits. Women are targeted because they "feel shame more quickly and consider non-repayment as a betrayal of family honour." The social means produce anti-social ends.
  - **Anti-political innovation** -- Microfinance fragments responsibility onto individuals, closing down the space for structural critique. "By rejecting the notion that poverty has structural causes, they deny the need for collective responses."
  - **Anti-inventive innovation** -- The spread of microfinance blocks alternative economic arrangements. Innovation paradoxically cements the old.

### Chapter 5: Cunning -- Metis and Designerly Statecraft

- **Argument**: Design has always been about laying traps, but Plato banished this knowledge (metis -- cunning intelligence) from the political lexicon. The chapter traces how "design for government" deploys empathy as a governance tool: service designers probe grandma's "real needs" to determine if her hearing aid right can be withdrawn. De Certeau's "tactics of the weak" have been captured by the strong.
- **Key passage**: "Empathy is a trap." -- Designerly empathy deployed as a governance technique for probing citizens' desires, shifting the scale from Rights to Effects, bypassing political contestation.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Metis** -- Greek cunning intelligence, neither techne (skill) nor poiesis (making) but trap-laying. "A complex but very coherent body of mental attitudes and intellectual behaviour which combine flair, wisdom, forethought, subtlety of mind, deception, resourcefulness, vigilance, opportunism." (Detienne & Vernant) Plato expelled it from the republic because it was amoral -- useful for means, useless for judging ends.
  - **Citizens with rights → users with needs** -- The fundamental transformation when design enters government. Rights are non-negotiable political achievements. "Needs" are designerly objects to be investigated, prioritized, and potentially denied.
  - **Tactical urbanism captured** -- De Certeau's tactics-vs-strategy schema has imploded. Planning authorities now use "tactical" methods. The Gothenburg sauna built by Raumlabor operates through "trials and prototyping" that circumvent formal planning -- but in the service of development interests, not citizens.
  - **Neuroliberal government** -- Design-informed governance as a new mode of cybernetic control, using affective proximity to map and modulate citizen behavior.

### Chapter 6: Hypocrisy -- Of Virtue and Vice

- **Argument**: Can a realist designer avoid cynicism? The chapter traces how good intentions and empathy became design's self-image through Papanek's Cold War straddling, then examines hypocrisy through Shklar and Runciman. The Robert Moses parkway case demonstrates that "artifacts have politics" operates through hypocrisy, not transparency. Some hypocrisy may be necessary -- Hobbes and Bentham suggest that the alternative (total sincerity in politics) is worse.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Papanek's Cold War position** -- His "real world" design was simultaneously a humanitarian turn and "yet another theater of conflict in the cold war." Design's moral vocabulary emerged from geopolitical positioning, not pure idealism.
  - **Productive hypocrisy** -- Runciman's argument that some political hypocrisy is functional: the alternative (leaders who say exactly what they think) is Trump-style "authenticity" that is worse. The realist designer may need to maintain some distance between stated values and operational practice.

### Chapter 7: Closing Propositions -- After Empathy, Realdesign

- **Argument**: Sketches "Realdesign" -- a realism-informed design practice. Not a return to cynicism but a "utopian realism" (Geuss via Carr) that is context-bound, value-judging, and power-aware. The aim: find a realism not bogged down with impossibilities, find a utopianism that is not moralist, reclaim the creative element inherent in realism. Von Rochau's four questions become the designer's diagnostic framework.
- **Concepts**:
  - **Realdesign** -- Design practice that starts from outcomes rather than intentions, from power rather than empathy, from the particular rather than the universal. Not anti-design but design with its eyes open.
  - **Utopian realism** -- Geuss's formulation: "wishful visions" rather than "wish-dreams." Context-bound imagination that doesn't deny the potency of power.

## Vocabulary

- **Virtú** -- Machiavelli's quality of the effective prince: not moral virtue but the capacity to counter fortune and corruption. "Alert, on the make; it is not a habit."
- **Corruzione** -- The general tendency of institutions to decay over time. Not inevitable but requiring active resistance.
- **Fortuna** -- The element of chance in political life. Innovation may reintroduce it.
- **Mantenere lo stato** -- Maintain the state of things. The realist's first concern.
- **Metis** -- Greek cunning intelligence. Trap-laying, deviousness, resourcefulness. Expelled from Plato's lexicon, reintroduced by de Certeau as "art of the weak" but now captured by the strong.
- **Post-political** -- Mouffe/Zizek/Ranciere: a condition where participation is offered but contestation is foreclosed. Democracy is not repressed but "foreclosed."
- **Reflexive impotence** -- Fisher: knowing things are bad while knowing you can't change them. A self-fulfilling prophecy operating inside participatory processes.
- **Protocol** -- Galloway: "a technique for achieving voluntary regulation within a contingent environment." The material formats of co-design (Post-its, posters, PowerPoints) that sort voice from noise.
- **Realdesign** -- The book's proposed alternative: realism-informed design practice that takes power, betrayal, corruption, and cunning as design material rather than pretending they don't exist.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Alexander's pattern language** -- Alexander assumed participatory design reveals a shared "quality without a name." Von Busch and Palmas argue that participation can be captured, corrupted, weaponized. The tension is productive: both are on Lee's shelf. Alexander is utopian about process; von Busch/Palmas are realist.

2. **Ferguson, *The Anti-Politics Machine*** -- Ferguson showed how a development project in Lesotho depoliticized. Von Busch and Palmas show how co-design depoliticizes at the micro level -- through protocol, not ideology. The Dialogue Process and the Thaba-Tseka project produce the same structural outcome (political proposals treated as noise) through different mechanisms.

3. **Krause, *The Good Project*** -- Krause showed NGOs as organizations with their own logic. Von Busch and Palmas show design studios and planning offices the same way: organizational protocols that fragment the reasoning they claim to serve. The World Bank's adoption of participatory budgeting is Krause's "good project" at institutional scale.

4. **Franklin, holistic vs. prescriptive technologies** -- Franklin's prescriptive technology (where the process controls the worker) maps onto co-design protocols that control what participants can say. The brainstorming rule, the visualization requirement, the planning categories -- all prescriptive in Franklin's sense.

5. **Tsing, *Friction*** -- Tsing's "zones of awkward engagement" describe what happens when global models (PB, microfinance) meet local conditions. Von Busch and Palmas show the same friction but from the design side: the traveling design that loses integrity as it moves through corrosive environments.

6. **Adams, *Metrics*** -- Adams showed how counting reshapes health priorities. Von Busch and Palmas show how design protocols reshape what counts as a "vision" -- the visualizable displaces the conceptual, the plannable displaces the political. Same mechanism, different domain.

## Limits

- The book is stronger on diagnosis than prescription. "Realdesign" gets one chapter against six of critique. The four diagnostic questions from von Rochau are useful but thin -- no worked examples of Realdesign in practice, no cases where the realist approach produced better outcomes than the idealist one.
- The theoretical apparatus is heavy for the design audience it addresses. Machiavelli, Mouffe, Geuss, ANT, Ranciere, Fisher, de Certeau, Foucault, Hobbes, Shklar -- the density risks losing practitioners who need the argument most.
- The microfinance critique, while devastating, is not new. The development studies literature had already arrived at these conclusions (Bateman, Sinclair, etc.). The contribution is bringing it into design theory, not the critique itself.
- Nothing here about digital participatory design, which is where co-design has moved most dramatically since 2020. The examples are physical (urban planning, saunas, posters) while the most aggressive co-optation is happening in platform-mediated participation.
