## The Move

Chapin argues that elaborate note-taking systems destroy the thing they claim to serve -- the natural salience filter that determines what's alive in your thinking. The essay isn't anti-notes. It's anti-system: the moment note-taking becomes its own activity, it replaces organic synthesis with curation theater. The real punchline is that originality comes from "read/write errors" -- the fragmentary, distorted versions of ideas that survive in memory -- not from accurate retrieval.

## Notes (the essay is a numbered list, not sections)

### The salience filter argument

- **Argument**: Your natural filter for what sticks is already the right filter. Systematic note-taking substitutes external criteria ("seems like it should be interesting") for organic salience. This is a tradeoff, and usually a bad one.
- **Key passage**: "Your natural salience filter is a great determinant of what's most alive to you. If you begin to rely on any other filter, you will increasingly record what *seems like it should be interesting according to some preexisting criteria* rather than what organically sticks to your mind."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Natural salience filter** -- What your mind keeps without effort. Not random; it reflects what matters to your actual thinking. External capture systems override it with borrowed criteria.
  - **"Seems like it should be interesting"** -- The tell. When you're filing notes because they fit a category rather than because you can't stop thinking about them, the system is running you.

### The avoidance argument

- **Argument**: Knowledge management is a high-class form of procrastination. It convinces you that unpreparedness is the barrier to creative work, when in fact there is no future version of you that will be perfectly prepared.
- **Key passage**: "If you believe you're *unprepared,* know that you will never transmute into the perfectly prepared person that you think exists in the future. Unfortunately, you have to start with the person currently in this chair."
- **Concepts**:
  - **System-as-avoidance** -- Organizing notes feels productive. It is not producing. The system becomes a buffer between you and the threatening act of making something.
  - **The person in this chair** -- The only writer who will ever write your work is the one sitting here now, unready. No amount of systematic preparation creates a different person.

### The originality argument

- **Argument**: The best writing synthesizes "the previously unarticulated in the moment" rather than retrieving stored material. The distortions that memory introduces -- "read/write errors" -- are what we call originality.
- **Key passage**: "If it's some stunted, fragmentary version of an idea you were exposed to previously, that is good. These read/write errors are what we call *originality.*"
- **Concepts**:
  - **Read/write errors as originality** -- The core move. Memory is lossy, and the losses are productive. What survives distortion in your head is different from what you filed in your database -- and the difference is where original thought lives.
  - **"Presentation of factual insight" as limitation** -- Chapin names a specific trap: assuming that the core of your work is the accurate presentation of facts you've collected, rather than the synthesis that happens when you write from what you carry.

### The LARPing distinction

- **Argument**: Legitimate systematic note-taking exists -- when you're tracking "a million references" for a specific research task. If the system isn't adapted to a specific context of use, "you're LARPing."
- **Key passage**: "If your note-taking system is adapted to a specific context of use such as this, then you're working. If it's not, then you're LARPing."
- **Concepts**:
  - **Working vs. LARPing** -- The test is whether the system serves a specific task or exists for its own sake. A literature review database is working. A second brain with no output is cosplay.
  - **The da Vinci standard** -- "Leonardo da Vinci kept all of his notes in one big book. If he liked something he put it down. This is known as a *commonplace book,* and it is about how detailed your note-taking system should be unless you plan on thinking more elaborately than Leonardo da Vinci."

### The aesthetic warning

- **Key passage**: "Shun the useless adoption of the aesthetic of the useful. When something can be like work or like play, never make it work."
- **Concept**:
  - **Aesthetic of the useful** -- The visual and procedural trappings of productive work (databases, tags, links, reviews) adopted as an end in themselves. The system looks like scholarship; the output is the system.

## Vocabulary

- **Natural salience filter** -- What sticks in your mind without effort. Chapin's argument that this is the only filter worth trusting for creative work.
- **Read/write errors** -- Memory's distortions of what you've read or experienced. Chapin's reframe: these are originality, not information loss.
- **LARPing** -- Live-action role-playing. Here: maintaining a knowledge system without producing anything from it. The system is the performance, not the preparation.
- **Commonplace book** -- A single undifferentiated notebook. Da Vinci's method. Chapin's ceiling for how complex your notes should be.
- **Aesthetic of the useful** -- Adopting the appearance of productive work as a substitute for the work itself.

## Cross-Shelf Connections

1. **Mills' "On Intellectual Craftsmanship"** -- Mills kept a single file (his "intellectual journal") and advocated for exactly the kind of low-system, high-use note-keeping Chapin describes. Mills: the file should be a tool for thinking, not an end in itself. The difference: Mills assumed the thinker would *do something* with the file. Chapin is writing for a generation that confuses the file with the doing.

2. **Polanyi's tacit dimension** -- Chapin's "natural salience filter" is tacit knowledge applied to reading. What sticks is what your embodied understanding found important, even if you can't articulate why. Polanyi: "we know more than we can tell." Chapin: we remember more usefully than we record.

3. **Berry's refusal logic** -- Berry refuses a computer; Chapin refuses a note-taking system. Both are making the same structural argument: the new tool promises capability but displaces something that already works (Berry: a human collaborator; Chapin: organic synthesis). Both locate the problem in the compulsion to adopt, not in the technology per se.

4. **Perry's "Tendrils of Mess"** -- A second brain is a highly ordered system that projects strong tendrils of implied order onto everything around it. When reality (your actual thinking) doesn't match the system's categories, the mismatch registers as mess -- not because your thinking is disordered but because the system's demands are too rigid. Perry's framework explains why elaborate note systems eventually feel oppressive.

## Limits

- Chapin writes for writers, not researchers. The distinction between "working" and "LARPing" is real, but he dispenses with the research case in one sentence ("I will also give a pass..."). Someone managing 144 papers across six arms needs more than a commonplace book, and the essay has nothing to say about where the line is.
- No engagement with the possibility that note-taking systems change how you think, not just what you record. The tools-shape-users argument (Illich, Franklin, Winner) would complicate Chapin's clean distinction between natural salience and system-imposed salience -- what if the system alters the filter itself?
- "I am waiting for any evidence that our most provocative thinkers and writers are those who rely on elaborate, systematic note-taking systems" -- this is rhetoric, not evidence. Luhmann's Zettelkasten produced 70+ books. The claim is fun but doesn't survive one counterexample.
