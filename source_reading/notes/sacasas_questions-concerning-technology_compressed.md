## L.M. Sacasas -- "The Questions Concerning Technology"

### The move

Reframes technology ethics from preventing misuse to examining normal use. 41 questions that treat every technology as morally active -- not neutral until corrupted. The correction to Om Malik's proposal to "add" ethics to tech: the moral dimension is already there, embedded in design. The title nods at Heidegger; the method is Illich; the newsletter is named for Illich's key term. The questions were drafted in 2016 and republished without revision six years later, which either means they were ahead of their time or too general to be affected by specifics.

### Passages that stuck

1. "I took the opportunity to suggest that we needn't add an emotional and moral dimension to tech, it was already there. The only question was as to its nature." [structural -- the correction that anchors the whole project: recognition, not addition]

2. "What sort of person will the use of this technology make of me?" [Q1 -- the formation question, and the essay's center of gravity]

3. "What was required of other human beings so that I might be able to use this technology?" [Q11 -- Berry's direct-dependence chain as a question]

4. "Does my use of this technology make it easier to live as if I had no responsibilities toward my neighbor?" [Q40 -- the question about moral distance]

5. "Can I be held responsible for the actions which this technology empowers? Would I feel better if I couldn't?" [Q41 -- the sharpest question. Names the desire to be unaccountable as a feature, not a bug.]

### Where it connects

1. **Winner, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?"** -- Sacasas explicitly extends Winner. Winner: artifacts have politics. Sacasas: artifacts have ethics. The 41 questions operationalize the insight -- if every technology carries moral properties, here's how to surface them.

2. **Berry's nine standards** -- Same project, different register. Berry is declarative (the tool *should* be cheaper, repairable, non-displacing); Sacasas is interrogative (what does it cost, who repairs it, what does it displace?). Berry's Standard 9 maps directly to Sacasas' Q7-8 (what practices cultivated, what displaced?).

3. **Franklin's holistic/prescriptive** -- The formation questions (Q1-2, Q7-8) are asking whether a technology is holistic or prescriptive. Holistic: develops the user's skill. Prescriptive: develops the system at the user's expense. "What sort of person will this make of me?" is Franklin's question stated personally.

4. **Illich** -- The newsletter is *The Convivial Society*. Sacasas' project is Illich updated for networked technologies. Illich's test: does the tool serve human-scale agency or institutional control? The 41 questions are 41 ways of asking that.

5. **McLuhan** -- Q3-4 (time, place) and Q9-10 (notice, ignore) are McLuhan's insight in diagnostic form. The medium reshapes perception before you choose what to do with it.

Where Berry provides the declarative standards and Franklin provides the analytical framework, Sacasas provides the diagnostic questions -- the intake form. Q11-13 (what was required of others, of creatures, of the earth) are directly usable in consulting: apply them to any program that claims to "empower" without naming costs. Q41 (would I feel better if I couldn't be held responsible?) is the question that uncovers what automation and institutional layering actually promise: moral distance.

### What it does NOT say

All 41 questions address the individual user. No questions about collective refusal, regulation, or institutional redesign -- the Illichian inheritance locates moral action in the person, not the system. No prioritization: all questions carry equal weight, which risks either paralysis or pick-what-suits-you. And the essay republished 2016 questions in 2021 without visible revision -- six years of fairness/accountability/transparency discourse didn't change the list.
