## Venkat Rao -- "How to Draw and Judge Quadrant Diagrams" (2009)

### The move

Identifies the precise conditions under which a 2x2 matrix earns its keep: high ambiguity, but dominant patterns visible. Too structured for narrative, too fuzzy for statistics. The quadrant diagram is a tool for the boundary zone between formal analysis and artistic judgment. Rao grades three examples (C-, B+, A) and the grades track how honestly each diagram owns its position on that boundary -- the worst one pretends to be analytical; the best one embraces the artistic choice.

### Passages that stuck

1. "Visual constructs live in a special sweet spot inhabited by issues that are too complex for rigorous analysis, and too structured or impoverished to support full-blown narrative treatments in the form of novels or stories." [epistemological -- locates the diagram in the gap between math and storytelling]

2. "Unlike 'High-high,' the phrase 'Master and commander' picks out a *prototypical* example of being in the state of high perspective and high control. It at once suggests more implied complexity than the long feature lists in the Loehr/Schwarz diagram, while retaining coherence." [craft -- why pointing beats listing]

3. "The choices made here are essentially artistic ones, not statistical 'key indicators' or first-principles self-evident concepts." [on the Microsoft scenarios' axis selection -- diagnostic -- the honest diagram admits its axes are chosen, not derived]

4. "The point isn't to be cute. It is to suggest a pattern of complexity, and a calibration/benchmark point, without a hopeless attempt to *list* all the attributes that create the complexity." [prescriptive -- what naming does when done right]

5. "In the right hands, it defuses polarizations, reframes arguments, separates out coherent alternatives and makes everybody's life a lot easier. In the wrong hands, it produces amusement, supplies fodder for Dilbert jokes, and gives mediocre consultants a picturesque path for their descent into madness." [tonal -- the Rao voice: dry, knowing, precise about the distance between competence and farce]

### Where it connects

- **Rao's "Legibility" essay**: Legibility warns against simplifying complex realities. This essay asks when simplification into a 2x2 is justified. Together they define the narrow zone where pattern-compression is productive -- the quadrant works only when formal tools can't and the material has enough structure that narrative alone would waste it.
- **Flyvbjerg's phronesis**: The "right-brained statistics and first-principles guesswork" Rao describes is phronesis applied to visual rhetoric -- practical judgment about particular situations, irreducible to technique. The quadrant-maker's skill is exactly the knowledge Flyvbjerg says social science should value but doesn't.
- **Polanyi's tacit dimension**: Evocative names work because they activate tacit knowledge the audience already has. "Hurricane Katrina" does more than a feature list because it points at what "we know more than we can tell." The essay is an argument for compressed reference over exhaustive description.
- **Law's *After Method***: The best quadrant (Microsoft) gets an A because it stops pretending to analytical rigor and owns the aesthetic judgment. Law's argument that honest method means admitting the mess rather than hiding it behind procedure.

### What it does NOT say

Doesn't address power. Who draws the quadrant controls the frame -- Microsoft's "best" diagram happens to organize the future around Microsoft's strategic concerns. The essay evaluates craft without asking whose interests the craft serves. Doesn't provide a way to *acquire* the taste it describes -- if you already recognize good quadrant-chemistry, the essay sharpens your vocabulary; if you don't, the grades are assertions. The self-referential move (a meta-quadrant for when to use quadrants) is clever but doesn't survive its own evaluation criteria.
