# CEV Map – Lee Wilkers' Intellectual Identity

> This document maps Lee's intellectual identity across 10 dimensions, derived from his shelf and stream on leewilkers.com. A model reading this cold should be able to extrapolate Lee's taste, judgment, and voice well enough to write in the right register.
>
> The shelf is 46 items across 4 lanes: craft, perception, technology, intervention. The stream is 34 items (18 sites, 16 essays). Together they form a compressed map of how Lee thinks.

---

## 1. TEXTUAL – The Source Material

Lee gravitates toward prose that is **rigorous but readable, concrete but not simplistic, opinionated but earned**. The authors on his shelf share a quality: they write about abstract ideas through specific, material examples. Mumford explains modernity through the clock. Berger explains power through oil painting. Mattern explains politics through repair manuals.

The essays are more than arguments – they are **demonstrations**. Victor's essays are interactive. Sloan's "Fish" is a tap essay. Chimero's "The Web's Grain" treats the web as a material with properties. The form enacts the thesis.

**What to absorb**: Sentences that do work. No warming up, no throat-clearing. Mills' appendix "On Intellectual Craftsmanship" is the methodological anchor – the practice of thinking as a manual activity. Polanyi's "we know more than we can tell" is the epistemological anchor. Berry's "Why I Am Not Going to Buy a Computer" is the moral anchor.

**Signal for models**: When writing for Lee, lead with the concrete. Name the object, the mechanism, the example – then draw the principle from it. Never the reverse.

## 2. CURATORIAL – Lee's Act of Choosing

The shelf is not a reading list. It is a **territory claim**. Each item is there because it does something for Lee's thinking, not because it's important or canonical.

### The hover notes as a corpus

The 42 hover notes (4 items lack them) operate in a specific register: **dry, knowing, insider**. They are not thesis summaries. They are margin notes from someone who has already read the book and is signaling to another person who might.

**Four moves the hover notes make:**

1. **Author's own phrase**: "we know more than we can tell" (Polanyi), "seeing comes before words" (Berger), "zones of awkward engagement" (Tsing). The author said something memorable; the note quotes it.

2. **The concept that sticks**: "shearing layers" (Brand), "active inertia" (Sull), "flocking, forces, fractals" (Shiffman). Not the thesis – the image or framework that survived reading.

3. **Insider positioning**: "blogosphere-era take on Seeing Like a State" (Rao), "book-length Hail the Maintainers" (Vinsel & Russell), "not a typo" (McLuhan/Fiore). These assume the reader already knows the territory. They locate, not explain.

4. **Lee's own reframe**: "all NGOs are media companies" (Krause), "exactly what it sounds like" (Berry), "Realpolitik for designers" (von Busch & Palmås). These are Lee's voice – provocative compressions that reframe the book's argument.

**What gets killed**: Thesis summaries ("design with the web, not against it"), clever constructions ("the McLuhan you can actually read"), and words like "infrastructure" (flagged as a claude-ism). Round 2 of hover note workshopping proposed 17 notes; ALL were rejected. Round 3 succeeded because it researched the authors' actual words first.

**Signal for models**: When describing anything on Lee's shelf, use the hover note register. Don't summarize; position. Assume the reader is smart. If you can't find the right phrase, leave it bare.

### Lane assignments as taxonomy

The four lanes – **craft, perception, technology, intervention** – are Lee's taxonomy of knowledge. They map roughly to:

- **Craft**: How things are made. Tools, materials, process, the hand. (Illich, Victor, Alexander, Sennett, Korn, Schwarz)
- **Perception**: How things are seen. Method, epistemology, the eye. (Mills, Flyvbjerg, Berger, Law, McLuhan)
- **Technology**: What things do to us. Systems, maintenance, critique. (Franklin, Winner, Mattern, Berry, Beer)
- **Intervention**: What happens when you try to help. Development, health, power. (Ferguson, Tsing, Fassin, Packard, Adams, Krause)

These are not academic categories. "Craft" includes woodworking (Schwarz) next to interaction design (Victor). "Intervention" includes organizational theory (Sull) next to ethnography (Tsing). The lanes are **Lee's way of seeing**, not a conventional classification.

**Cross-lane items**: Polanyi (craft + perception), McLuhan (craft + perception), Illich (craft + technology). Some books resist a single lane. The interesting ones live on the borders.

### What ordering reveals

Within each lane, order reflects priority – but not a strict ranking. The first few items in each lane are the **anchors**: Illich for craft, Franklin for technology, Flyvbjerg for perception. Later items are important but less foundational. Order 30+ items (Shiffman, Berry's Unsettling, Pinney/Peterson) are there for completeness or specificity.

## 3. RELATIONAL – The Intellectual Conversation

The shelf is not 46 isolated items. It is a network of conversations, responses, and tensions.

### Major conversations

**The maintenance conversation**: Russell & Vinsel's "Hail the Maintainers" (2016) is the manifesto. Mattern's "Maintenance and Care" (2018) broadens it from infrastructure to care work. Mattern's "Step by Step" (2024) narrows it to repair manuals. Vinsel & Russell's "Innovation Delusion" (2020) is the book-length treatment. But the roots go deeper: Franklin's "Real World of Technology" (1989) distinguished holistic from prescriptive technologies. Illich's "Tools for Conviviality" (1973) is the ur-text. Winner's "Whale and the Reactor" (1986, in the stream) bridges them.

**The seeing conversation**: Berger's "Ways of Seeing" (1972) is about how images condition perception. McLuhan's "Understanding Media" (1964) is about how media condition perception. Pinney's "Photography's Other Histories" (2003) is about whose perception gets centered. Flyvbjerg's "Making Social Science Matter" (2001) is about what counts as valid perception in research. These don't cite each other much, but they share a question: who sees what, and why?

**The making conversation**: Polanyi's "Tacit Dimension" (1966) says we know more than we can tell. Sennett's "Craftsman" (2008) says the hand teaches the head. Ingold's "Making" (2013) says making is a way of knowing. Korn's "Why We Make Things" (2013) says the workshop doesn't tolerate bullshit. The through-line: embodied knowledge is real knowledge, and it can't be fully articulated.

**The development critique**: Ferguson's "Anti-Politics Machine" (1990) showed how development projects depoliticize. Tsing's "Friction" (2004) showed how global connections are always messy. Packard's "History of Global Health" (2016) showed the recurring pattern of magic bullets. Adams' "Metrics" (2016) showed how counting reshapes health. Fassin's "Humanitarian Reason" (2011) showed the moral economy of compassion. Krause's "Good Project" (2014) showed how NGO logic fragments reason. This is Lee's professional world – the conversation he works inside.

### Key tensions

- **Alexander vs. von Busch/Palmås**: Alexander's pattern language assumes participatory design is inherently good. Von Busch and Palmås argue that co-design has been corrupted – participation can be co-opted. Both are on the shelf. The tension is productive.

- **Berry vs. the web people**: Berry refuses to buy a computer; Victor, Mod, Chimero, Sloan are web-native. Lee holds both. The resolution: Berry's *values* (care, locality, appropriate tools) apply even inside digital practice.

- **Flyvbjerg vs. Law**: Flyvbjerg wants social science to matter by doing phronesis (practical wisdom). Law says social science is inherently messy and methods can't clean it up. Both are right, at different scales.

## 4. TONAL – The Shared Frequency

Despite spanning disciplines (woodworking, cybernetics, public health, web design), the shelf authors share a tonal register:

**Anti-academic but rigorous**. These authors know the literature but don't write for journals. Berger was an art critic, not an art historian. Mills wrote against sociology's professionalization. Victor dropped out of academia entirely. Even the academic books (Flyvbjerg, Tsing, Ferguson) are written to be read, not just cited.

**Concrete over abstract**. Mumford explains the history of technology through specific machines. Mattern explains maintenance through specific objects (road surfaces, manuals, public records). Ferguson explains development through one project in Lesotho. The arguments are grounded in things you can see and touch.

**Historical without nostalgia**. Brand, Mumford, Berry, and Franklin all look backward – but not to idealize. Brand's "How Buildings Learn" argues buildings get better over time through use. Berry's argument for agriculture isn't about going back; it's about what's been lost and what could be recovered. The posture is: the past has things to teach, not to copy.

**Skeptical of progress narratives**. Innovation is overvalued (Russell & Vinsel). Development is depoliticization (Ferguson). Metrics distort what they measure (Adams). Technology is not neutral (Winner, Franklin). The shelf is deeply suspicious of "moving forward" as a default good.

**Signal for models**: When writing in Lee's register, adopt this frequency. No boosterism. No "exciting possibilities." Ground claims in examples. Qualify where necessary but don't hedge into meaninglessness. The tone is: serious, observant, unimpressed by fashion.

## 5. POSITIONAL – The Negative Space

What the shelf excludes is as informative as what it includes.

**No pure philosophy**. Despite obvious affinity with Heidegger (technology critique), Dewey (pragmatism), or Wittgenstein (tacit knowledge), no philosophers appear. The shelf prefers thinkers who work through cases, not concepts.

**No Silicon Valley**. No Andreessen, Thiel, Altman, or "build" ideology. No startup memoirs. No "how we scaled." Victor and Mod work in technology but from positions of critique and craft, not industry.

**No self-help or productivity**. No "Deep Work," "Atomic Habits," "Getting Things Done." The shelf's relationship to work is through craft (Sennett, Korn, Schwarz), not optimization.

**No conventional management theory**. The one HBR piece (Sull) is about why management fails. No "Good to Great," no McKinsey frameworks, no OKRs.

**No AI/ML canon**. Despite Lee working with AI tools daily, no Bostrom, no Russell (Stuart), no Tegmark, no "alignment" literature. The omission is conspicuous and deliberate.

**Minimal fiction**. Only Labatut, which is barely fiction – it's a novelistic treatment of real scientific history. No novels, no poetry, no short stories. This may evolve as the stream grows.

**No critical theory / continental theory**. No Foucault, no Deleuze, no Butler, no Latour (despite ANT's relevance to many shelf items). The shelf draws on these traditions indirectly (through Berger, through Tsing, through Law) but doesn't include the theorists themselves.

**Signal for models**: These absences define a register. Don't channel startup optimism, productivity culture, pure abstraction, or continental theory jargon when working with Lee. The shelf says: I read widely but I trust people who work through examples, not systems.

## 6. PRACTICAL – How Lee Uses These

Lee is a qualitative researcher and ethics consultant in global health. The shelf items serve different roles in his practice:

**Consulting frames** (intervention lane): Fassin's "politics of pity," Krause's "all NGOs are media companies," Adams' "what counting does to health" – these are lenses Lee applies to real organizational situations. Ferguson's "depoliticization" diagnosis is a recurring frame.

**Argument anchors** (perception lane): Mills' "sociological imagination" (personal troubles as public issues), Flyvbjerg's phronesis, Franklin's holistic/prescriptive distinction – these are the load-bearing concepts Lee builds on in professional writing and analysis.

**Tool-thinking** (craft lane): Victor's information design, Alexander's pattern language, Norman's affordances – these inform how Lee thinks about interfaces, systems, and designed objects. Not tools he uses directly, but tools for thinking about tools.

**Identity markers** (cross-lane): Berry's agrarian ethics, Illich's conviviality, Brand's shearing layers – these are less about professional practice and more about intellectual identity. They signal values: care, locality, learning through use, skepticism of scale.

**Signal for models**: When Lee is writing about global health, intervention-lane references carry weight. When he's building something (a website, a system), craft-lane references apply. When he's analyzing a problem, perception-lane references anchor the method. Don't mix registers without reason.

## 7. TEMPORAL – The Chronological Arc

The shelf spans 90 years: Mumford (1934) to Shiffman (2024) and Mattern (2024).

**The oldest items are the most foundational**. Mumford (1934), Mills (1959), McLuhan (1964), Polanyi (1966) – these aren't historical curiosities. They're anchors that the newer work builds on. Mumford's "the clock, not the steam engine" is still the most concise statement of technology's social shaping.

**The 1970s cluster is the ethical core**: Illich (1973), Beer (1973), Berger (1972), Berry (1977). This is when the technology-critique tradition crystallized. Franklin (1989) and Winner (1986) are the late additions.

**The 2000s–2020s wave is the "new maintenance"**: Russell & Vinsel (2016), Mattern (2018, 2024), Vinsel & Russell (2020). This is the most intellectually active thread – new work is still being published.

**What persists**: The ideas that hold across the full 90-year arc – that tools shape their users, that maintenance matters more than innovation, that seeing is politically conditioned, that development has unintended consequences – these are not trendy. They're structural.

## 9. AESTHETIC – Form as Argument

Several shelf and stream items are interesting not just for what they argue but for **how they exist as objects**:

- **Craig Mod's website** (craigmod.com) is itself an argument about publishing. The design, the typography, the care in presentation – it practices what it preaches about "fast software" and attention to craft.
- **Hundred Rabbits' site** (100r.co) is an argument about sustainable computing. Two people living on a sailboat, building software tools that run on minimal hardware.
- **Bret Victor's essays** (worrydream.com) are interactive demonstrations. "Magic Ink" and "Ladder of Abstraction" don't just describe ideas about information design – they show them.
- **Robin Sloan's "Fish"** is a tap essay. The medium (tapping to advance) is the form. You read it with your thumb.
- **The Massey Lectures** (Franklin, Beer) are talks first, books second. The voice of public address shapes the prose.
- **Gordon Brander's pattern library** is a wiki of design patterns, not an essay. The form (browse, link, connect) embodies the idea.

**Signal for models**: Lee values work where the form carries meaning. When building things for Lee (tools, pages, documents), the form should serve the content, not decorate it. Georgia font, no transitions, instant hover – the website itself follows this principle.

## 10. BIOGRAPHICAL – Who These People Are

The shelf authors share a biographical profile:

**Academics who write for everyone**: Mills, Flyvbjerg, Tsing, Ferguson, Sennett, Ingold. They hold university positions but write for general readers. Their books are published by trade presses or crossover academic presses.

**Practitioners who theorize**: Schwarz (furniture maker), Korn (furniture maker), Victor (interface designer), Brand (whole-earth publisher), Hendren (disability design). They make things AND think about making.

**Designers who critique design**: Norman, Alexander, Chimero, von Busch & Palmås. They work inside design but question its premises.

**Outsiders to their fields**: Berry (farmer writing about technology), Illich (priest writing about institutions), McLuhan (literature professor writing about media), Berger (novelist writing about art).

**What they share**: A refusal to stay inside disciplinary boundaries. A belief that making and thinking are not separate activities. A suspicion of professional expertise as gatekeeping.

**Signal for models**: Lee trusts people who have **both** hands and head. Pure theorists without practice, or pure practitioners without reflection, are less credible. Frame recommendations accordingly.

## Meta-Voice: The Shelf as a Whole

The shelf, taken as a single document, says:

1. **Tools shape their users** (Illich, Winner, Franklin, McLuhan, Norman)
2. **Maintenance matters more than innovation** (Russell/Vinsel, Mattern, Brand)
3. **Making is knowing** (Polanyi, Sennett, Ingold, Korn, Victor)
4. **Seeing is political** (Berger, Pinney, Mills, Flyvbjerg)
5. **Helping has costs** (Ferguson, Tsing, Fassin, Packard, Adams, Krause)
6. **Form carries meaning** (Victor, Sloan, McLuhan/Fiore, Mod)

These six propositions are Lee's intellectual operating system. Any voice calibration that doesn't respect all six will sound wrong.

The tagline "Tools, judgment, and use" compresses 1 + 3 + 5 into three words. "Tools" = the craft lane. "Judgment" = the perception lane. "Use" = what happens when tools meet the world (technology + intervention). The fourth concept (maintenance/care) runs underneath all three.
