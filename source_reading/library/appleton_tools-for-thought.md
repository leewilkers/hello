Appleton's essay argues that "tools for thought" has been narrowly interpreted as computational note-taking software, when historically the concept encompasses a much broader range of cultural practices -- from written language and Aboriginal songlines to the Socratic method. She traces how the term became computer-centric through a specific demographic of male, white, American engineers, and argues the current discourse is really about "computational mediums for white-collar knowledge work."
