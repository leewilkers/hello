## Green wood

Working wood that is alive — recently felled, or felled within the same season — is different work from working kiln-dried industrial stock. Green wood moves. It warps, it shrinks, it twists, and the way it does so is species-specific and time-dependent. A board worked on Tuesday will not be the same board on Friday. The joinery that holds it together has to accommodate that movement rather than try to eliminate it. The craft tradition surrounding green wood is full of moves — riving along the grain, steam-bending, coopering, wedged through-tenons — that exploit the natural direction of fiber rather than fight it.

Kiln-dried industrial stock is the opposite. It is dead. It has been dried to predictability, dimensionally standardized, flattened and normalized, optimized for interchangeability. Tolerances are enforced externally rather than read from the material. Knots — the places where branches once grew and the grain had to negotiate around them — are treated as defects. Material individuality is a hazard to be machined away.

Most global health systems are designed for kiln-dried stock. They assume standardized users, standardized spaces, standardized climates, standardized failure modes, standardized tools. They are not wrong to assume these things; large-scale interventions cannot work without standardization. But communities are not dimensionally stable. Institutions warp under political moisture. Incentives swell and shrink seasonally. Programs move after installation. A health system is not MDF.

Most people who run programs already know this. The vocabulary that names it is mostly missing.

## Knots, and what they remember

In traditional industrial woodworking, knots are evidence of weakness. They interrupt the grain, they create machining hazards, they ruin the visual uniformity of a clear board. Lumber is graded down for knots. The straight-grained, knot-free board is the aesthetic and moral ideal.

But a knot is not a defect of the wood. A knot is a record. It is where a branch grew under constraint, where the trunk had to incorporate adaptation, where stress was negotiated into structure. The knot is the tree's memory of having had to bend around something.

Institutions grow knots. The places in a program where adaptation became necessary, where local conditions forced negotiation, where the formal protocol had to incorporate what the field actually needed — those are knots. They are not failures of execution. They are the structural record of a program that has been alive long enough to have learned something. Reading them requires the same patience that reading the grain of a piece of figured maple requires: you have to follow what the wood did, not what the cut list said it should do.

The reaction-wood case sharpens this further. Trees that grow on a slope, or that are forced into a lean, produce different wood on the loaded side: compression wood on the lower side in conifers, tension wood on the upper side in hardwoods. Tension wood in hardwoods is, in most species, indistinguishable from ordinary wood to the naked eye. The grain looks right. The board looks straight. The difference does not show until the board is sawn, planed, or dried — at which point it warps three to five times more along its length than normal wood does, springs back when cut from internal stress release, and leaves a fuzzy or woolly surface when planed because the gelatinous fibers in its cell walls do not sever cleanly. *Institutions have this too. Hidden internal tensions that do not show on the org chart, that do not appear in the strategic plan, but that determine what bends and what splits once load is applied.* The reaction-wood pattern in an institution does not reveal itself in the discovery meeting. It reveals itself after the program is in motion, when the boards start moving.

## Reading the grain

Good woodworkers read grain before they cut. They anticipate tearout. They understand hidden tension. They work with fiber direction rather than against it. They know where force will travel through a joint and where stress will concentrate at a transition. The work of reading the grain is partly visual, partly tactile, partly tacit — a kind of knowledge that builds up only through repeated contact with material that misbehaves.

Good practitioners read institutions the same way. They notice where the load is actually being carried, which is not always where the org chart says it is being carried. They notice where stress is concentrating, which is not always at the formal pressure point. They notice which trust relationships are load-bearing and which are decorative, which routines accumulate residue, which decisions are reversible and which cannot be unmade. They read what the institution has done, in the order it did it, and treat the present state as the rational residue of a long sequence of reasonable choices made under constraints that may no longer be visible.

Reading the grain in either material is hard. It cannot be reduced to a checklist. The carpenter who can read a board cannot always say how. The researcher who can read a program cannot always say how either. What both share is the discipline of staying with the material long enough to let its actual structure become legible.

## Four cases

The history of global health is, among other things, a history of programs that learned to read the grain too late or just in time.

**Smallpox eradication** is the canonical case. The early strategy emphasized mass vaccination targets, blanket coverage, centralized planning. In many places this failed because populations moved, records were weak, conflict disrupted campaigns, and local political realities mattered more than formal plans. The pivot Foege and others made toward surveillance-containment was not just a tactical refinement. It was an epistemological shift: from imposing a universal geometry on a population to building local intelligence networks that could identify transmission patterns already present. Local health workers, teachers, market knowledge, informal social maps, and rumor-tracking became more important than the centralized planning grid. The campaign succeeded partly because it became less rigidly industrial over time.

**Oral rehydration therapy** circulates in policy mythology as a magic-bullet, perfectly-scalable intervention. The chemistry is standardized. The implementation is not. Programs succeed when they adapt to local feeding practices, caregiver beliefs, household measurement systems, existing caregiving networks, and local language metaphors for the illness being treated. Campaigns have failed because instructions assumed literacy that was not present, preparation methods mismatched the household tools that were available, or messaging conflicted with the local illness models that governed what mothers actually did. The intervention works. The joinery between intervention and household is what determines whether it gets used.

**The HIV/AIDS response** through the late 1980s and 1990s repeatedly demonstrated that the communities affected were not passive beneficiaries. ACT UP, the treatment action movements, and local HIV organizations forced the field to recognize structures that had been hidden in plain sight: stigma networks, trust dynamics, informal care systems, pharma access barriers, the identity and risk politics that determined who would even present for testing. The hidden grain became visible, and the field's institutional infrastructure for clinical trial design, community consultation, access pathways, and ethics frameworks all had to change to accommodate it.

**The 2014 Ebola response in West Africa** failed early because responders interpreted local burial practices as irrational obstacles to safe disposal rather than as the deeply structured social obligations they were. Burial rituals were kinship structures, moral systems, and social legitimacy mechanisms before they were epidemiological hazards. Top-down prohibition, externally designed protocols, and securitized containment ran into something they could not plane away. Programs improved when responders negotiated adaptations, co-designed safer burial practices, worked through trusted intermediaries, and recognized the social function of ritual. The grain in this case was structural, not decorative. It could not be machined out.

In each case the move that mattered was the same move: a shift from imposing a geometry on the field to reading the structure already present. None of these are exotic insights inside global health. They are canon. They are taught in every implementation science syllabus and rehearsed in every retrospective on the last forty years of the field. What may be missing is a vocabulary that lets the diagnostic move appear as a method, not a virtue.

## Two epistemologies

The field oscillates between two ways of knowing institutions. One is industrial. It emphasizes standardization, universal protocols, measurable outputs, interchangeable units, scalability, certainty, abstraction. The other is craft. It emphasizes situated adaptation, tacit knowledge, local negotiation, practical judgment, contextual fit, maintenance, and relationship-sensitive implementation. The field cannot fully abandon either. Pure craft does not scale enough. Pure industrial logic snaps under real-world variability.

This is not a critique of standardization. Vaccines scale because of standardization. Supply chains require abstraction. Surveillance requires comparability. Protocols save lives. IKEA is a triumph of industrial civilization for a reason, and the flat-pack innovation that lets a bookshelf travel through a parcel network and be assembled in a small apartment is genuinely worth celebrating. But scale requires abstraction, and abstraction loses grain. The actual tension in large systems is that both are necessary and the boundary between them is not stable. The question is not which epistemology to commit to. The question is when you are machining aluminum and when you are bending green ash.

Most institutional strategy assumes straight grain: stable incentives, standardized conditions, predictable load paths. Real systems do not behave that way. The buzzwords the field has accumulated — adaptive, resilient, systems-aware, community-centered, localization, participatory, context-sensitive — gesture at the craft epistemology while still operationalizing programs as though variability were incidental, institutions were interchangeable, metrics were reality, implementation were downstream of planning, and relationships were soft factors rather than load-bearing structure. The vocabulary advanced. The operating assumptions often did not. Programs continue to misread where the load is actually being carried.

Many of the implementation failures that get attributed to technical problems are something else. They are failures of fit, of maintenance, of trust, or of institutional geometry. They are reaction wood revealing itself under load.

## Joinery, and what it costs to repair

The diagnostic move in each of those cases is not the whole picture. What the field-level practitioners were doing was not only reading the structure already there. They were also designing — or redesigning — the intervention's joinery. ORT failed where literacy-dependent instructions met households without the assembly tools. Ebola response improved when responders co-designed methods that local people could carry, fix, and pass on. Foege's surveillance networks held because local health workers, teachers, and market traders were field-repairable infrastructure for transmission detection. The cases reveal not only what to read but what to build, and the difference between buildable and breakable is mostly a question of joinery.

There is a distinction in the woodworking tradition between joinery that can be repaired in the field and fastenings that require the factory. A wedged through-tenon, properly cut, will hold for a hundred years and can be re-pegged with hand tools by anyone who finds the chair in a barn. An IKEA cam-lock is designed to be assembled once with an Allen key, perform reliably under expected loads, and be replaced cheaply when it fails. In a supply chain that ships replacements overnight and a population trained to use Allen keys, the cam-lock is the right engineering choice. A vaccine cold chain is cam-lock construction at its best: standardized cassettes, interchangeable parts, factory-calibrated components that any technician anywhere can swap out without rebuilding the whole system. Through-tenon work would not deliver a vaccine. Both are legitimate engineering choices. They optimize for different things.

Most ambitious global health programs are cam-lock construction shipped to places without IKEA stores. The fasteners depend on infrastructure — training pipelines, replacement parts, software updates, donor cycles, the continued attention of the institution that designed the program — that is not reliably present in the field where the program runs. When the cam-lock fails, there is nothing to replace it with, and no one local who is trained to fix it. The whole assembly comes apart. The same intervention designed with field-repairable joinery — methods that can be carried by people who already live in the place, fixed by people who already work with the program, sustained by social structures already present — survives the discontinuities that cam-lock construction does not.

Naming this distinction is not the same as romanticizing pre-industrial joinery. Wedged through-tenons cannot scale a furniture industry. Hand tools cannot equip a factory floor. But for interventions that have to live in places where the supporting institutional infrastructure is thin, intermittent, or politically vulnerable, joinery is the better engineering choice and cam-locks are the worse one. The default of the field has often been the opposite.
