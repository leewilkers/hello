## The diagnostic that frames the whole syllabus

**You are trying to describe a retrospective process prospectively, and it keeps collapsing.**

Sensemaking practitioners are extremely good at the work in the room and extremely bad at writing prospectus copy about it — because the work is retrospective by nature. Every attempt to write *"what I do"* in advance produces something thin or slop-shaped. This isn't a writing problem; it's an epistemological one. The fix is to stop describing the process and describe **patterns of outcomes** — *the kinds of situations that resolve differently after you've been in the room.*

That's the read. The syllabus below explains why, in stages.

## The reading, in order

### 1. Donald Schön, *The Reflective Practitioner* (1983) — BOOK
**Read:** intro + ch. 1-2 only.
**Unblocks:** the gap between technical rationality (what professional schools teach, what consulting decks sell) and the *swampy lowland* where real problems live. Names "reflection-in-action" — the only honest account of what good qualitative researchers do. Stops you apologizing for not having a framework to sell.
**Cost:** academic prose, slow.

### 2. Rao, "A Tale of Two Schools" (Art of Gig: Foundations) — SHORT ESSAY ⚡ *tonight*
**Unblocks:** Permission. The dominant consulting genre is Positioning School; you're People School. Names the schism explicitly. The People School playbook ("take inventory of named players, make up a story using abductive reasoning, start nudging") IS what you do. You're already doing it. You just haven't named it as consulting.

### 3. Peter Block, *Flawless Consulting* (1981/2011) — BOOK (partial)
**Read:** ch. 2-4 only. Skip the rest.
**Unblocks:** Transaction structure. Distinguishes diagnostic capacity from answer-delivery. Block's load-bearing claim: *clients hire consultants partly to have someone to blame, and the consultant's job is to refuse that role.* You need to understand the transaction structure before you can design your offer.

### 4. Rao, "Reality Arbitrage" (Art of Gig: Superstructures) — SHORT ESSAY ⚡ *tonight*
**Unblocks:** Structural-position-as-value. *"Things being equal, those closer to a reality will think faster and better about it."* Your structural position — outside the org, qual-research-trained, tech-critic-edged — puts you closer to certain realities than your clients are. That's not a soft perspective claim; it's a structural feature that translates into concrete value. Internalize this and you stop needing proprietary frameworks to justify your fee. The indie consultant as *"transparent intermediary of larger environmental forces."*

### 5. Lucy Suchman, *Plans and Situated Actions* (1987) — BOOK (partial)
**Read:** intro + ch. 1-2 only.
**Unblocks:** Plans are *post-hoc accounts*, not action-guides. Practitioners improvise; the plan is the story they tell afterward. Lets you describe your practice truthfully — *"frameworks are retrospective artifacts"* — without apologizing.
**Cost:** dense HCI framing; don't let it pull you into human-computer-interaction territory.

### 6. Karl Weick, *The Social Psychology of Organizing* (1969/1979) — BOOK (partial)
**Read:** ch. 1 + ch. 6 only.
**Unblocks:** Organizations don't *process information* — they *enact environments*. The "organizing" not "organization" distinction is the conceptual key. **Equivocality reduction is what your clients are actually paying for** — and they need that phrase. The rigorous version of "organizations lie to themselves in patterned ways."
**Cost:** dated empirical examples; ignore them, use the ontology.

### 7. Edgar Schein, *Process Consultation* vol. 1 (1969) — BOOK (partial)
**Read:** ch. 1-3 only. NOT Schein's later popular books (*Humble Inquiry*, *Organizational Culture*).
**Unblocks:** The distinction between **doctor-patient model** and **process model** of consulting. You're a process consultant whether you know it or not. Reading this *before* you write the page prevents accidentally writing a doctor-patient pitch.
**Cost:** stay in ch. 1-3; don't drift into Schein's later OD orthodoxy.

### 8. Rao, "The Gervais Principle" parts I and III — SHORT ESSAYS ⚡ *tonight*
**Read for the *move pattern*, not the content.** Three steps: (1) take a phenomenon everybody observes, (2) name it precisely, (3) explain why the standard account is wrong, (4) collect the audience that recognizes itself.
**Unblocks:** Your *"organizations lie to themselves in patterned ways"* observation is a Gervais-Principle-scale insight waiting to be named. You need to see the genre, not just the concept.

### 9. Rao, "A Basic Literacy in Strategy" (Ribbonfarm, 2015) — SHORT ESSAY ⚡ *tonight*
**Unblocks:** **Doctrine → strategy → tactics layering.** You're trying to write tactics (page copy, service descriptions, positioning statements) without naming your doctrine. Tactics float because they have no doctrine to anchor them.

> **The doctrine statement IS the product description, once compressed.**
> **You cannot write "what I offer" until you've written "what I believe about how change works in organizations."**

This is the structural reason you're stuck.

### 10. Walter Kiechel, *Lords of Strategy* (2010) — BOOK
**Unblocks:** Genre translation. The history of the People-vs-Positioning schism in strategy consulting. What you lack is not credibility but **translation** — you need to say what you do in Positioning School terms without betraying your actual method. Kiechel hands you the map of the schism so you can navigate across it consciously.
**Note:** vgr called this out because People School types are explicitly McKinsey's *exploitable blind spot* — "throw obscure qualitative management ideas at them, and they hesitate." That's not accident. It's structural weakness. Know which school you belong to before you write a word of positioning copy.

### 11. Karl Weick, *Sensemaking in Organizations* (1995) — BOOK — **THE LOAD-BEARING ENTRY**
**The closest book in this syllabus to your actual blockage.**

> *"Practitioners who work this way are extremely good at sensemaking in rooms with clients, and extremely bad at describing what they do in advance. Because sensemaking is retrospective by definition, you can't write a prospectus for it. Every attempt to write 'what I do' in advance produces something that sounds thin, vague, or like a worse version of what a Positioning School consultant would say.*
>
> *You are probably experiencing this as a writing problem. It is not. **It is an epistemological problem: you are trying to describe a retrospective process prospectively, and it keeps collapsing.** The move is to stop trying to describe the process and describe the **pattern of outcomes** instead — the kinds of situations that resolve differently after you've been in the room."*

**Unblocks:** The reason every draft today read as slop — yours, Claude's, ChatGPT's. The fix isn't better drafting; it's writing about outcomes-as-recognized-after-the-fact, not process-as-claimed-in-advance.

## Why this answers your actual question

You asked: *"what i offer, what i should offer, what i am."*

The syllabus's answer, compressed:

- **What you offer** — equivocality reduction (Weick), reality-proximity (Rao), process consultation (Schein), reflection-in-action (Schön) — *all under the buyer-facing label of "management consultant" / "tough-nut cracker"* because the buyer-side fiction is load-bearing (per vgr's earlier diagnosis).
- **What you should offer** — depends on what doctrine you commit to. *"You cannot write 'what I offer' until you've written 'what I believe about how change works in organizations.'"* Doctrine first. Then strategy. Then tactics (the page).
- **What you are** — a People School practitioner who has been mistakenly trying to write Positioning School copy about a process that's structurally retrospective. Once you name that, the page writes itself differently — as descriptions of outcomes that resolve differently after you've been in the room.

The blockage isn't a lack of insight about your work. It's that you've been trying to write the wrong genre of self-description.
